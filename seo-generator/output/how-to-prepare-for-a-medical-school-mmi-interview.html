How to Prepare for a Medical School MMI Interview

Practical guidance from the Leading Tuition team

Book a Free Consultation

If you've received an MMI interview invitation from a UK medical school, you might be feeling a mixture of relief and anxiety in equal measure. The Multiple Mini Interview format is unlike anything most applicants have encountered before — there's no single panel of interviewers watching you for an hour, no chance to warm up gradually, and no opportunity to recover from a shaky start by impressing later in the same conversation. Each station is its own self-contained challenge, assessed by a different person, and the whole process moves quickly. That unfamiliarity is exactly what makes it feel so daunting. The good news is that the MMI is a learnable format, and with the right preparation, most candidates find it far less intimidating than they expected.

What Is the MMI Format?

The MMI was developed to assess medical applicants more consistently and fairly than traditional panel interviews. Rather than spending 20 to 30 minutes with one group of interviewers, you rotate through a circuit of individual stations — typically between 5 and 10 stations, each lasting 5 to 8 minutes. A bell or buzzer signals when to move on.

Crucially, there is a different assessor at each station. This means your performance at one station does not colour how the next assessor perceives you. A stumble at station three has no bearing on how you're scored at station five. This is actually one of the format's strengths — it reduces the risk of a single bad impression defining your entire interview.

Before entering each station, you're usually given a short prompt — often 1 to 2 minutes to read a scenario outside the door. You then enter and respond, either to a question, a task, or an actor playing a role. Medical schools using the MMI format in the UK include the University of Nottingham, King's College London, Queen Mary University of London, and many others. The exact number of stations and timing varies by institution, so always check the specific guidance from your chosen schools.

The Most Common MMI Station Types

Understanding what you might face is the first step to preparing effectively. While no two MMI circuits are identical, most draw from a recognisable set of station types.

  • Ethical scenarios: You're presented with a dilemma — a patient refusing treatment, a colleague behaving unprofessionally, or a resource allocation problem — and asked to reason through it.
  • Role play stations: An actor plays a patient, relative, or colleague. You might need to break bad news, handle a complaint, or support someone in distress.
  • Data interpretation: You're shown a graph, table, or short passage of health data and asked to explain or discuss it.
  • Written task: Some schools include a station where you write a short response to a prompt, testing clarity of thought under time pressure.
  • Empathy and communication stations: These assess how well you listen, respond to emotional cues, and demonstrate genuine care — without needing any clinical knowledge.
  • Motivation and personal insight: A more traditional question-and-answer format asking why medicine, what you've learned from work experience, or how you handle stress.

What Assessors Are Looking For

This is where many applicants go wrong. The MMI is not a test of clinical knowledge. Assessors are not expecting you to diagnose conditions, recite pharmacology, or demonstrate medical expertise. What they are evaluating is whether you have the personal qualities to become a good doctor.

Specifically, assessors are looking for communication skills — can you express yourself clearly, listen actively, and adapt your language to the situation? They're assessing ethical reasoning — not whether you arrive at the "right" answer, but whether you can identify the competing values at play, consider different perspectives, and reason through a dilemma in a structured and honest way.

They're also looking for self-awareness. Can you reflect on your own limitations? Do you understand why medicine is demanding? Have you thought seriously about what the role involves, beyond the appeal of helping people? Candidates who demonstrate genuine reflection — including acknowledging uncertainty — tend to score better than those who project false confidence.

Empathy is assessed not through grand statements but through behaviour. How do you respond when an actor appears upset? Do you acknowledge their feelings before launching into information? Small moments of human connection matter far more than polished speeches.

How to Prepare for MMI Stations

A preparation timeline of roughly 6 to 10 weeks is widely recommended for MMI practice. Starting earlier than that risks burning out; leaving it until the final fortnight rarely allows enough time to develop genuine fluency with the format.

Begin by familiarising yourself with the four pillars of medical ethics — autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice. You don't need to memorise definitions, but you should be able to apply these concepts naturally when discussing a scenario. Read the GMC's Good Medical Practice guidance, which outlines the professional standards expected of doctors in the UK. This is the framework assessors often use when evaluating ethical responses.

For role play stations, practise with a friend, family member, or tutor who can play the role of a distressed patient or frustrated relative. The goal is not to rehearse a script — it's to become comfortable with the discomfort of an emotionally charged conversation. Focus on active listening: make eye contact, reflect back what you've heard, and resist the urge to fill every silence with information.

For data interpretation stations, spend time reading NHS reports, public health statistics, and NICE guidance summaries. You don't need to memorise figures, but you should be able to look at a graph showing, for example, childhood obesity rates across different UK regions, and discuss what it might mean for healthcare provision.

Timed practice is essential. Set a timer for six minutes and answer a mock question out loud. This feels awkward at first, but it builds the mental discipline you'll need on the day. Leading Tuition works with many medical applicants on exactly this kind of structured, timed MMI preparation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

One of the most frequent errors is treating the MMI like a knowledge test and trying to demonstrate how much you know. Assessors are not impressed by candidates who recite facts — they want to see how you think.

Another common mistake is rushing. The stations are short, and the temptation is to fill every second with words. In reality, a brief pause to gather your thoughts reads as composure, not weakness. Saying "that's a genuinely difficult question — let me think through it" is far better than launching into an incoherent answer.

Candidates also sometimes treat role play stations as discussions rather than interactions. If an actor is playing a worried parent, your first job is to acknowledge their worry — not to explain the medical situation. Empathy comes before information.

Finally, avoid memorising model answers. Assessors can tell when a response is rehearsed, and scripted answers tend to sound hollow. Prepare your thinking, not your lines.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does an MMI interview typically last?

Most MMI circuits take between 45 minutes and two hours in total, depending on the number of stations and the transition time between them. Individual stations are usually 5 to 8 minutes each, with 1 to 2 minutes to read the prompt beforehand. Check the specific format for each medical school you've applied to, as this varies between institutions.

Will every station involve an ethical dilemma?

No. While ethical scenarios are common, a typical MMI circuit includes a range of station types — role play, data interpretation, personal motivation questions, and sometimes a written task. Some schools weight certain station types more heavily than others. Preparing broadly across all station types gives you the best chance of performing consistently throughout the circuit.

What should I do if I get completely stuck during a station?

Be honest and think aloud. Saying something like "I'm not sure of the best approach here, but I think the key tension is between X and Y" shows self-awareness and structured thinking — both of which assessors value. Silence for a few seconds while you gather your thoughts is fine. What doesn't help is pretending to have an answer you don't have, or giving a vague non-response to avoid committing to a position.

How can I practise effectively on my own?

Start by sourcing MMI question banks — many are available through medical school preparation resources and university websites. Practise answering questions out loud, ideally timed, and record yourself if possible. Practising with another person is more valuable still, particularly for role play stations. Working with a tutor who specialises in medical school preparation, such as those at Leading Tuition, can provide structured feedback that's difficult to replicate through self-study alone.

The MMI rewards candidates who are genuinely reflective, communicative, and prepared to engage honestly with difficult questions. It's a format that, with practice, begins to feel much more manageable — and for many applicants, it turns out to be a far more natural way to show who they really are than a traditional panel interview ever could be.
