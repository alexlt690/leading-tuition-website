University Personal Statement Help

Expert support from Leading Tuition

Book a Free Consultation

If your child is approaching their UCAS application and the personal statement feels like the most daunting part of the whole process, you are not alone. Many parents describe it as a strange, frustrating experience — their son or daughter has worked hard for years, achieved strong grades, and yet when it comes to writing about themselves, the page stays blank. The guidance schools provide is often vague, deadlines creep closer, and the stakes feel enormous. A personal statement is not just a formality. For competitive courses and universities, it can genuinely influence whether an offer is made. This page explains what universities are really looking for, what separates a strong statement from a weak one, and how specialist support can make a real difference.

What Universities Actually Want

It helps to understand what admissions tutors are actually doing when they read a personal statement. They are not looking for a list of achievements or a polished biography. They are asking a much more specific question: does this student genuinely understand what this subject involves at degree level, and are they ready to engage with it seriously?

UCAS recommends that roughly 75 to 80 per cent of the personal statement focuses on academic interest and subject-specific motivation, with the remaining space used to show relevant wider activities or skills. Admissions tutors at universities such as Bristol, Durham, and Exeter have publicly stated that they are far more interested in a student who can reflect critically on a book they have read or a concept they have explored than one who simply lists extracurricular activities.

For competitive courses — medicine, law, economics, and Oxbridge applications in particular — the bar is higher still. Selectors want evidence of independent thinking, genuine curiosity, and the ability to connect ideas. A student applying for History who can explain why a particular period challenged their assumptions will always stand out over one who simply says they enjoy the subject.

What Makes a Strong Personal Statement

The strongest personal statements share several qualities that are easier to describe than to achieve without guidance. First, they open with a specific idea, question, or moment rather than a broad generalisation. Admissions tutors read thousands of statements that begin with sweeping claims about passion for a subject. A statement that opens with a precise observation or intellectual puzzle immediately signals a more mature writer.

Second, strong statements develop an argument rather than simply listing experiences. Each paragraph should build on the last, showing how the student's thinking has evolved. A student who read a particular book, found it challenged something they believed, and then pursued that question further is demonstrating exactly the kind of intellectual engagement universities value.

Third, the writing itself must be clear, confident, and the student's own voice. Admissions tutors are experienced readers and can identify writing that has been heavily rewritten by a parent or copied from a template. The goal of good support is to help the student express their own ideas more effectively, not to replace those ideas.

Common Mistakes Students Make

Even motivated, capable students make predictable errors when writing their personal statement without proper guidance. Being aware of these can save a great deal of time and stress.

  • Starting with a quote: UCAS has explicitly flagged this as a red flag, and many universities filter these out immediately.
  • Listing rather than reflecting: Describing what a student did is far less valuable than explaining what they thought, questioned, or concluded.
  • Vague enthusiasm: Phrases like "I have always been passionate about" are meaningless without specific evidence to support them.
  • Ignoring the word limit strategically: The UCAS personal statement allows 4,000 characters or 47 lines, whichever comes first. Many students waste space on irrelevant detail early on and then rush the academic content.
  • Writing for one university: Because the same statement goes to all five choices, it must work across different institutions and sometimes slightly different course titles.

How Leading Tuition Supports Personal Statement Writing

Our tutors are experienced in university admissions and understand what different departments at different institutions are genuinely looking for. This is not generic writing coaching — it is subject-specific, targeted support.

When Students Should Start

This is one of the most common questions parents ask, and the honest answer is earlier than most students do. The UCAS deadline for most undergraduate applications is in January, but students applying to Oxford or Cambridge, or to medicine, dentistry, or veterinary science, face an earlier deadline of 15 October. That means those students need a finished, polished statement by mid-September at the latest.

For all other students, beginning serious work on the personal statement in June or July of Year 12 gives enough time to draft, reflect, revise, and seek feedback without the pressure of a looming deadline. A statement written in a rush during the first weeks of Year 13 is almost always weaker than one that has had time to develop.

Starting early also allows students to be more intentional about the experiences they pursue in the final months before submission — whether that is reading around their subject, attending a relevant lecture, or completing a summer programme. These experiences can then be woven naturally into the statement rather than added as an afterthought.

Frequently Asked Questions

My child's school says they will help with the personal statement. Do they still need a tutor?

School support is valuable, but it is often limited to one or two brief reviews from a form tutor or UCAS coordinator who is managing dozens of students at the same time. A specialist tutor works with your child individually, over multiple sessions, with subject-specific knowledge. The two types of support complement each other well, and many of our students use both.

Can a tutor write the personal statement for my child?

No, and any service that offers to do this is putting your child at serious risk. UCAS uses plagiarism detection software, and universities can withdraw offers if they believe a statement is not the student's own work. Our role is to guide, question, and help your child express their own ideas as clearly and compellingly as possible — the thinking and the voice must always be theirs.

What if my child does not have much to write about?

This is more common than parents realise, and it is rarely as true as it feels. Most students have read, discussed, or encountered ideas that genuinely interest them — they simply have not been asked the right questions to surface them. Our tutors are skilled at drawing out authentic material and helping students see that depth of reflection matters far more than an impressive list of activities.

How many sessions does personal statement support usually take?

Most students benefit from between three and five sessions, depending on where they are starting from and how competitive their target courses are. A student applying to Oxbridge or medicine will typically need more time than one applying to less selective courses. We are always happy to discuss your child's specific situation and recommend an appropriate level of support before you commit to anything.
