MMI Interview Coaching

If your child has just received their UCAT results and is now facing the prospect of a medical school interview, it is completely normal to feel overwhelmed. The MMI — or Multiple Mini Interview — is unlike any interview format most students have encountered before, and many parents find it difficult to know how to help. There is no single panel of professors firing questions across a table. Instead, your child will move through a circuit of short, timed stations, each assessed by a different person, each testing something different. Understanding what that actually looks like, and preparing for it properly, makes an enormous difference to how confidently your child walks in on the day.

What Is the MMI Format?

The MMI was developed to reduce interviewer bias and assess a broader range of skills than a traditional panel interview allows. Rather than one long conversation with two or three academics, candidates rotate through a series of 5 to 10 stations, spending 5 to 8 minutes at each one. A bell or buzzer signals when to move on. Each station is staffed by a different assessor, which means no single interviewer's impression of your child dominates the outcome. Scores from all stations are combined to produce an overall MMI result.

This is fundamentally different from a traditional panel interview, where the same two or three interviewers assess everything from clinical knowledge to communication skills in a single sitting. The MMI is deliberately designed to be harder to prepare for by rote — but that does not mean preparation does not matter. It means the right kind of preparation matters enormously.

What Medical Schools Look for at MMI

Medical schools are not simply looking for academically strong students. They are assessing whether a candidate has the personal qualities to become a safe, compassionate, and effective doctor. Across MMI stations, assessors are typically evaluating:

  • Ethical reasoning — the ability to think through competing values without jumping to a quick answer
  • Communication skills — clarity, listening, and the ability to adapt language to different audiences
  • Empathy and professionalism — genuine warmth and appropriate boundaries
  • Critical thinking — interpreting data, questioning assumptions, and reasoning under pressure
  • Self-awareness — understanding personal strengths, weaknesses, and motivations for medicine

It is worth noting that the General Medical Council's Good Medical Practice framework underpins much of what medical schools are looking for, even at interview stage. Candidates who understand the four domains — knowledge, skills and performance; safety and quality; communication, partnership and teamwork; and maintaining trust — tend to perform more confidently across ethical and role-play stations.

The Most Common MMI Station Types — with Example Prompts

Knowing what to expect at each station type removes a significant amount of anxiety. Here are the station types your child is most likely to encounter, each with a realistic example prompt:

  • Ethical scenario: "A patient refuses a blood transfusion on religious grounds, but without it they are likely to die. How do you approach this situation?"
  • Role play: "The actor in front of you is a friend who has just told you they have been skipping lectures because they are struggling with their mental health. Respond as you naturally would."
  • Data interpretation: "You are given a graph showing antibiotic resistance rates across five countries over ten years. Describe what you see and suggest what might explain the trend."
  • Written station: "Read the passage provided and write a short summary of the key argument, noting any weaknesses in the reasoning."
  • Presentation station: "You have two minutes to prepare a brief explanation of why hand hygiene is important in hospitals, to be delivered to a group of primary school children."
  • Empathy station: "The person in front of you has just been told their elderly parent has been diagnosed with dementia. They are distressed. Respond appropriately."

How Leading Tuition MMI Coaching Works

Our MMI coaching is built around one principle: your child should feel genuinely prepared, not just briefed. Every student we work with is different — some are naturally confident communicators who need help structuring their ethical reasoning; others have excellent analytical skills but freeze in role-play scenarios. We begin with a diagnostic mock MMI session to identify exactly where the gaps are.

From there, coaching sessions are tailored to the specific station types your child finds most challenging. We use real-style prompts drawn from reported MMI circuits at UK medical schools, and our tutors provide detailed, honest feedback after every practice station. We also help students understand the medical ethics frameworks — including autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice — that underpin so many ethical scenario stations.

Because the MMI comes after the UCAT and personal statement stages, timing is often tight. If your child is also still working on their UCAT preparation, we can coordinate support across both areas to avoid overlap and make the most of available time before interview invitations arrive.

Your 6-Week MMI Preparation Schedule

  • Week 1 — Foundations: Understand the MMI format in detail; complete a baseline diagnostic mock session; identify the two or three station types that need the most work; read an introduction to the four principles of medical ethics.
  • Week 2 — Ethical reasoning: Work through ten ethical scenario prompts using a structured framework; practise thinking aloud without rushing to conclusions; review NHS values and the GMC's Good Medical Practice guidance.
  • Week 3 — Communication and role play: Complete at least four role-play scenarios with a tutor acting as the other party; focus on active listening, appropriate body language, and pacing; debrief after each one.
  • Week 4 — Data and written stations: Practise interpreting graphs, statistics, and short passages under timed conditions; work on structuring written responses clearly within a short word limit; review basic health data literacy.
  • Week 5 — Full mock MMI circuit: Complete a full timed circuit of six to eight stations with different assessors where possible; receive written feedback on each station; identify remaining weak areas and address them with targeted practice.
  • Week 6 — Refinement and confidence: Revisit the stations that scored lowest in the mock circuit; practise transitions between stations to manage nerves; run a final light mock session two to three days before the interview to consolidate without over-rehearsing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which UK medical schools use the MMI format?

Many of the UK's leading medical schools now use the MMI, including Birmingham, Bristol, King's College London, Leicester, Brighton and Sussex, Hull York, Exeter, Nottingham, Barts, and St George's. The format is also used at Aberdeen and Cardiff, among others. Because admissions processes can change, always verify the current format on each school's admissions page before your child's interview.

How is the MMI different from a traditional medical school interview?

In a traditional panel interview, two or three interviewers assess your child across a single extended conversation. In an MMI, your child moves through a circuit of 5 to 10 short stations, each lasting 5 to 8 minutes, with a different assessor at every station. This means no single interviewer's bias or first impression determines the outcome, and a weaker performance at one station does not necessarily damage the overall score.

My child is a strong student but very shy — will the MMI suit them?

The MMI can actually work in favour of quieter students, because each station is a fresh start with a new assessor. There is no accumulated impression to overcome. With the right preparation — particularly on role-play and communication stations — shy students often perform better than they expect. Our coaching specifically addresses how to come across as warm and engaged without needing to be naturally extroverted.

How far in advance should we start MMI preparation?

Ideally, structured MMI preparation should begin as soon as your child receives an interview invitation, which typically arrives between October and January for standard UCAS applicants. Six weeks of focused preparation is enough time to make a significant difference, but even three to four weeks of intensive coaching is far better than no preparation at all. Starting earlier also reduces last-minute pressure considerably.
