Oxford and Cambridge Medicine Interviews

Most students preparing for Oxbridge Medicine interviews assume that performing well means knowing more — more anatomy, more biochemistry, more clinical facts. That assumption is wrong, and it is one of the main reasons capable candidates underperform. Oxford and Cambridge are not looking for a student who has memorised a textbook. They are looking for someone who can think like a scientist in real time: someone who can engage with an unfamiliar problem, reason carefully under pressure, and change their mind when the evidence demands it. The Medicine interview is not a knowledge test. It is a thinking test, and preparing for it requires a fundamentally different approach from anything you will have encountered at school or sixth form.

What to Expect in a Medicine Oxbridge Interview

Both Oxford and Cambridge conduct multiple interviews per candidate, typically with two or three tutors present. These are not conversational assessments of your enthusiasm for medicine. They are structured intellectual exchanges designed to push you beyond what you already know. Tutors will often introduce new information mid-question — a graph, a scenario, a piece of data — and ask you to reason through it on the spot. The discomfort you feel when you do not immediately know the answer is intentional. How you respond to that discomfort is precisely what is being assessed.

At Oxford, interviews are held at individual colleges and are typically conducted by two academics. The format tends to be rigorous and problem-focused, with questions that escalate in difficulty as the conversation progresses. At Cambridge, the structure is similar, though candidates often find that interviewers at Cambridge place particular emphasis on scientific reasoning and the ability to connect biological principles across different systems. In both cases, you should expect to be challenged on your personal statement, asked to interpret unfamiliar material, and invited to think through ethical or clinical scenarios.

The Admissions Tests: No Written Test (UCAT + Interview) at Oxford and No Written Test (UCAT + Interview) at Cambridge

Neither Oxford nor Cambridge currently requires a written subject-specific admissions test for Medicine. The UCAT — the University Clinical Aptitude Test — is the primary pre-interview filter used by both universities, alongside your academic record and personal statement. A strong UCAT score, particularly in the Verbal Reasoning and Decision Making sections, will help secure your interview invitation, but it does not replace interview preparation. The UCAT tests aptitude; the interview tests how you think about medicine as an intellectual discipline.

Because there is no written test to prepare for beyond the UCAT, all of your subject-specific preparation should be directed toward the interview itself. This means practising scientific reasoning, engaging with medical ethics, and developing the habit of thinking aloud — skills that no amount of UCAT practice will build on their own.

How to Prepare for Your Medicine Interview

The single most important skill to develop is thinking aloud. Tutors cannot assess reasoning they cannot hear. When you encounter a question you find difficult, your instinct may be to go quiet while you think. Resist that instinct. Narrate your reasoning, even when it is incomplete. Say what you notice, what you are uncertain about, and what you would need to know to go further. This is not a performance — it is exactly how good scientists and clinicians think.

Super-curricular preparation matters significantly for Medicine. Reading beyond the A-level syllabus — whether that is following developments in medical research, reading publications aimed at scientifically literate non-specialists, or reflecting carefully on clinical or ethical issues you have encountered in work experience — gives you the intellectual material to engage with interview questions at depth. Tutors can tell the difference between a candidate who has genuinely thought about medicine and one who has rehearsed a set of prepared answers.

Practical preparation should include:

  • Regular mock interviews with someone who will push back on your answers, not simply affirm them
  • Practising with unfamiliar scientific passages or data sets and reasoning through them verbally
  • Reviewing the ethical frameworks commonly applied in medical contexts, including autonomy, beneficence, and justice
  • Revisiting core A-level Biology and Chemistry concepts so you can apply them fluidly, not just recall them
  • Reflecting on your work experience in a way that goes beyond description — what did it make you think about medicine as a discipline?

Example Interview Questions for Medicine

The following questions are representative of the kind of material you may encounter. They are not designed to be answered from memory — they are designed to generate a conversation.

  • A patient with a genetic condition wants to have children but does not want to know whether they carry the gene themselves. How would you approach this situation as their doctor?
  • If I told you that a new drug reduced the risk of heart attack by 50%, what questions would you want to ask before deciding whether to prescribe it?
  • Why does the body maintain a constant blood pH, and what mechanisms does it use to do so? What might happen if those mechanisms failed?
  • Here is a graph showing the relationship between antibiotic use and resistance rates across several countries. What do you notice, and what might explain the pattern?
  • Should healthcare systems prioritise treatments that extend life, or treatments that improve quality of life? Is that even a meaningful distinction?
  • A new screening programme detects a disease earlier but does not change survival rates. Is it still worth implementing?

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most common mistake is treating the interview as an exam. Candidates who try to retrieve a correct answer rather than reason toward one tend to freeze when questions move into unfamiliar territory — which they always do. The interview is a dialogue, not a test of recall.

A second frequent error is abandoning a line of reasoning the moment it becomes difficult. Tutors often ask follow-up questions specifically to see whether you will hold your ground, refine your thinking, or collapse under mild pressure. Changing your mind in response to new evidence is a strength. Abandoning your reasoning because you feel uncomfortable is not.

Candidates also frequently underestimate the importance of their personal statement. Everything you have written is fair game, and tutors will probe it. If you mentioned a book, be ready to discuss its argument critically. If you described a clinical placement, be ready to reflect on what it raised for you intellectually, not just what you observed.

Finally, avoid the trap of over-rehearsing fixed answers to common questions. Interviewers are experienced at recognising scripted responses, and a rehearsed answer to an unexpected question often sounds worse than an honest, exploratory one.

Frequently Asked Questions about Medicine Oxbridge Interviews

How long do Medicine Oxbridge interviews typically last?
Most interviews last between 20 and 30 minutes, though this varies by college. Candidates usually have two or three separate interviews during their visit, which may be on the same day or across consecutive days. Each interview is typically conducted by two academics.

Will I be tested on specific medical knowledge I have not studied yet?
Not in the way you might expect. Tutors may introduce unfamiliar concepts or data, but they are not expecting you to have prior knowledge of them. What they are assessing is how you engage with new information — whether you can reason carefully, ask sensible questions, and build on what you do know. A-level Biology and Chemistry provide the foundation; the interview tests what you do with it.

What is the best way to practise for a Medicine Oxbridge interview?
The most effective preparation involves regular verbal practice with someone who will challenge your reasoning rather than simply listen. Mock interviews that replicate the pressure and format of the real thing are far more valuable than reading lists alone. Practising with unfamiliar scientific material — reasoning through it aloud in real time — is particularly useful for building the habits that Oxbridge interviewers are looking for.

What should I do if I genuinely do not know the answer to a question?
Say so — and then keep thinking. Telling an interviewer that you are not sure, but that you would approach the problem by considering X or Y, is a far stronger response than silence or guessing. Tutors are not expecting you to know everything. They are expecting you to engage honestly and thoughtfully with difficulty. Candidates who admit uncertainty and reason through it openly often perform better than those who attempt to bluff.
