Oxford and Cambridge Law Interviews

A Law interview at Oxford or Cambridge is unlike anything you will have encountered in school. It is not a test of how much you know, nor is it a chance to recite your personal statement back to a panel of academics. What tutors are looking for is something far more specific: your ability to think legally. That means engaging with an argument you have never seen before, identifying its weaknesses, following a line of reasoning under pressure, and being willing to change your mind when presented with a better point. Students who walk in expecting to impress with their A-level knowledge often find themselves wrong-footed. Those who succeed are the ones who treat the interview as a conversation — rigorous, intellectually honest, and genuinely curious.

What to Expect in a Law Oxbridge Interview

At both Oxford and Cambridge, Law interviews are conducted by one or more academic tutors, usually within the college you have applied to. You may have more than one interview, and at Oxford it is common to be interviewed at an additional college as part of the pooling process. Interviews typically last between twenty and thirty minutes, though this varies by college.

The format is almost always problem-based. You will be given a legal scenario, a short passage, or a philosophical proposition and asked to reason through it. Tutors are not expecting polished answers — they are watching how you think. They will push back on what you say, introduce complications, and ask you to defend or revise your position. This is not hostility; it is the tutorial method in action, and it is exactly what your undergraduate experience will look like if you are offered a place.

At Cambridge, interviews tend to place particular emphasis on logical reasoning and the ability to construct and dismantle arguments from first principles. Oxford interviews often involve close reading of short legal or philosophical texts provided on the day, with tutors probing how you interpret language and apply concepts. In both cases, intellectual flexibility matters more than certainty.

The Admissions Tests: LNAT (Oxford) and LNAT (Cambridge)

Both Oxford and Cambridge require the LNAT — the Law National Aptitude Test — as part of the admissions process. The test has two sections: a multiple-choice comprehension section based on argumentative passages, and a thirty-minute essay in which you choose one question from a short list and argue a position.

At Oxford, the LNAT is used to shortlist candidates for interview, which means a strong score significantly affects whether you are invited at all. At Cambridge, the LNAT is considered alongside your application but is weighted differently across colleges, so its role in shortlisting varies. In both cases, the essay section is particularly important — it gives tutors a direct window into how you construct an argument under time pressure.

Preparing for the LNAT is not separate from preparing for your interview. The skills are the same: reading carefully, identifying the structure of an argument, spotting assumptions, and writing or speaking with precision. Practising LNAT essays will sharpen the analytical habits that tutors reward in the interview room.

How to Prepare for Your Law Interview

The most effective preparation combines structured practice with genuine intellectual engagement. Start by reading beyond your A-level syllabus. Follow legal commentary in publications such as the London Review of Books, the Law Gazette, or the Supreme Court's own case summaries. Read introductory texts — The Concept of Law by H.L.A. Hart is a classic starting point — not to memorise arguments, but to develop the habit of engaging with ideas critically.

Practise thinking aloud. This is one of the most important skills you can develop before your interview. When you encounter a question you are unsure about, resist the urge to go silent. Tutors want to hear your reasoning process, including the false starts and corrections. Saying "I'm not sure, but my instinct is X because..." is far more valuable than silence followed by a polished answer.

Work through mock interviews with someone who will genuinely challenge you — not just nod along. At Leading Tuition, our Law tutors are Oxford and Cambridge graduates who know the tutorial style from the inside. They will present you with the kind of problems that appear in real interviews and push you to develop your reasoning in real time.

  • Read recent Supreme Court judgments and ask yourself whether you agree with the reasoning
  • Practise LNAT essays under timed conditions and review them critically
  • Discuss legal and ethical dilemmas with peers or tutors — out loud, not just in writing
  • Read introductory jurisprudence to build a framework for thinking about what law is and does
  • Keep a note of arguments that surprised or challenged you, and work out why

Example Interview Questions for Law

The following questions are representative of the kind of problems Oxford and Cambridge tutors use. They are designed to have no single correct answer — what matters is how you engage with them.

  • If a law is unjust, do citizens have an obligation to obey it?
  • A man pushes another off a lifeboat to save himself. Has he committed murder?
  • Should judges make law, or only interpret it? What is the difference?
  • If two people sign a contract under a shared misunderstanding, should the contract be enforceable?
  • Is there a meaningful legal distinction between doing harm and allowing harm to occur?
  • Can a corporation be held morally responsible for its actions in the same way a person can?

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most common mistake is treating the interview like an exam. Candidates who try to retrieve a memorised answer, or who freeze when pushed beyond familiar territory, signal to tutors that they are not yet thinking independently. The interview is designed to take you beyond what you know — that is the point.

A second mistake is abandoning a position the moment a tutor questions it. Intellectual confidence means being willing to defend your reasoning while remaining genuinely open to revision. If a tutor challenges you, do not immediately capitulate — engage with the challenge, test it, and only change your view if the argument genuinely warrants it.

Finally, many candidates underestimate the importance of precision in language. Law is a discipline built on the exact meaning of words. If you use a term loosely, a tutor will ask you to define it. Practise being specific: not "it seems unfair" but "it violates the principle that like cases should be treated alike."

Frequently Asked Questions about Law Oxbridge Interviews

How long do Law interviews at Oxford and Cambridge typically last?
Most Law interviews run between twenty and thirty minutes, though this varies by college. At Oxford, you are likely to have at least two interviews — one at your chosen college and potentially one at another college if you enter the pool. At Cambridge, the number of interviews also varies, but one or two per college is typical. Each interview is short but intense, so preparation for sustained focus matters.

Will I be tested on legal knowledge I have not studied yet?
No prior legal knowledge is assumed or required. Tutors are not testing whether you know contract law or criminal procedure — they are testing how you reason. You may be given a short passage or scenario on the day and asked to work through it from scratch. What matters is your analytical approach, not your existing knowledge base.

What is the best way to practise for a Law Oxbridge interview?
The most effective method is regular mock interviews with someone who will genuinely challenge your reasoning — not simply affirm it. Supplement this with timed LNAT essay practice, reading legal and philosophical texts critically, and discussing ethical and legal dilemmas aloud. The goal is to make rigorous verbal reasoning feel natural, not rehearsed.

What should I do if I genuinely do not know the answer to a question?
Say so — and then keep thinking. Tutors are not looking for omniscience. Saying "I'm not sure, but let me think through this" and then reasoning carefully is exactly the right response. What tutors want to see is intellectual honesty combined with the willingness to engage. Going silent or guessing without reasoning is far more damaging than admitting uncertainty and working through it openly.
