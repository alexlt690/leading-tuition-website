What to Expect in an Economics Oxbridge Interview

If you are expecting your Oxbridge Economics interview to feel like a particularly demanding A-level lesson, you will need to adjust your expectations significantly. Oxford and Cambridge tutors are not interested in whether you can reproduce textbook definitions or recite the conditions for perfect competition. What they want to see is how you think — in real time, under pressure, with a problem you have probably never encountered before. The interview is a simulation of the tutorial or supervision system that defines undergraduate life at both universities: a one-to-one intellectual exchange where you are expected to engage, reason, and revise your thinking as new information emerges. That is a fundamentally different skill from exam performance, and it is one that can be developed with the right preparation.

What to Expect in an Economics Oxbridge Interview

Most Economics interviews at Oxford and Cambridge last between 20 and 30 minutes, and you will typically have two separate interviews, often with different tutors. You may be given a short passage, a graph, or a data extract to consider before the interview begins — particularly at Oxford, where some colleges use stimulus material as a starting point for discussion.

The questions will often begin accessibly and then escalate in difficulty. Tutors are deliberately probing the edges of your understanding. They want to find out where your thinking breaks down — not to humiliate you, but to assess how you respond when you reach that boundary. Do you freeze? Do you bluff? Or do you slow down, acknowledge the difficulty, and reason your way forward? The last of these is what distinguishes a strong candidate.

At Oxford, interviews tend to be conducted by the tutors who would actually teach you, and there is often a stronger emphasis on mathematical reasoning and model-building. Cambridge interviews, particularly for the Economics course (which leads into the Economics Tripos), may place slightly greater emphasis on your ability to engage with economic arguments at a conceptual level, though mathematical fluency is equally valued. In both cases, intellectual curiosity and the willingness to be challenged are non-negotiable.

The Admissions Tests: TSA (Oxford) and TMUA (Cambridge)

Before you reach the interview stage, you will need to sit an admissions test. Oxford Economics applicants sit the Thinking Skills Assessment (TSA Oxford), which tests critical thinking and problem-solving rather than subject knowledge. It is sat before interview and is used to shortlist candidates, so strong performance matters. The TSA rewards careful, logical reasoning — skills that are directly transferable to the interview itself.

Cambridge Economics applicants sit the Test of Mathematics for University Admission (TMUA). This is a more mathematically demanding test, assessing your ability to apply mathematical reasoning to unfamiliar problems. A strong TMUA score can significantly strengthen your application, and the mathematical thinking it demands is precisely the kind of thinking Cambridge tutors will probe in interview.

Preparing for your admissions test and preparing for your interview are not separate tasks. The habits of mind each requires — working carefully through unfamiliar problems, checking your reasoning, and expressing your thinking clearly — are the same habits that will serve you in the interview room.

How to Prepare for Your Economics Interview

The most important thing you can do is practise thinking aloud. This feels unnatural at first, but it is essential. Tutors cannot assess reasoning they cannot hear. When you work through a problem in your head and then announce your conclusion, you give the interviewer nothing to engage with. When you narrate your thinking — including your false starts and corrections — you demonstrate exactly the intellectual process they are looking for.

Beyond that, your preparation should include:

  • Reading beyond the A-level syllabus — The Economist, core texts such as Varian's Intermediate Microeconomics, and accessible books like Tim Harford's The Undercover Economist will all sharpen your economic intuition
  • Practising with past TSA or TMUA papers under timed conditions
  • Working through unfamiliar economic scenarios and asking yourself what a model would predict — and where the model might fail
  • Discussing economic ideas with someone who will push back on your arguments, not just agree with them
  • Reviewing your personal statement carefully, since tutors may use it as a starting point

Super-curricular engagement matters here. If you have read a paper on behavioural economics, attended a lecture, or followed a particular policy debate in depth, be ready to discuss it critically — not just to summarise it, but to evaluate it. Tutors are impressed by candidates who have genuinely grappled with ideas, not those who have simply accumulated a reading list.

Example Interview Questions for Economics

The following questions are representative of the kind of problems you might encounter. They are designed to be intellectually demanding and to reward careful reasoning over instant answers.

  • If the government imposes a tax on sugar, who ultimately bears the cost — and what determines the answer?
  • Why might two countries both benefit from trade even if one of them can produce everything more efficiently than the other?
  • A firm is making a loss. Under what circumstances should it continue to operate in the short run?
  • How would you model the decision of a student choosing whether to go to university? What does your model assume, and are those assumptions reasonable?
  • Is GDP a good measure of a country's economic wellbeing? If not, what would you replace it with — and what problems does your alternative introduce?
  • Suppose the minimum wage is raised significantly. Walk me through the possible effects — and explain why economists disagree about what would actually happen.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most common mistake is treating the interview like an exam — trying to produce a polished, complete answer before speaking. This leads to long silences, visible anxiety, and a missed opportunity to demonstrate the reasoning process tutors actually want to see. Speak earlier, think aloud, and treat the interview as a conversation.

A second frequent error is abandoning an argument the moment a tutor challenges it. Pushback does not always mean you are wrong. Sometimes tutors challenge a correct answer deliberately, to see whether you have the intellectual confidence to defend it. Evaluate the challenge, acknowledge any merit in it, but do not capitulate simply because someone senior has raised an eyebrow.

Finally, many candidates underestimate the importance of precision. Saying that a tax "affects prices" is far weaker than explaining the incidence of the tax in terms of relative elasticities. The more precisely you can express an economic idea, the more convincingly you demonstrate that you understand it.

Frequently Asked Questions about Economics Oxbridge Interviews

How long do Economics Oxbridge interviews typically last?
Most interviews run for 20 to 30 minutes, and you will usually have two separate interviews at your college, sometimes with an additional interview at another college. The format varies slightly between Oxford and Cambridge, and between colleges, but you should plan for roughly an hour of interview time in total across your visit.

Will I be tested on specific economic knowledge I have already studied?
Not in the way you might expect. Tutors are not checking whether you have memorised A-level content. They are more interested in whether you can apply economic reasoning to new situations. That said, a solid grounding in core concepts — supply and demand, elasticity, market structures, basic game theory — gives you the tools to engage with unfamiliar problems, so your existing knowledge is a foundation, not the focus.

How should I practise for the interview?
The most effective preparation involves mock interviews with someone who will genuinely challenge you — not simply ask questions and nod. Working with an experienced tutor who knows the Oxbridge format allows you to practise thinking aloud, receive honest feedback on your reasoning, and build the confidence to engage rather than freeze when a question is difficult. Supplementing this with admissions test practice and wide reading will round out your preparation.

What should I do if I genuinely do not know the answer to a question?
Say so — clearly and without panic — and then reason from what you do know. Tutors are not expecting you to have encountered every problem before. What they want to see is intellectual honesty and the ability to construct an argument from first principles. A candidate who says "I'm not sure, but if I think about it in terms of incentives..." and then reasons carefully will always outperform one who bluffs or falls silent.
