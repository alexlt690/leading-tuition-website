Oxford and Cambridge English Interviews

An Oxbridge English interview is not a test of how much you have read, how confidently you can summarise a text, or how polished your essay technique is. It is something more demanding and more interesting than any of that. Tutors want to watch you think — in real time, under pressure, with material you may never have encountered before. The skills that earn you top marks in A-level English, careful planning, structured argument, textual recall, are not what will distinguish you here. What matters is intellectual curiosity, the ability to notice something unexpected in a passage, and the willingness to follow an idea wherever it leads, even if you are not sure where that is yet. If you are expecting an interview that rewards preparation through memorisation, you will need to adjust your expectations. If you are willing to engage genuinely and think on your feet, you are already closer to what Oxford and Cambridge are looking for than you might realise.

What to Expect in a English Oxbridge Interview

Both Oxford and Cambridge English interviews are conducted by academics — often the very tutors who would teach you if you were offered a place. At Oxford, you will typically have two interviews, sometimes at your first-choice college and sometimes at a second college as part of the pooling process. Cambridge interviews are usually held at your applied college, and you may have one or two, depending on the college's process.

In both cases, you are likely to be given an unseen passage — a poem, a prose extract, or occasionally a short dramatic text — and asked to respond to it. This is not a comprehension exercise. Tutors are not checking whether you understand the words. They want to see how you read: what you notice first, what questions the text raises for you, how you move between close observation and broader interpretation. The conversation will develop from your initial response, and tutors will push back, offer alternative readings, or introduce new angles. This is not hostility — it is the tutorial method in miniature.

At Cambridge, there is sometimes slightly more emphasis on your personal statement and the reading you have done independently. Tutors may ask you to discuss a text you have mentioned, or to compare two writers you have studied. Oxford interviews tend to be more consistently focused on unseen material, though your wider reading may still come up. In both cases, the quality of your thinking matters far more than the breadth of your reading list.

The Admissions Tests: ELAT (Oxford) and No written test (Cambridge)

Oxford requires applicants to sit the English Literature Admissions Test, known as the ELAT, before interview. The ELAT presents six short passages from different periods and genres, and asks you to write a comparative essay on two or more of them in ninety minutes. It is sat in November, before interviews take place, and your score will influence whether you are called for interview at all. Strong ELAT performance demonstrates the close reading and analytical writing skills that Oxford tutors value, so preparing for it is not separate from interview preparation — it is part of the same process. Practising with past ELAT papers will sharpen exactly the skills you need in the interview room.

Cambridge does not use a written admissions test for English. Selection is based on your UCAS application, your school's reference, any written work submitted, and the interview itself. This places even greater weight on how you perform in conversation with your interviewers, making thorough interview preparation particularly important for Cambridge applicants.

How to Prepare for Your English Interview

The most effective preparation is also the most enjoyable: read widely, read carefully, and practise talking about what you read. Pick up poems you have never studied and spend ten minutes writing down everything you notice — rhythm, imagery, syntax, tone, what the poem seems to want from its reader. Then say it aloud. The gap between thinking something and articulating it clearly is where most candidates lose marks, and the only way to close that gap is practice.

Thinking aloud is a skill in itself. When you encounter something in a passage that you do not immediately understand, do not go silent. Say what you notice, say what it reminds you of, say what question it raises. Tutors are not waiting for a correct answer — they are watching how you handle uncertainty. A candidate who says "I'm not sure, but the repetition here feels deliberate — it might be creating a kind of insistence, or perhaps exhaustion" is doing exactly what is required. A candidate who says nothing until they have a fully formed thought is not.

Super-curricular reading matters too. Go beyond your A-level syllabus. Read literary criticism — try something by Roland Barthes, Terry Eagleton, or Seamus Heaney's prose essays. Read poetry collections in full rather than individual poems. Explore writers from different centuries and traditions. You do not need to have read everything, but you should have read some things deeply and be able to talk about them with genuine enthusiasm.

  • Practise close reading unseen passages aloud, ideally with a teacher or tutor who can challenge your interpretations
  • Record yourself responding to a poem cold — play it back and notice where you hesitate or become vague
  • Read at least one work of literary criticism and be prepared to discuss its argument
  • Revisit texts from your personal statement and think about what you would say if asked to defend or complicate your views
  • Practise disagreeing respectfully — if a tutor offers a reading you find unconvincing, you are allowed to say so

Example Interview Questions for English

  • Here is a poem you have not seen before. Read it through, and then tell me what you make of it.
  • This passage uses the second person throughout. What effect does that have on you as a reader, and why might the writer have made that choice?
  • Is it possible to separate a writer's biography from the meaning of their work? Does it matter whether we try?
  • You mentioned in your personal statement that you admire the way this novelist uses silence. Can you say more about what you mean by silence in a written text?
  • Two critics have described this poem as optimistic and despairing respectively. Can both readings be right at the same time?
  • What does it mean to say that a metaphor fails? Can you think of an example from something you have read?

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most common mistake is treating the interview as a performance rather than a conversation. Candidates who have rehearsed polished answers often struggle when tutors take the discussion somewhere unexpected, because they are waiting to deliver what they prepared rather than responding to what is actually being asked. Preparation should build flexibility, not scripts.

A second mistake is retreating to plot summary or biographical context when faced with a difficult question. If you are asked what a metaphor is doing in a particular line, talking about the author's life or the historical period is usually a way of avoiding the question. Stay close to the text.

Finally, many candidates are too reluctant to take a position. Tutors want to know what you think, not what the consensus view is. If you find a celebrated poem unconvincing, say so — and say why. Intellectual honesty, backed by close reading, is far more impressive than safe agreement.

Frequently Asked Questions about English Oxbridge Interviews

How long do English Oxbridge interviews typically last?
Most interviews run between twenty and thirty minutes, though this varies by college. At Oxford, where you are likely to have two interviews, each will usually fall within this range. The time passes quickly, which is why it is important to engage immediately rather than spending the first few minutes settling in.

Will I be tested on specific texts or prior knowledge?
Not in the way you might expect. The focus is almost always on how you read and think, not on what you already know. Unseen passages are common precisely because tutors want to see your analytical process without the safety net of prior familiarity. That said, if you mention a text in your personal statement, you should be prepared to discuss it in some depth.

How should I practise for the interview?
The most effective method is regular close reading practice with someone who will ask you questions and challenge your responses. A subject-specialist tutor can replicate the tutorial dynamic more accurately than most school settings allow. You should also practise reading passages aloud and articulating your first impressions before you have had time to construct a polished argument — that is the condition the interview creates.

What should I do if I genuinely do not know how to answer a question?
Say so honestly, and then keep thinking aloud. Silence is far more damaging than uncertainty. Tutors understand that a question may be difficult or that a passage may be unfamiliar — what they are watching for is how you respond to that difficulty. Saying "I'm not sure, but here is what I notice" and working from there is a perfectly valid and often impressive response.
