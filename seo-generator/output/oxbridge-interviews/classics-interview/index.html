Oxford and Cambridge Classics Interviews

A Classics interview at Oxford or Cambridge is unlike anything you will have encountered in a school classroom or sixth-form seminar. Your teachers have spent years rewarding accurate recall, careful essay structure, and thorough preparation. Oxbridge tutors are looking for something quite different: they want to see how you think. They will push you beyond what you know, introduce ideas you have never considered, and watch how you respond when the ground shifts beneath you. The interview is not a test of your existing knowledge — it is a live demonstration of your intellectual curiosity, your willingness to reason under pressure, and your capacity to engage seriously with a subject you claim to love. Understanding this distinction is the single most important step in preparing well.

What to Expect in a Classics Oxbridge Interview

Most Classics candidates at both Oxford and Cambridge will have two or three interviews, typically lasting between twenty and thirty minutes each. You may be interviewed by two tutors simultaneously, or by different tutors in separate sessions. At Oxford, interviews often focus on specific areas of the course — you might have one interview weighted towards ancient languages or literature, and another towards ancient history or philosophy. At Cambridge, the interview tends to be slightly more holistic, with tutors exploring your general intellectual range across the classical world, though they will still probe deeply into areas you have flagged as interests.

Both universities are looking for candidates who can engage with a text, an argument, or a problem in real time. You may be shown a short unseen passage — in English, Latin, or Greek — and asked to respond to it. You might be asked to defend a position, challenge a received interpretation, or simply explain what you find interesting about a particular period or author. The conversation is designed to be genuinely collaborative: tutors are not trying to catch you out, but they will press you, and the pressure is intentional.

There is a meaningful difference in emphasis between the two universities. Oxford Classics interviews tend to be more structured around the specific course content — the combination of language, literature, history, and philosophy — and tutors may probe your linguistic ability quite directly if you are applying for the course with Latin or Greek. Cambridge interviews, particularly for Classics at King's or Trinity, often feel more like an open intellectual conversation, with tutors following threads of genuine curiosity wherever they lead.

The Admissions Tests: No Written Test (Oxford) and No Written Test (Cambridge)

Neither Oxford nor Cambridge currently requires a written admissions test for Classics. This means that the interview carries even greater weight in the selection process than it does for some other subjects. There is no pre-interview written hurdle to clear, and no separate test score to offset a weaker interview performance. Everything rests on how you present yourself in the room. This makes thorough, specific interview preparation not just useful but essential. The absence of a written test is not a reason to prepare less — it is a reason to prepare differently, with the interview itself as your primary focus.

How to Prepare for Your Classics Interview

The most effective preparation combines subject-specific reading with regular practice at thinking and speaking aloud. Begin by revisiting the texts and topics you know best, but push yourself to form genuine opinions about them. Why does Thucydides matter? What is actually interesting about Virgil's use of allusion? What does Roman law tell us about Roman values? Tutors can tell immediately when a candidate is reciting a rehearsed answer rather than thinking in the moment.

Super-curricular reading is genuinely valuable here. Reading beyond the A-level syllabus — whether that is Mary Beard on Roman culture, Simon Goldhill on Greek tragedy, or primary sources in translation you have not studied formally — gives you material to draw on and signals the kind of independent intellectual engagement that Oxbridge tutors are looking for. Keep a brief reading journal so you can articulate what you found interesting and why.

Practise thinking aloud. This is a skill, and it does not come naturally to most students. When you are uncertain, say so — and then reason through the uncertainty rather than falling silent. Tutors respond well to candidates who say, "I'm not sure, but if I think about it this way..." and then actually think about it. Silence, or a flat "I don't know," closes the conversation. Engaged uncertainty opens it.

  • Read widely beyond your A-level texts — primary sources in translation, accessible academic writing, and classical reception in modern culture
  • Practise discussing your reading aloud, ideally with a teacher, tutor, or informed peer who will challenge you
  • Form genuine views on contested questions in ancient history, literature, and philosophy
  • Work on your Latin or Greek if you are applying for the language route — tutors may ask you to translate or comment on a short passage
  • Review your personal statement carefully and be ready to discuss everything you have mentioned in depth

Example Interview Questions for Classics

The following questions are representative of the kind of intellectually demanding, open-ended questions Classics candidates encounter at Oxford and Cambridge interviews:

  • Is the Iliad a poem that glorifies war, or one that questions it? How do you know?
  • What does it mean to say that a Roman author is being ironic? Can you give an example and explain how we can be confident in that reading?
  • If you could only preserve one ancient text for future generations, which would you choose and why?
  • How much can we trust ancient historians? Does it matter if Thucydides invented speeches?
  • Is it possible to understand ancient religion without believing in the gods yourself?
  • What is the difference between a Greek tragedy and a Greek comedy — and is that distinction as clear as it seems?
  • Do you think Julius Caesar was a great man? What would it mean to answer that question?

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most common mistake Classics candidates make is treating the interview like an exam. They prepare set answers to anticipated questions and deliver them fluently — but without genuine engagement. Tutors find this easy to spot and difficult to reward. The second most common mistake is shutting down when challenged. If a tutor pushes back on something you have said, it does not mean you were wrong — it means the conversation is working. Engage with the challenge rather than retreating from your position or abandoning it immediately.

Candidates also frequently underestimate the importance of their personal statement. Everything you have written is fair game, and tutors will probe it. If you mentioned an interest in Stoic philosophy or Roman architecture, be ready to discuss it with real depth. Vague enthusiasm is not enough — specific, reasoned engagement is what distinguishes strong candidates.

Frequently Asked Questions about Classics Oxbridge Interviews

How long do Classics interviews at Oxford and Cambridge typically last?

Most interviews last between twenty and thirty minutes. Classics candidates usually have two or three interviews across their visit, which may be with different tutors or panels. The total interview time across all sessions is typically between forty minutes and an hour and a half, depending on the college and the specific course route you have applied for.

Will I be tested on specific knowledge I have already studied?

Not in the way a school exam tests knowledge. Tutors are far more interested in what you do with ideas than in whether you can recall facts. You may be shown an unseen passage or asked about a topic you have not formally studied, precisely to see how you reason without a safety net. Breadth of reading helps, but the ability to think carefully in the moment matters more.

How should I practise for the interview?

The most effective practice involves speaking aloud, not just reading or writing. Ask a teacher or tutor to conduct mock interviews using open-ended questions, and ask them to challenge your answers rather than accept them. Record yourself if no one is available — hearing yourself reason through a difficult question is genuinely instructive. Working with an experienced Oxbridge preparation tutor who knows the Classics interview format closely is particularly valuable.

What should I do if I genuinely do not know the answer to a question?

Say so honestly, and then reason through it. Tutors are not expecting you to know everything — they are watching how you handle not knowing. A response such as "I haven't come across that before, but thinking about what I do know about the period, I would expect..." demonstrates exactly the kind of intellectual confidence and curiosity that Oxbridge tutors are looking for. Silence or a flat refusal to engage is the one response that genuinely does not help you.
