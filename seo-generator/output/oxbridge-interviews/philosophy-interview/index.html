Oxford and Cambridge Philosophy Interviews

Philosophy interviews at Oxford and Cambridge are unlike anything you will have encountered in a classroom. Your teachers reward accurate recall and well-structured essays; your interviewers are looking for something entirely different. They want to watch you think. They will hand you a problem you have never seen before, or push back on something you say with apparent conviction, not because you are wrong, but because they want to see whether you can hold your ground, revise your position, or find a more precise way of saying what you mean. The ability to write a polished A-level essay on the mind-body problem is almost irrelevant here. What matters is whether you can reason carefully, out loud, under pressure, in real time.

What to Expect in a Philosophy Oxbridge Interview

Most Philosophy candidates at both Oxford and Cambridge will have two or three interviews, each lasting between twenty and thirty minutes. At Oxford, Philosophy is almost always combined with another subject — Politics, Psychology, Linguistics, or Theology — so you should expect interviews in both disciplines. At Cambridge, Philosophy sits within the Humanities faculty, and interviews are typically conducted by two academics who will take turns leading the conversation.

The format is deliberately conversational. You will not be asked to recite definitions or summarise a text. Instead, you might be given a short passage to read and respond to, or asked a deceptively simple question — Can you know anything for certain? or Is it ever wrong to tell the truth? — and then followed wherever your answer leads. Interviewers are trained to probe, challenge, and redirect. A question that seems to have an obvious answer almost certainly does not. The conversation is designed to be difficult, and that difficulty is the point.

Oxford tutors tend to be particularly interested in whether you can engage with analytic precision — distinguishing terms, identifying hidden assumptions, and recognising when an argument is valid but unsound. Cambridge interviewers share this interest but often place slightly more emphasis on breadth of philosophical curiosity and your ability to connect ideas across different areas of the subject.

The Admissions Tests: PHIL (Oxford) and No written test (Cambridge)

If you are applying to Oxford for a course that includes Philosophy, you will almost certainly sit the PHIL admissions test before your interview. The PHIL is a written paper sat in November, and it typically includes a passage-based comprehension and argument analysis section alongside a short essay. It is not a test of prior philosophical knowledge — you are not expected to have read Kant or Hume — but it does reward the same skills your interview will demand: careful reading, precise reasoning, and the ability to construct and evaluate arguments under time pressure.

Preparing for the PHIL is therefore not separate from preparing for your interview. Practising past papers will sharpen exactly the analytical habits that interviewers are looking for. Pay particular attention to how you identify the structure of an argument, not just its conclusion, and how you articulate objections clearly and fairly.

Cambridge does not use a written admissions test for Philosophy. Your interview performance carries more weight as a result, which makes thorough preparation even more important. Cambridge will have your personal statement, your predicted grades, and the conversation in the room — nothing else.

How to Prepare for Your Philosophy Interview

The single most important thing you can do is practise thinking aloud. This feels unnatural at first. Most students are used to thinking privately and then presenting a finished answer. In a Philosophy interview, the process is the answer. When you are uncertain, say so — and then reason through it. I am not sure, but I think the key distinction here might be between... is a far stronger response than silence followed by a guess.

Beyond mock interviews, your preparation should include:

  • Reading introductory philosophy texts that develop analytical habits — Thomas Nagel's What Does It All Mean? and Simon Blackburn's Think are excellent starting points
  • Engaging with podcasts and lecture series such as Philosophy Bites or Yale's open course on Death by Shelly Kagan, which model rigorous philosophical dialogue
  • Practising argument mapping — taking a claim and identifying its premises, assumptions, and potential counterexamples
  • Reading quality philosophy journalism in publications like Aeon or the Times Literary Supplement to develop breadth of reference
  • Discussing philosophical problems with peers or a tutor, not to reach conclusions, but to practise the discipline of sustained, structured disagreement

Super-curricular engagement matters because it signals genuine intellectual appetite. You do not need to have read primary texts in depth, but you should be able to speak with enthusiasm and some precision about ideas that have genuinely interested you outside the classroom.

Example Interview Questions for Philosophy

The following questions are representative of the kind of problems you might encounter. None of them has a single correct answer, and all of them are designed to open into deeper complexity the moment you begin to respond.

  • If a ship has every one of its planks replaced over time, is it still the same ship? What does your answer tell us about personal identity?
  • Is it possible to act purely selflessly, or is every action ultimately motivated by self-interest?
  • Could a being with no sensory experience have any concept of colour? What does this suggest about the relationship between language and experience?
  • If determinism is true — if every event is caused by prior events — can anyone ever be morally responsible for anything?
  • Is there a meaningful difference between allowing harm to happen and actively causing it? Does your answer change depending on the context?
  • Can something be true but unknowable? And if so, what does that mean for the relationship between truth and evidence?

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most frequent error candidates make is treating the interview like an exam — trying to produce a complete, polished answer before they have fully understood the question. Philosophy interviewers are not impressed by fluency alone. They are looking for intellectual honesty, and that means being willing to say when a question is harder than it first appears.

A second common mistake is abandoning a position too quickly when challenged. If an interviewer pushes back on something you have said, that does not necessarily mean you were wrong. It may mean they want to see whether you can defend your reasoning. Ask yourself whether their objection actually undermines your argument, or whether it addresses a different point. You are allowed to say: I think my original claim still holds, because...

Finally, many candidates under-prepare for the specific demands of thinking aloud. They practise what to think, but not how to narrate their thinking. In a Philosophy interview, the two are inseparable.

Frequently Asked Questions about Philosophy Oxbridge Interviews

How long do Philosophy Oxbridge interviews typically last?
Most interviews last between twenty and thirty minutes. At Oxford, where Philosophy is combined with another subject, you will usually have separate interviews for each discipline, so the total time in interview across your visit may be closer to an hour. At Cambridge, you will typically have two interviews, each of a similar length.

Will I be tested on specific philosophical knowledge or texts I have studied?
Not directly. Neither Oxford nor Cambridge expects you to have studied formal philosophy before arriving. You will not be asked to explain Descartes' ontological argument from memory. What you will be asked to do is reason carefully about problems, some of which may touch on ideas you have encountered, and some of which will be entirely new. Prior reading helps develop your analytical instincts, but it is not a substitute for them.

How should I practise for a Philosophy interview?
The most effective preparation combines mock interviews with a tutor who can challenge your reasoning in real time, alongside independent work on argument analysis and philosophical reading. Practising with a friend can help, but only if they are willing to push back seriously rather than simply agree. Recording yourself answering questions aloud and listening back is also a surprisingly useful exercise for identifying where your reasoning becomes vague or circular.

What should I do if I genuinely do not know the answer to a question?
Say so — and then think through it anyway. Interviewers are not expecting you to know everything. They are expecting you to engage honestly with difficulty. A response that begins I am not sure I have a clear answer to this, but let me try to work through it... and then does exactly that is far more impressive than a confident-sounding answer that does not withstand scrutiny. Intellectual honesty is not a weakness in a Philosophy interview; it is one of the qualities being assessed.
