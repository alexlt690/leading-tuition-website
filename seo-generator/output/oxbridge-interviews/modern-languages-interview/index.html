Oxford and Cambridge Modern Languages Interviews

Modern Languages interviews at Oxford and Cambridge are unlike anything you will have encountered in a classroom or sixth-form setting. Your teachers reward accurate knowledge, careful preparation, and polished answers. Oxbridge tutors are doing something quite different: they are watching how you think. In a Modern Languages interview, you may be handed an unseen poem in a language you have never studied, asked to speculate about the cultural politics of a novel you mentioned in passing, or invited to defend a literary interpretation you have never considered before. The interview is not a test of what you already know — it is a live demonstration of how you engage with language, literature, and ideas under pressure. Understanding this distinction is the single most important shift you can make in your preparation.

What to Expect in a Modern Languages Oxbridge Interview

Most candidates applying for Modern Languages at Oxford or Cambridge will have two or three interviews, typically lasting between twenty and thirty minutes each. At Oxford, interviews are usually conducted by two tutors and may be split between a language-focused session and a literature or culture session. At Cambridge, the format varies by college, but you can generally expect a similar combination of close reading, discussion of your personal statement, and unprepared intellectual challenge.

Both universities are looking for intellectual curiosity and the ability to develop an argument in real time. However, there is a meaningful distinction in emphasis. Oxford interviews tend to be more structured around close textual analysis — you may be given a short passage or poem and asked to respond to it analytically, sometimes in the target language. Cambridge interviews often place slightly more weight on the breadth of your cultural and literary engagement, and tutors may follow threads from your personal statement more extensively. In both cases, fluency in your target language will be assessed, but tutors understand that your spoken language will develop significantly at university — they are not expecting perfection.

The Admissions Tests: MLAT (Oxford) and No Written Test (Cambridge)

If you are applying to Oxford, you will sit the Modern Languages Admissions Test, known as the MLAT, before your interview. The MLAT typically includes a passage for translation or comprehension in your chosen language, as well as an essay in English on a literary or cultural topic. Your MLAT score informs which candidates are invited to interview, and tutors will have seen your paper before you arrive. This means your interview may directly engage with ideas or weaknesses visible in your written work — so it is worth reflecting carefully on what you wrote and being prepared to develop or defend your thinking.

Cambridge does not use a written admissions test for Modern Languages. Instead, the interview itself carries more of the selection weight. This makes thorough interview preparation especially important for Cambridge applicants, since there is no prior written evidence to contextualise your performance. In both cases, the intellectual habits you develop while preparing for interview — close reading, analytical argument, thinking on your feet — are exactly the habits that will serve you in the MLAT as well.

How to Prepare for Your Modern Languages Interview

The most effective preparation combines deep engagement with your chosen texts and languages with regular practice of thinking aloud. Read widely in your target language — not just the set texts from your A-level syllabus, but short stories, essays, journalism, and poetry that genuinely interest you. When you encounter something that surprises or unsettles you, sit with that reaction and try to articulate why. Tutors are drawn to candidates who have genuine intellectual responses to what they read, not those who have memorised critical opinions.

Super-curricular engagement matters here more than in many subjects. Watching foreign-language films, reading literary criticism in translation, listening to podcasts about European or world literature, and attending cultural events all build the kind of contextual awareness that makes your answers richer and more confident. You do not need to have read everything — but you do need to have thought seriously about what you have read.

Practise thinking aloud. This is not natural for most students, but it is essential. When you are uncertain, say so — and then reason through the uncertainty. Tutors are not looking for instant correct answers; they are looking for a mind that can work productively with difficulty. Practise with a teacher, a tutor, or even a trusted friend who will push back on your ideas rather than simply affirm them.

Example Interview Questions for Modern Languages

The following questions are representative of the kind of challenge you might face. They are designed to have no single correct answer — what matters is the quality of your engagement with them.

  • Here is a short poem in French you have not seen before. Read it aloud and tell me what you notice about the language and form.
  • Is it possible to translate a joke? What does your answer tell us about the relationship between language and culture?
  • You mention Camus in your personal statement — do you think L'Étranger is a political novel, or a philosophical one? Can it be both?
  • If a literary text loses something in translation, does that mean translation is always a form of failure?
  • How does the language a writer chooses shape what they are able to say? Can you think of an example from something you have read?
  • What does it mean for a novel to be described as belonging to a national literature? Is that a useful category?

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most common mistake candidates make is treating the interview like an exam they can pass by giving the right answer. When a tutor pushes back on something you have said, many students immediately abandon their position — not because they have been persuaded, but because they assume the tutor must be correct. This is a significant error. Tutors often challenge you precisely to see whether you can defend your thinking. Hold your ground if you believe in your argument, while remaining genuinely open to new evidence or reasoning.

A second common mistake is over-preparing specific answers to anticipated questions. If you have rehearsed a response to a question about Flaubert and the tutor asks something slightly different, a rehearsed answer will sound hollow and may prevent you from actually engaging with what is being asked. Prepare your thinking, not your scripts.

Finally, many candidates underestimate the language component. Even if your spoken French, Spanish, or German is not yet fluent, you should be able to discuss ideas in your target language with some confidence. Practise speaking about abstract topics — not just everyday conversation — in the weeks before your interview.

Frequently Asked Questions about Modern Languages Oxbridge Interviews

How long do Modern Languages Oxbridge interviews typically last?
Most interviews last between twenty and thirty minutes. You will usually have more than one interview — often two or three across your visit — which may cover different aspects of the subject, such as language, literature, and cultural or historical context.

Will I be tested on specific prior knowledge in the interview?
Not in the way you might expect. Tutors are less interested in what you have memorised than in how you think. You may be given unseen material to respond to on the spot. If your personal statement mentions specific texts or authors, be prepared to discuss them in depth — but the interview is not a knowledge quiz.

How should I practise for a Modern Languages interview?
The most effective practice involves reading widely in your target language, discussing texts and ideas aloud with someone who will challenge you, and working through unseen passages analytically. Mock interviews with an experienced tutor who knows the Oxbridge format are particularly valuable, as they replicate the pressure and unpredictability of the real thing.

What should I do if I do not know the answer to a question?
Say so honestly — and then think through the question anyway. Tutors are not expecting you to know everything; they are watching how you handle uncertainty. A candidate who says "I'm not sure, but if I think about it from this angle..." and then reasons carefully is far more impressive than one who bluffs or falls silent. Intellectual honesty combined with genuine effort is exactly what Oxbridge tutors want to see.
