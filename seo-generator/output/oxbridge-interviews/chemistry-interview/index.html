Oxford and Cambridge Chemistry Interviews

A Chemistry interview at Oxford or Cambridge is unlike anything you will have encountered in school. Your teachers assess whether you have learned the material; Oxbridge tutors assess whether you can think like a chemist. You will not be rewarded for reciting definitions or reproducing textbook mechanisms. Instead, you will be given problems you have never seen before — sometimes deliberately at the edge of your current knowledge — and the tutors will watch how you reason through them. The question is never really whether you get the right answer. It is whether you engage with the problem honestly, think out loud, and show the intellectual flexibility that makes someone worth teaching at degree level. If you are preparing for a Chemistry interview at Oxford or Cambridge, understanding this distinction is the single most important place to start.

What to Expect in a Chemistry Oxbridge Interview

Most Oxford Chemistry candidates have two or three interviews, typically with tutors from their chosen college and sometimes from another college as part of a pool process. Cambridge interviews are usually conducted at the college you applied to, though pooled candidates may be seen elsewhere. In both cases, you should expect to spend between 20 and 40 minutes per interview working through problems with one or two tutors.

The format is conversational but demanding. A tutor might hand you a graph, a molecular structure, or a short passage of data and ask you to interpret it. They may ask you to explain a concept and then immediately push you beyond it. At Oxford, interviews tend to be tightly focused on problem-solving within chemistry itself — organic mechanisms, physical chemistry calculations, and conceptual questions about atomic structure or bonding are all common territory. At Cambridge, there is sometimes a slightly broader scientific flavour, with questions that bridge chemistry and physics or biology, reflecting the Natural Sciences framework. In both cases, the tutors are not looking for a finished scientist; they are looking for someone who is genuinely curious and intellectually honest about what they do and do not yet understand.

The Admissions Tests: CAT (Chemistry Aptitude Test) and ESAT

Before you reach the interview stage, you will need to sit an admissions test. Oxford uses the Chemistry Aptitude Test (CAT), a two-hour paper sat in November that tests mathematical reasoning, physical chemistry, and your ability to apply chemical knowledge to unfamiliar contexts. It is not a memory test — it rewards candidates who can work methodically under pressure. Your CAT score directly influences whether you are called for interview, so strong preparation matters.

Cambridge applicants for Natural Sciences (Chemistry pathway) sit the Engineering and Science Admissions Test (ESAT), which includes a compulsory Mathematics section alongside subject-specific modules. The ESAT is designed to assess scientific reasoning and mathematical fluency rather than curriculum coverage alone.

Preparing for these tests and preparing for your interview are not separate tasks. The analytical habits you build working through CAT or ESAT papers — reading questions carefully, showing your reasoning, checking units and assumptions — are exactly the habits tutors want to see in the interview room. Past papers for both tests are available and should form a core part of your preparation from September onwards.

How to Prepare for Your Chemistry Interview

The most effective preparation combines deep subject work with deliberate practice at thinking aloud. Here is where to focus your energy:

  • Revisit the fundamentals with rigour. Bonding, periodicity, organic mechanisms, equilibrium, and thermodynamics are all fair game. Make sure you understand the why behind each concept, not just the procedure.
  • Work through unfamiliar problems. Use CAT and ESAT past papers, Olympiad papers, and university-level problem sets to practise reasoning beyond A-level.
  • Practise thinking aloud. This is a skill, and it feels unnatural at first. Narrate your reasoning with a friend, a tutor, or even into a recording. Tutors cannot follow your thinking if it stays inside your head.
  • Read beyond the syllabus. Chemistry World, university lecture notes, and books such as Why Chemical Reactions Happen by Keeler and Wothers give you the kind of conceptual depth that makes interview conversations richer.
  • Mock interviews are essential. Practising under realistic conditions — with someone who will push back on your answers — is the only reliable way to build confidence and identify gaps.

Super-curricular engagement matters too. If you have read around a particular area of chemistry — catalysis, spectroscopy, green chemistry, or anything else — be ready to discuss it with genuine enthusiasm. Tutors notice the difference between a candidate who has memorised a talking point and one who has actually followed their curiosity somewhere interesting.

Example Interview Questions for Chemistry

The following questions are representative of the kind of problems you might encounter. They are designed to be worked through, not answered instantly.

  • Here is a graph of reaction rate against temperature for two different reactions. What can you tell me about the activation energies involved, and how would you expect a catalyst to change this picture?
  • Why does carbon dioxide have no dipole moment, but water does? What does this tell you about the relationship between molecular geometry and polarity?
  • If I give you a molecule with a chiral centre, how would you determine whether it is the R or S enantiomer? Now — what happens to optical activity when you have two chiral centres?
  • Explain why the boiling point of hydrogen fluoride is anomalously high compared to the other hydrogen halides, and then tell me why water's boiling point is even higher than you might expect from the same argument.
  • I have a compound with the molecular formula C₄H₈O. Suggest as many structural isomers as you can, and for each one, tell me what you would expect to see in an infrared spectrum.
  • Why does the ionisation energy of oxygen dip below that of nitrogen, even though oxygen has a higher nuclear charge? What does this tell you about electron-electron repulsion?

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Many well-prepared candidates underperform in Chemistry interviews for reasons that have nothing to do with their subject knowledge. The most common mistake is going silent when uncertain. Tutors expect you to encounter things you cannot immediately solve — that is the point. Silence reads as disengagement. Instead, say what you do know, identify where your uncertainty lies, and reason forward from first principles. A candidate who says "I'm not sure, but if I think about the bonding here..." is far more impressive than one who freezes.

A second common error is rushing to an answer without checking it. In chemistry, units, signs, and orders of magnitude matter. Slow down, show your working, and verify your conclusions. Tutors will often let you talk yourself into a mistake just to see whether you catch it.

Finally, avoid treating the interview as a test to pass rather than a conversation to have. The tutors are, in part, imagining what it would be like to teach you for three or four years. Candidates who engage with genuine curiosity — who ask a clarifying question, or say "that's interesting, I hadn't thought about it that way" — tend to leave a stronger impression than those who perform confidence they do not feel.

Frequently Asked Questions about Chemistry Oxbridge Interviews

How long do Chemistry Oxbridge interviews typically last?
Most interviews run between 20 and 40 minutes. Oxford candidates usually have two or three interviews across one or two days; Cambridge candidates typically have two interviews at their college, though pooled candidates may have additional ones. Each interview is with one or two tutors and focuses almost entirely on subject-specific problem-solving.

Will I be tested on things I haven't studied yet?
Tutors may introduce concepts that go slightly beyond A-level, but they will guide you through unfamiliar territory rather than expecting you to already know it. What they are assessing is how you respond to new information — whether you can follow a line of reasoning, ask sensible questions, and apply what you do know to something you haven't seen before.

What is the best way to practise for a Chemistry interview?
The most effective preparation combines working through challenging problem sets (CAT and ESAT past papers, Chemistry Olympiad questions, and university-level resources) with regular practice at thinking aloud. Mock interviews with a knowledgeable tutor who can replicate the pressure and unpredictability of the real thing are particularly valuable — reading about interviews is not the same as doing them.

What should I do if I genuinely don't know the answer to a question?
Say so — clearly and without panic — and then keep going. Tell the tutor what you do know that might be relevant, identify what is confusing you, and try to reason from first principles. Tutors are not expecting you to know everything; they are watching how you handle uncertainty. A candidate who engages honestly with a difficult question will always outperform one who bluffs or falls silent.
