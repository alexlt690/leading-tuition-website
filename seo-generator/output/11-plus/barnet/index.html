Barnet sits in an unusual position among London boroughs: it is home to two of the most academically demanding state schools in the entire country. Queen Elizabeth's Boys' School in Barnet town centre and Henrietta Barnett School in East Finchley are not simply good grammar schools — they are institutions that attract applications from across North London and beyond, with competition levels that bear little resemblance to the 11+ process in most other parts of England. If you are a parent in Barnet, or in a neighbouring borough such as Enfield, Hertsmere, or Haringey, understanding exactly what these schools require — and how their entrance exams differ from one another — is the essential first step in any serious preparation plan.

Preparing for the 11+ in Barnet — Where to Start

The first thing to understand is that Barnet's 11+ landscape is not a single process. Queen Elizabeth's Boys' School runs its own bespoke entrance examination, entirely independent of any consortium. Henrietta Barnett School uses the GSHSA (Girls' Schools of Hertfordshire and Selective Academies) consortium test, which is shared with a group of selective schools across Hertfordshire. These are two different exams, testing overlapping but distinct skills, with different structures, timings, and marking approaches. A child preparing for one is not automatically prepared for the other, and families targeting both schools need a preparation strategy that addresses each exam on its own terms.

Starting early matters here more than in almost any other borough. The children who perform well at QE Boys and Henrietta Barnett are not simply bright — they are well-prepared, confident under timed conditions, and fluent in the specific question formats each exam uses. That fluency takes time to build.

Which Schools and Which Exams?

Queen Elizabeth's Boys' School is consistently ranked the number one grammar school in England by A-level results. It is fully selective and non-fee-paying, and the entrance exam is written and marked entirely in-house. The test assesses verbal reasoning, non-verbal reasoning, and mathematics, with a strong emphasis on speed and accuracy. The cohort of boys sitting this exam each year includes many who have been preparing for twelve months or more, and the score required to receive an offer is exceptionally high.

Henrietta Barnett School is widely described as the most selective state school for girls in the United Kingdom. Entry is through the GSHSA consortium test, which is sat in September of Year 6. The consortium exam covers English, mathematics, and verbal reasoning. Henrietta Barnett's cut-off scores are among the highest of any school using this test, meaning that performing well on the GSHSA is a necessary but not sufficient condition for an offer — a girl needs to perform exceptionally well.

Key facts to hold in mind when planning:

  • QE Boys uses its own exam, sat in the autumn term of Year 6 — check the school's website each year for the precise date
  • The GSHSA test for Henrietta Barnett is typically sat in early September, making summer preparation critical
  • Both schools are heavily oversubscribed, with hundreds of applicants competing for each available place
  • Neither school offers a catchment area advantage — selection is purely by exam score

What the Exams Test — and Where Children Come Unstuck

The QE Boys exam places particular demands on mathematical reasoning and verbal reasoning under time pressure. Many well-prepared children find that they can answer the questions correctly in practice but struggle to complete the paper within the allotted time in exam conditions. This is one of the most common failure points: accuracy without pace is not enough. Targeted timed practice — not just working through questions, but actively training a child to manage time across a full paper — is essential preparation for this exam specifically.

For the GSHSA test used by Henrietta Barnett, the English component deserves particular attention. The comprehension and writing tasks require children to respond with precision and maturity, and many girls who are strong mathematically underestimate how much work the English section demands. Vocabulary breadth, the ability to infer meaning from complex texts, and the skill of writing clearly under pressure are all tested directly. Practising with GSHSA-style papers, rather than generic 11+ materials, gives children a much more accurate picture of what to expect.

A concrete preparation tip for both exams: build a consistent habit of timed, full-paper practice from at least six months before the exam date, and review every error carefully rather than simply moving on to the next paper. Understanding why a wrong answer was wrong is more valuable than completing an additional paper without reflection.

A Realistic Preparation Timeline for Barnet Families

For most families targeting QE Boys or Henrietta Barnett, a preparation period of twelve to eighteen months is realistic and appropriate. This does not mean intensive drilling from the age of nine — it means building the right foundations early and increasing the pace and specificity of practice as the exam approaches.

In Year 5, the focus should be on consolidating core skills: arithmetic fluency, reading widely and analytically, and an introduction to verbal and non-verbal reasoning question types. In the first half of Year 6, practice should become more structured and exam-specific, with timed papers introduced progressively. In the final weeks before the exam, full mock papers under realistic conditions — timed, quiet, with no assistance — are the most valuable preparation a child can do.

Families targeting Henrietta Barnett face an additional constraint: the GSHSA test in early September means that the summer holiday before Year 6 is not a rest period but a critical preparation window. A child who arrives at the exam having done little work over the summer is at a significant disadvantage relative to those who have maintained consistent practice through July and August.

Because these are distinct exams with different formats and demands, our tutors work with each child individually — assessing where they are strong, identifying the specific areas that need development, and building a preparation plan that reflects the actual exam they will be sitting.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the QE Boys exam the same as the GSHSA test used by Henrietta Barnett?

No — they are entirely separate examinations. Queen Elizabeth's Boys' School writes and marks its own entrance exam, which is not shared with any other school. Henrietta Barnett uses the GSHSA consortium test, which is also used by a number of selective schools in Hertfordshire. A child applying to both schools will need to sit both exams and prepare for the specific demands of each.

When are the exams sat, and how much notice do families get?

The GSHSA test for Henrietta Barnett is typically held in early September of Year 6, meaning registration and preparation need to be well advanced before the summer holiday. The QE Boys exam is usually held in the autumn term of Year 6. Exact dates are published by each school, and it is worth checking their websites directly each year as dates can shift slightly.

My daughter is bright and reads a lot — does she still need structured preparation for Henrietta Barnett?

Being a strong reader is a genuine advantage for the GSHSA English paper, but it is not sufficient preparation on its own. The exam tests specific skills — timed comprehension, inference, and writing under pressure — in a format that most children will not have encountered in school. Familiarity with the question style, practice at managing time across the full paper, and targeted work on any weaker areas all make a material difference to performance, regardless of a child's natural ability.

How early should we start preparing for QE Boys or Henrietta Barnett?

For schools at this level of selectivity, beginning structured preparation in Year 5 is sensible for most families. This allows time to build skills properly rather than cramming, and means a child arrives at the exam having practised the right question types over a sustained period. Starting in Year 6 is not impossible, but it leaves less room to address gaps and build the timed fluency these exams require.
