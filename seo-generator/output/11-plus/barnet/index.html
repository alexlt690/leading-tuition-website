Barnet is one of the most competitive boroughs in England for selective school entry. It is home to five significant selective schools spanning both the state and independent sectors: Queen Elizabeth's Boys' School, Henrietta Barnett School, Haberdashers' Boys, Haberdashers' Girls, and North London Collegiate School. Between them, these schools attract applications from across North London, Hertfordshire, and beyond. Each uses a different entrance process, tests different combinations of skills, and has its own registration deadlines — understanding the differences is the essential starting point for any family beginning to plan. Our 11+ resources hub brings together practice papers, guides, and preparation advice across all of these routes.

Preparing for the 11+ in Barnet — Where to Start

Barnet's selective school landscape involves at least three distinct examination routes: the QE Boys bespoke exam, the GSHSA consortium test used by Henrietta Barnett, and the ISEB Common Pre-Test used by Haberdashers' Boys, Haberdashers' Girls, and North London Collegiate. A child applying to multiple schools may need to prepare for two or even three of these simultaneously, with different registration windows, different timings, and genuinely different exam formats to navigate. Families who start planning early — ideally in Year 5 — have a meaningful advantage over those who begin in Year 6 when several deadlines are already approaching.

The children who perform well at these schools are not simply bright — they are well-prepared, confident under timed conditions, and fluent in the specific question formats each exam uses. That fluency takes time to build. Visit our 11+ resources hub for free practice papers and detailed preparation guides tailored to each of these schools.

The State Selective Schools: QE Boys and Henrietta Barnett

Queen Elizabeth's Boys' School is consistently ranked the number one grammar school in England by A-level results. It is fully selective and non-fee-paying, and the entrance exam is written and marked entirely in-house. The test assesses verbal reasoning, non-verbal reasoning, and mathematics, with a strong emphasis on speed and accuracy. The cohort sitting this exam each year includes many boys who have been preparing for twelve months or more, and the score required to receive an offer is exceptionally high. Our 11+ tuition service includes specialist preparation for the QE Boys bespoke exam format.

Henrietta Barnett School is widely described as the most selective state school for girls in the United Kingdom. Entry is through the GSHSA (Girls' Schools of Hertfordshire and Selective Academies) consortium test, sat in September of Year 6. The consortium exam covers English, mathematics, and verbal reasoning. Henrietta Barnett's cut-off scores are among the highest of any school using this test, meaning a girl needs to perform exceptionally — not merely well.

Key facts for families targeting the state selective schools:

  • QE Boys uses its own exam, sat in the autumn term of Year 6 — check the school's website each year for the precise date
  • The GSHSA test for Henrietta Barnett is typically sat in early September, making summer preparation critical
  • Both schools are heavily oversubscribed, with hundreds of applicants competing for each available place
  • Neither school offers a catchment area advantage — selection is purely by exam score

The Independent Schools: Haberdashers' Boys, Haberdashers' Girls, and North London Collegiate

Alongside the state selective schools, Barnet and its immediate surroundings are home to three academically outstanding independent schools that select at 11+ using the ISEB Common Pre-Test: Haberdashers' Boys School (Borehamwood), Haberdashers' Girls' School (Elstree), and North London Collegiate School (Edgware). All three regularly feature among the top independent schools in the country by examination results, and competition for places is intense.

The ISEB (Independent Schools Examinations Board) Common Pre-Test is an online adaptive assessment used by many leading independent schools as a first-round screening tool. It is typically sat in October or November of Year 6 and covers four areas: verbal reasoning, non-verbal reasoning, mathematics, and English. Because the test adapts to each child's performance in real time — presenting harder questions to those who answer correctly — children are not all answering the same questions. A child who answers confidently and accurately on progressively harder questions will achieve a higher scaled score than one who rushes and makes errors. Our 11+ resources hub includes materials specifically relevant to ISEB Pre-Test preparation.

A strong ISEB result is typically the prerequisite to being invited to a school's own second-round assessments. Haberdashers' Boys and Haberdashers' Girls each hold their own interview and further assessment days for shortlisted candidates. North London Collegiate also invites shortlisted children for its own individual assessments before making offers. Preparation for the independent school route in Barnet therefore has two distinct phases: performing well enough on the ISEB Pre-Test to be shortlisted, and then being ready for the school's own assessment day.

Key facts for families targeting independent schools in Barnet:

  • Registration for the ISEB Pre-Test must be completed through the individual school — contact the admissions offices at Haberdashers' Boys, Haberdashers' Girls, or NLCS directly, as each has its own registration deadline
  • The ISEB test is sat online, typically in October or November of Year 6 — check with each school for their specific sitting arrangements
  • All three schools are fee-paying; means-tested bursaries are available at each, and academic scholarships are awarded to high-performing candidates
  • Children who perform well in the ISEB Pre-Test will typically have strong foundations in verbal and non-verbal reasoning as well as secure core maths and English skills

What the Exams Test — and Where Children Come Unstuck

The QE Boys exam places particular demands on mathematical reasoning and verbal reasoning under time pressure. Many well-prepared children find that they can answer questions correctly in practice but struggle to complete the paper within the allotted time. Accuracy without pace is not enough. Targeted timed practice — actively training a child to manage time across a full paper, not just completing questions — is essential preparation for this exam. Our 11+ preparation resources include timed practice papers designed for the QE Boys format.

For the GSHSA test used by Henrietta Barnett, the English component deserves particular attention. The comprehension and writing tasks require children to respond with precision and maturity, and many girls who are strong mathematically underestimate how much work the English section demands. Vocabulary breadth, the ability to infer meaning from complex texts, and writing clearly under pressure are all tested directly. Practising with GSHSA-style papers rather than generic 11+ materials gives a much more accurate picture of what to expect on the day.

For the ISEB Pre-Test used by Haberdashers' and NLCS, the adaptive nature of the test means that the goal is accuracy on progressively harder questions — not simply finishing. Non-verbal reasoning is the area where many children are least practised, since it receives relatively little attention in primary school. Building familiarity with the full range of NVR question types before October of Year 6 is one of the most impactful things a child can do. See our 11+ resources for targeted NVR and verbal reasoning practice materials.

A Realistic Preparation Timeline for Barnet Families

For most families targeting QE Boys or Henrietta Barnett, a preparation period of twelve to eighteen months is realistic and appropriate. This does not mean intensive drilling from the age of nine — it means building the right foundations early and increasing the pace and specificity of practice as the exam approaches. In Year 5, the focus should be on consolidating core skills: arithmetic fluency, reading widely and analytically, and an introduction to verbal and non-verbal reasoning question types. In the first half of Year 6, practice should become more structured and exam-specific, with timed papers introduced progressively.

For families also targeting the independent school route — Haberdashers' Boys, Haberdashers' Girls, or NLCS — the ISEB Pre-Test in October or November means preparation needs to be well advanced by the start of Year 6. This makes Year 5 a particularly important year for children applying to both state and independent schools simultaneously. Our 11+ resources hub includes a detailed timeline guide for families managing multiple school applications at once.

Families targeting Henrietta Barnett face an additional constraint: the GSHSA test in early September means the summer holiday before Year 6 is a critical preparation window, not a rest period. A child who arrives at the exam having done little work over the summer is at a significant disadvantage relative to those who have maintained consistent practice through July and August.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the QE Boys exam the same as the GSHSA test used by Henrietta Barnett?

No — they are entirely separate examinations. Queen Elizabeth's Boys' School writes and marks its own entrance exam, not shared with any other school. Henrietta Barnett uses the GSHSA consortium test, shared with selective schools across Hertfordshire. A child applying to both will need to sit both exams and prepare for the specific demands of each. See our 11+ resources hub for preparation materials tailored to both exams.

What is the ISEB Common Pre-Test, and which Barnet schools use it?

The ISEB Common Pre-Test is an online adaptive assessment used by many leading independent schools as a first-round screening tool. In and around Barnet, it is used by Haberdashers' Boys School, Haberdashers' Girls' School, and North London Collegiate School. Children typically sit it in October or November of Year 6. It covers verbal reasoning, non-verbal reasoning, mathematics, and English. A strong result is needed to progress to each school's own individual assessment round. Our 11+ resources hub includes practice materials relevant to the ISEB format.

When are the exams sat, and how much notice do families get?

The GSHSA test for Henrietta Barnett is typically held in early September of Year 6 — registration and preparation need to be well advanced before the summer holiday. The QE Boys exam is usually held in the autumn term of Year 6. The ISEB Pre-Test for the independent schools is typically sat in October or November of Year 6. Exact dates and registration deadlines are published by each school on their admissions pages.

My daughter is bright and reads a lot — does she still need structured preparation for Henrietta Barnett or NLCS?

Being a strong reader is a genuine advantage for both the GSHSA English paper and the ISEB English section, but it is not sufficient on its own. Both tests assess specific skills — timed comprehension, inference from complex texts, and writing under pressure — in formats most children will not have encountered in school. Targeted practice on the actual question style, and attention to any weaker areas, makes a material difference regardless of natural ability. Our 11+ English resources include practice materials for both the GSHSA and ISEB formats.

How early should we start preparing for QE Boys, Henrietta Barnett, or the independent schools?

For schools at this level of selectivity, beginning structured preparation in Year 5 is sensible for most families. This allows time to build skills properly rather than cramming, and means a child arrives at the exam having practised the right question types over a sustained period. For families applying to both state and independent schools, Year 5 preparation is especially important given that the ISEB Pre-Test falls in October of Year 6 — very early in the academic year.
