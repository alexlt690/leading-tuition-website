University College London Medicine Entry Requirements

Choosing where to apply for medicine is one of the most significant decisions you will make during your A-Level years, and University College London sits at the very top of most applicants' lists for good reason. UCL Medical School combines world-class research with genuinely integrated clinical teaching, all within one of the most stimulating cities on earth. The medical school is part of a university consistently ranked among the top ten in the world, and its location in central London gives students access to some of the busiest and most diverse NHS trusts in the country. If you are drawn to a research-rich environment, want early patient contact, and are serious about medicine as both a science and a vocation, UCL deserves careful consideration.

Why Choose University College London for Medicine?

UCL's six-year iBSc integrated programme is one of its most distinctive features. Unlike many medical schools where an intercalated degree is optional, UCL builds a Bachelor of Science degree into the standard MBBS programme, meaning every student graduates with both an MBBS and a BSc. This gives UCL graduates a genuine grounding in scientific enquiry and research methodology that sets them apart at the point of foundation training applications and beyond.

The clinical placements are another major draw. UCL is affiliated with some of the most respected NHS teaching hospitals in the country, including University College Hospital, the Royal Free, Great Ormond Street, the Whittington, and the Eastman Dental Hospital, among others. London's extraordinary demographic diversity means that as a clinical student you will encounter presentations and patient backgrounds that simply cannot be replicated elsewhere in the UK. This breadth of clinical exposure is genuinely formative.

Beyond the academic and clinical offer, London itself matters. The city provides unparalleled opportunities for extracurricular engagement, whether that is medical society events, volunteering, research projects, or simply the cultural richness that comes with studying in one of the world's great capitals. UCL's Bloomsbury campus places you at the heart of all of this.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

UCL's standard A-Level offer for medicine is A*AA, with Chemistry required as one of the three subjects. Biology is strongly recommended and is effectively expected by most successful applicants, though UCL does not always list it as a strict requirement. The third A-Level can be from a broad range of academic subjects, and UCL looks favourably on students who have pursued intellectually demanding combinations.

For students taking Scottish Highers or the International Baccalaureate, UCL publishes equivalent requirements on its admissions pages, and it is worth checking these directly as they are updated annually. UCL does not typically accept General Studies or Critical Thinking as one of the three counted A-Levels.

UCL admits approximately 330 students per year to its MBBS programme, making it one of the larger intakes among the elite London medical schools, though competition remains fierce given the volume and quality of applications received.

UCAT Requirements at University College London

UCL uses the UCAT as a core part of its admissions process. As one of the UK's top-ranked medical schools, UCL attracts a highly competitive applicant pool, and UCAT scores play a meaningful role in determining who is shortlisted for interview. UCL does not publish a fixed minimum threshold, but in practice, successful applicants tend to score well above the national average across the cognitive subtests. A total score in the region of 2700 or above is broadly associated with competitive applications, though this figure shifts year on year depending on the cohort.

UCL considers the Situational Judgement Test as part of its assessment, so it is important not to neglect this section during your preparation. Consistent performance across all five subtests is more reassuring to admissions tutors than a very high score in one area offset by a weak performance in another.

The Interview Process at University College London

UCL uses a Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) format. This means that rather than a single panel interview, you rotate through a series of short, structured stations, each lasting around five to eight minutes and assessed by a different interviewer. Stations typically cover areas such as ethical scenarios, communication tasks, motivation for medicine, reflection on work experience, and your understanding of the NHS and wider healthcare issues.

The MMI format is designed to give a more rounded picture of a candidate than a single interview allows. Each station is scored independently, which means a difficult moment at one station does not derail your entire performance. UCL's interviewers are looking for clear thinking, genuine empathy, the ability to reason through unfamiliar problems, and evidence that you understand what a career in medicine actually involves.

Preparation should focus on practising out loud, ideally with a partner or tutor who can give honest feedback. Familiarity with common ethical frameworks and current NHS challenges is important, but so is the ability to listen carefully and respond to what is actually being asked rather than delivering a rehearsed answer.

What Makes a Strong University College London Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, UCL is looking for applicants who can demonstrate genuine intellectual curiosity and a mature understanding of medicine as a discipline. Your personal statement should reflect real engagement with your work experience rather than simply listing placements. Admissions tutors want to read about what you observed, what surprised you, what questions it raised, and how it shaped your thinking.

  • Work experience: Both clinical and non-clinical experience is valued. Shadowing a GP or hospital doctor is important, but so is any sustained voluntary work that demonstrates care, responsibility, and communication skills.
  • Academic engagement: Evidence of reading beyond the A-Level syllabus, attending lectures or medical conferences, or completing an Extended Project Qualification can strengthen an application.
  • Research awareness: Given UCL's research culture, showing some awareness of or involvement in scientific enquiry — even at a modest level — is well received.
  • Resilience and self-awareness: UCL values applicants who can reflect honestly on challenges and demonstrate the personal qualities needed for a long and demanding career.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to University College London

What UCAT score do I need for UCL Medicine?

UCL does not publish a fixed cut-off, but the applicant pool is highly competitive. Aiming for a total cognitive score above 2700 is a sensible target, though the precise threshold varies each year. Strong performance across all subtests, including the Situational Judgement Test, is important.

Is work experience required to apply to UCL Medicine?

UCL does not specify a minimum number of hours, but meaningful work experience is effectively essential for a competitive application. Admissions tutors expect to see evidence that you have engaged with healthcare settings and reflected thoughtfully on what you observed. Quality and reflection matter more than volume.

How should I prepare for the UCL MMI?

Start by familiarising yourself with the MMI format and the types of stations commonly used. Practise answering questions out loud under timed conditions, work through medical ethics scenarios, and stay informed about current issues facing the NHS. Mock MMI sessions with structured feedback are particularly valuable in the weeks before your interview.

Does UCL Medicine accept international students?

Yes, UCL accepts a limited number of international students each year. International applicants are subject to the same academic and UCAT requirements, but should be aware that the number of places available to overseas students is smaller than for home applicants, making competition even more intense. Tuition fees for international students are significantly higher, and UCL's website provides up-to-date figures for each entry year.
