University of Liverpool Medicine Entry Requirements

Liverpool is not a city that does things by halves, and the same is true of its medical school. The University of Liverpool offers one of the most clinically immersive undergraduate medicine programmes in the UK, with students gaining hands-on NHS experience from the very first year. If you are drawn to a course that integrates patient contact early, takes place in a city with genuine health challenges and a strong sense of community, and leads to a degree with real professional weight, Liverpool deserves serious consideration. Around 270 students are admitted each year, and competition is strong — but for the right applicant, this school offers something distinctive that goes well beyond its league table position.

Why Choose University of Liverpool for Medicine?

The University of Liverpool's MBChB programme is fully integrated, meaning that clinical and scientific learning are woven together from the outset rather than separated into pre-clinical and clinical blocks. Students begin working with patients and healthcare professionals in NHS settings during Year 1, which gives the course a sense of purpose and real-world relevance that many students find motivating. The school has deep, longstanding ties with Merseyside NHS Trusts, including the Royal Liverpool University Hospital, Alder Hey Children's Hospital, and Liverpool Women's Hospital — all of which serve as teaching sites.

Liverpool itself is an important part of the experience. It is a city with significant health inequalities, a diverse population, and a strong tradition of community medicine. For students who want to understand medicine as a social as well as a scientific discipline, this context is genuinely formative. The city is also affordable, lively, and culturally rich, with a student population that tends to feel at home quickly. Liverpool Medical School has a strong reputation for pastoral support, and students consistently report a collegiate atmosphere within the school.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

The standard A-Level offer for medicine at the University of Liverpool is AAA, with Chemistry required as one of the three subjects. A second science — Biology, Physics, or Maths — is strongly preferred, and most successful applicants hold Chemistry and Biology alongside a third subject of their choice. Some applicants are made an offer of A*AA depending on their UCAT performance and interview score, so it is worth being aware that the final offer level can vary.

Liverpool does not accept General Studies or Critical Thinking as one of the three A-Level subjects. For students taking Scottish Highers, the typical requirement is AAAAB or AAAAA at Higher level, with Advanced Highers also considered. International Baccalaureate applicants typically need a total of 36 points, with 6,6,6 at Higher Level including Chemistry.

Re-sit applicants are considered, though the university looks carefully at the circumstances behind any re-sits. Graduate applicants are also welcome, and a strong undergraduate degree in a relevant science subject can support an application, though the standard entry requirements still apply.

UCAT Requirements at University of Liverpool

The University of Liverpool uses the UCAT as a significant part of its selection process. There is no fixed published cut-off score, but in practice, competitive applicants tend to score in the upper half of the national cohort — broadly speaking, a total score above 2600 across the four cognitive subtests is generally considered competitive, though this can shift year on year depending on the applicant pool.

Liverpool uses UCAT scores to help rank applicants for interview, so a strong score can make a meaningful difference to whether you are invited. The Situational Judgement Test (SJT) is also considered, and a Band 4 result may disadvantage your application. It is worth treating UCAT preparation seriously and starting early — ideally in the spring or early summer before you sit the test in Year 12 or 13.

The Interview Process at University of Liverpool

Liverpool uses a Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) format. This involves rotating through a series of short stations — typically around eight to ten — each lasting a few minutes, with a brief pause between them. Each station is assessed independently by a different interviewer, which means your overall score reflects a broad range of interactions rather than a single conversation.

The stations at Liverpool are designed to assess a range of competencies, including communication skills, ethical reasoning, empathy, teamwork, and your understanding of the NHS and the medical profession. You may be asked to respond to a scenario, discuss a healthcare topic, or reflect on an experience from your personal statement. There are no trick questions — the assessors are looking for thoughtful, honest responses and evidence that you can think clearly under mild pressure.

Interviews typically take place between January and March for standard UCAS cycle applicants. Preparation should include practising speaking aloud, working through common medical ethics scenarios, and being able to discuss your work experience in a reflective and specific way.

What Makes a Strong University of Liverpool Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, Liverpool is looking for applicants who can demonstrate genuine engagement with medicine as a vocation. The following qualities and experiences tend to strengthen an application:

  • Relevant work experience — clinical or care-based settings are preferred; shadowing a GP, volunteering in a hospital, or working in a care home all demonstrate commitment and provide material for your personal statement and interview
  • Reflective personal statement — Liverpool values applicants who can articulate what they have learned from their experiences, not just list them
  • Understanding of NHS values — given the school's strong NHS integration, showing awareness of how the health service works and the challenges it faces is genuinely relevant
  • Teamwork and communication — evidence from outside medicine (sport, music, volunteering, part-time work) that shows you can work with others and communicate effectively
  • Commitment to Liverpool specifically — while not essential, being able to articulate why Liverpool appeals to you — its integrated curriculum, clinical access, or city context — can come across well at interview

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to University of Liverpool

What UCAT score do I need for University of Liverpool?

Liverpool does not publish a fixed minimum UCAT score, but competitive applicants typically score above 2600 across the four cognitive subtests. Your SJT band also matters — aim for Band 1 or 2. Because UCAT is used to rank applicants for interview, a higher score gives you a better chance of being invited regardless of where your A-Level grades sit.

Is work experience required to apply to University of Liverpool?

Work experience is not formally required, but it is strongly expected and will be difficult to compensate for in your personal statement or at interview without it. Liverpool places significant emphasis on understanding what a career in medicine actually involves, and relevant experience — clinical, caring, or voluntary — is the most convincing way to demonstrate that understanding.

How should I prepare for the Liverpool MMI?

Start by familiarising yourself with the MMI format and practising with a timer so that you are comfortable giving structured responses in a short window. Work through common medical ethics topics — consent, confidentiality, resource allocation — and be ready to discuss your work experience in specific, reflective terms. Mock MMI practice with another person is particularly useful, as it replicates the pressure of being assessed while speaking.

Does University of Liverpool accept international students for medicine?

Yes, the University of Liverpool does accept international students onto its MBChB programme, though the number of places available to international applicants is limited. International students are subject to the same academic and UCAT requirements, and the application process follows the same UCAS timeline. Tuition fees for international students are significantly higher than for home students, so it is worth reviewing the university's current fee schedule before applying.
