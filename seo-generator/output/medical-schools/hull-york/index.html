Why Choose Hull York Medical School for Medicine?

Hull York Medical School (HYMS) occupies a genuinely unusual position in UK medical education. It is a joint school, founded by the Universities of Hull and York, which means students benefit from two distinct campus environments across one of England's most historically rich regions. York itself is a compact, walkable city with a strong student community, while Hull offers a very different urban experience with a proud identity of its own. If you are drawn to a school that feels genuinely community-focused — where you will know your peers and tutors rather than disappearing into a large cohort — HYMS is worth serious consideration.

What makes HYMS particularly distinctive is its problem-based learning (PBL) curriculum. Rather than sitting through traditional lectures and absorbing information passively, students work through clinical cases in small groups from the very beginning of the course. This approach demands intellectual curiosity, the ability to collaborate, and a willingness to take ownership of your own learning. Students who thrive here tend to be those who enjoy discussion, can tolerate uncertainty, and are motivated by real patient stories rather than abstract theory. If that sounds like you, HYMS deserves a close look.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

The standard A-Level offer at Hull York Medical School is AAA, with Chemistry required as one of your three subjects. A second science — Biology, Physics, or Maths — is strongly preferred, though HYMS does consider applicants with other subject combinations in some circumstances. It is worth checking the most current entry requirements directly with the school, as these can be updated between admissions cycles.

HYMS does not typically make offers at A*AA, which makes it slightly more accessible on paper than some of the more competitive London schools. However, this does not mean the application process is straightforward — the UCAT threshold and interview stage are both competitive, and the school receives far more applications than it has places to offer. There are approximately 140 places available each year across the five-year programme, which is a relatively small cohort by UK medical school standards.

Scottish Highers and the International Baccalaureate are also accepted. For the IB, a total of around 36 points is typically expected, with strong scores in Higher Level Chemistry. If you are taking Scottish Highers, HYMS will consider AAAAB or equivalent, again with Chemistry included. Welsh Baccalaureate and EPQ qualifications are recognised but do not replace the core subject requirements.

UCAT Requirements at Hull York Medical School

HYMS uses the UCAT as a significant part of its initial shortlisting process. The school does not publish a fixed minimum score, but in practice, competitive applicants tend to score in the upper two deciles — broadly, a total score above 2700 across the four cognitive subtests is a reasonable benchmark, though this varies year on year depending on the applicant pool.

The Situational Judgement Test (SJT) component is also considered. HYMS typically expects a Band 1 or Band 2 result; a Band 4 is likely to disadvantage your application significantly. If your SJT score is lower than you hoped, it is worth reflecting carefully on whether to include HYMS on your UCAS form that cycle.

Because HYMS uses a PBL model, the school is particularly interested in applicants who demonstrate strong reasoning and communication skills — qualities that the Verbal Reasoning and Decision Making subtests are designed to assess. Investing time in structured UCAT preparation, ideally starting in the spring of Year 12, gives you the best chance of a score that keeps your application competitive.

The Interview Process at Hull York Medical School

HYMS uses a Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) format. This involves rotating through a series of short stations — typically around eight — each lasting a few minutes, with a brief pause between them. Each station is assessed independently by a different interviewer, which means a difficult station does not derail your entire performance.

The stations at HYMS are designed to reflect the values of the school and the demands of PBL-based study. You can expect scenarios that test your ethical reasoning, your ability to communicate clearly under pressure, your understanding of the NHS and current healthcare issues, and your capacity for self-reflection. Some stations may involve role-play — for example, a simulated conversation with a patient or colleague — while others present a written prompt to respond to verbally.

HYMS is not looking for polished, rehearsed answers. The interviewers want to see how you think, how you handle ambiguity, and whether you can engage honestly with difficult questions. Candidates who do well tend to be those who listen carefully, structure their responses clearly, and show genuine self-awareness rather than reciting pre-prepared scripts.

What Makes a Strong Hull York Medical School Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, HYMS is looking for evidence that you understand what studying medicine in a PBL environment actually involves. Your personal statement should reflect genuine engagement with healthcare — not just a list of work experience placements, but what you observed, what questions it raised for you, and how it shaped your thinking.

  • Work experience: HYMS expects meaningful clinical or care-related experience. This does not need to be hospital-based — care homes, hospices, GP surgeries, and voluntary roles in health settings all count. What matters is that you can reflect on what you saw and learned.
  • Teamwork and communication: Evidence of working collaboratively — whether through sport, music, volunteering, or leadership roles — supports your suitability for PBL-based learning.
  • Academic curiosity: Reading beyond the A-Level syllabus, engaging with medical news, or pursuing an EPQ on a health-related topic all demonstrate the kind of independent learning HYMS values.
  • Self-awareness: Applicants who can articulate their own strengths and limitations honestly tend to perform better at interview and in PBL tutorials alike.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to Hull York Medical School

What UCAT score do I need for Hull York Medical School?
HYMS does not publish a fixed cut-off, but competitive applicants typically score above 2700 across the four cognitive subtests, with a Band 1 or Band 2 in the Situational Judgement Test. Scores below this are not automatically disqualifying, but the higher your score, the stronger your position at the shortlisting stage.

Is work experience required to apply to Hull York Medical School?
HYMS strongly expects applicants to have some form of healthcare or caring experience before they apply. There is no minimum number of hours specified, but you should be able to discuss your experiences meaningfully in your personal statement and at interview. Quality of reflection matters more than quantity of hours.

How should I prepare for the HYMS MMI?
The most effective preparation combines practice with genuine reflection. Work through common ethical scenarios — consent, confidentiality, resource allocation — and practise articulating your reasoning out loud. Mock MMI sessions with a tutor or mentor who can give honest feedback are particularly valuable. Familiarise yourself with current NHS issues and read the GMC's Good Medical Practice to understand the professional values HYMS expects you to demonstrate.

Does Hull York Medical School accept international students?
HYMS does accept a small number of international (non-UK) applicants each year, though the majority of places are reserved for home students. International applicants must meet the same academic and UCAT requirements, and English language proficiency requirements also apply. It is advisable to contact the admissions team directly for the most current guidance on international applications, as policies can change between cycles.
