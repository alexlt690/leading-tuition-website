Why Choose University of Glasgow for Medicine?

Glasgow is one of those medical schools that tends to get under your skin. It is the largest medical school in Scotland, training around 270 students per year, and it sits within a city that genuinely supports medical education in a way that few others can match. The NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde health board is one of the biggest in the UK, meaning clinical exposure during your training is substantial, varied, and rooted in a population with genuinely complex health needs. If you want to understand medicine in the round — including the social determinants of health, health inequalities, and the realities of working in a large urban NHS system — Glasgow offers that from early in the course. Add to that a university with a research pedigree stretching back to the 15th century, a medical school that has produced figures including Joseph Lister, and a student city with a reputation for being welcoming, affordable, and culturally rich, and the case for Glasgow becomes clear without needing to oversell it.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

The standard A-Level offer for Medicine at the University of Glasgow is AAA, with Chemistry required as one of your three subjects. A second science is strongly preferred — Biology is the most common choice, though Physics and Mathematics are also accepted. Glasgow does not typically make A*AA offers in the way some English medical schools do, which makes it a realistic target for high-achieving students who may not be banking on A* grades across the board, though you should still be performing consistently at the top of your year.

Scottish students applying through the Highers system are typically expected to achieve AAAAB at Higher level, with Advanced Highers in Chemistry and at least one other science subject. If you are studying Scottish qualifications, it is worth checking the current UCAS entry requirements directly with the university, as these are updated annually.

Glasgow also requires GCSE performance to be strong, particularly in the sciences and Mathematics, though there is no published minimum grade threshold in the way some schools operate. The overall academic profile matters — a consistent record of high achievement across your subjects will support your application more than a single outstanding grade alongside weaker ones.

Graduate applicants are considered and should hold a 2:1 or above in a relevant degree, alongside the standard science A-Level requirements. A separate graduate entry route is not currently offered; graduates apply through the standard five-year programme.

UCAT Requirements at University of Glasgow

The University of Glasgow uses the UCAT as a core part of its selection process. As the largest medical school in Scotland, it receives a high volume of competitive applications, and the UCAT score plays a significant role in determining who is invited to interview. Glasgow does not publish a fixed minimum UCAT score, but in practice, competitive applicants tend to score in the upper deciles — a total score in the region of 2700 or above is generally considered competitive, though this varies year on year depending on the applicant pool.

Glasgow uses the UCAT to rank applicants alongside academic performance. The Situational Judgement Test component is also considered, so you should not neglect it in your preparation. If your UCAT score is lower than you hoped, it is worth being realistic about how this affects your overall application, given the volume of strong candidates Glasgow attracts each year.

The Interview Process at University of Glasgow

Glasgow uses a panel interview format. This typically involves a panel of two or three interviewers, which may include a clinician, an academic, and sometimes a medical student or lay representative. The interview is conversational in style but structured, and you should expect to be asked about your motivations for medicine, your understanding of the NHS and healthcare in the UK, your work experience, and your ability to reflect on ethical or clinical scenarios.

Unlike MMI formats, the panel interview means you are assessed holistically over a single extended conversation rather than across a series of short stations. This rewards candidates who can sustain a thoughtful, coherent discussion rather than those who have simply rehearsed scripted answers. Interviewers at Glasgow tend to probe your reasoning — they want to understand how you think, not just what you know. Preparation should focus on genuine reflection on your experiences, current NHS issues, and medical ethics, rather than memorising model answers.

What Makes a Strong University of Glasgow Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, the strongest Glasgow applicants tend to share a few characteristics worth understanding before you apply.

  • Meaningful work experience: Glasgow expects evidence of healthcare exposure, whether through shadowing, volunteering in a care setting, or other patient-facing roles. The quality of your reflection on that experience matters more than the number of hours logged.
  • Awareness of health inequalities: Given Glasgow's context — the city has historically faced significant public health challenges — applicants who demonstrate genuine awareness of social factors in health tend to stand out.
  • Academic consistency: A strong predicted grade profile with no significant weak subjects is important. Glasgow looks at the full picture.
  • A personal statement with substance: Your personal statement should connect your experiences to specific insights about medicine, not simply list activities. Admissions tutors read thousands of statements — clarity and genuine reflection are what make one memorable.
  • Commitment to Scotland or the NHS: While not a formal requirement, applicants who can articulate why they want to study and potentially work in Scotland are often more compelling candidates.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to University of Glasgow

What UCAT score do I need for University of Glasgow?

Glasgow does not publish a fixed cut-off, but competitive applicants typically score around 2700 or above in the cognitive subtests. The SJT band also contributes to your overall profile. Because Glasgow is the largest medical school in Scotland and attracts a high volume of strong applicants, a score in the upper half of the national distribution is generally needed to progress to interview.

Is work experience required to apply to University of Glasgow Medicine?

Work experience is not formally mandatory, but it is strongly expected. Glasgow wants to see that you have engaged with healthcare in a meaningful way and can reflect on what you observed. Shadowing a GP, volunteering in a hospital or care home, or working in a community health setting all count — what matters is that you can speak thoughtfully about what you learned.

How should I prepare for the Glasgow panel interview?

Start by reviewing your personal statement thoroughly, as interviewers will often use it as a starting point. Practise discussing your work experience reflectively, not just descriptively. Read up on current NHS issues, particularly those relevant to Scotland, and familiarise yourself with common medical ethics frameworks. Mock interviews with someone who can give honest feedback — a teacher, tutor, or doctor — are genuinely useful preparation.

Does the University of Glasgow accept international students onto its Medicine programme?

Yes, the University of Glasgow does accept international students, including those from outside the UK and EU. International applicants are subject to the same academic and UCAT requirements, and the number of places available to international students is limited. Tuition fees for international students are significantly higher than for home students, and you should check the university's current fee schedule and any country-specific entry requirements before applying.
