Why Choose University of Exeter for Medicine?

University of Exeter is one of the newer medical schools in the UK, having admitted its first cohort in 2013, but it has built a strong reputation quickly. For students who want a modern, research-informed curriculum delivered in a genuinely beautiful part of England, Exeter offers something distinct. The school is part of the Peninsula Medical School partnership with Plymouth, which means students benefit from a wide network of clinical placements across Devon and Cornwall — a region with diverse patient demographics, from rural farming communities to coastal towns and a significant elderly population. If you want early, meaningful clinical exposure rather than years of purely lecture-based learning, Exeter's approach is worth understanding carefully.

The city itself is compact, walkable, and consistently ranked among the best places to live in the UK. It has a strong student culture without the overwhelming scale of a major city, which many medical students find suits the intensity of the degree. The university's broader research strengths in areas like environmental science and psychology also feed into an interdisciplinary approach to medicine that appeals to students who think beyond the purely biomedical.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

The standard A-Level offer for Medicine at University of Exeter is A*AA, with Chemistry required as one of the three subjects. Biology is strongly preferred as a second science, though the school does consider applicants with other combinations — Physics or Maths alongside Chemistry is acceptable. What matters is that you can demonstrate the scientific rigour the course demands from day one.

If you are sitting your A-Levels and aiming for Exeter, you should be targeting top grades across all three subjects, not just your sciences. Exeter looks at the full academic picture, and a weaker performance in a non-science subject can raise questions about your capacity to manage the breadth of the medical curriculum. For Scottish Highers, the typical requirement is AAAAB or AAAAA at Advanced Higher level, with Chemistry essential. Graduate applicants should hold at least a 2:1 degree, though a First is more competitive in practice.

GCSE performance also matters. Exeter does not publish a rigid GCSE threshold, but successful applicants typically hold a strong profile at grade 7 or above across core subjects, with particular attention paid to English and the sciences. If your GCSE results were mixed, a strong upward trajectory at A-Level and a compelling application elsewhere can help, but it is worth being realistic about how competitive the pool is.

UCAT Requirements at University of Exeter

University of Exeter uses the UCAT as part of its admissions process, and given that it is a relatively newer school with a growing profile, competition for places is strong. Exeter does not publish a fixed minimum UCAT score, but in practice, applicants with a total scaled score below 2600 are unlikely to be competitive. Scoring in the 2700 to 2800 range or above puts you in a much stronger position, particularly if your Situational Judgement Test result is Band 1 or Band 2.

Exeter uses the UCAT alongside your academic profile to decide who to invite to interview — it is not used in isolation. This means a very high UCAT score will not compensate for weak predicted grades, and strong grades alone will not guarantee an interview if your UCAT performance is significantly below the cohort average. Preparing thoroughly for the UCAT, ideally starting in the spring of Year 12, gives you the best chance of a score that keeps all your options open.

The Interview Process at University of Exeter

Exeter uses a Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) format. This involves rotating through a series of short stations — typically around eight to ten — each lasting a few minutes, with a brief pause between them. Each station is assessed independently by a different interviewer, which means a difficult station does not derail your entire interview. Stations at Exeter tend to cover areas including ethical scenarios, communication exercises, role-play situations, and questions about your motivation for medicine and your understanding of the NHS.

What Exeter is looking for in the MMI is not rehearsed answers but genuine engagement. They want to see how you think through a problem in real time, how you communicate with someone you have just met, and whether you demonstrate the kind of empathy and self-awareness that medicine requires. Preparation should focus on practising out loud with another person, working through common ethical dilemmas, and reflecting honestly on your work experience so you can speak about it naturally rather than reciting a script.

What Makes a Strong University of Exeter Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, Exeter is looking for applicants who have genuinely engaged with medicine as a vocation rather than an aspiration. Work experience is important — not because you need an impressive list of placements, but because you need to be able to speak meaningfully about what you observed and what it taught you about patient care, clinical environments, and your own suitability for the profession.

Strong applicants to Exeter typically demonstrate the following:

  • Sustained work experience in a healthcare setting, ideally including both clinical and non-clinical environments such as a GP surgery, hospital ward, or care home
  • Evidence of caring responsibilities or voluntary work that shows genuine engagement with people in need
  • Intellectual curiosity beyond the A-Level syllabus — reading around medicine, engaging with health policy, or pursuing an Extended Project Qualification
  • Resilience and self-awareness, demonstrated through how they reflect on challenges in their personal statement
  • An understanding of the NHS and the pressures facing healthcare in the UK today

Exeter takes approximately 130 students per year onto its undergraduate Medicine programme. With thousands of applicants competing for those places, the difference between candidates who receive an offer and those who do not often comes down to the quality of reflection in the personal statement and performance at MMI — not just raw scores.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to University of Exeter

What UCAT score do I need for University of Exeter Medicine?

Exeter does not publish a fixed cutoff, but competitive applicants typically score 2650 or above in total, with a Situational Judgement result of Band 1 or Band 2. Scoring above 2750 places you comfortably within the competitive range. Your UCAT score is considered alongside your predicted grades, so both elements need to be strong.

Is work experience required to apply to University of Exeter Medicine?

Work experience is not formally required in the sense that there is no minimum number of hours specified, but it is effectively essential. Exeter expects applicants to demonstrate genuine insight into medicine and patient care, and without meaningful healthcare experience, it is very difficult to write a compelling personal statement or perform well at MMI. Aim for a range of settings rather than volume alone.

How should I prepare for the Exeter Medicine MMI?

Start by understanding the MMI format thoroughly so the structure itself does not surprise you on the day. Then practise answering ethical and scenario-based questions out loud with a partner or tutor who can give honest feedback. Reflect carefully on your work experience so you can discuss it naturally. Reading about current NHS challenges and medical ethics will help you engage confidently with the kinds of topics Exeter raises at interview.

Does University of Exeter Medicine accept international students?

University of Exeter does accept a small number of international students onto its Medicine programme each year, but the majority of places are reserved for Home students. International applicants are subject to the same academic and UCAT requirements, and competition is intense. Tuition fees for international students are significantly higher, so it is worth reviewing the university's current fee structure and any available scholarships before applying.
