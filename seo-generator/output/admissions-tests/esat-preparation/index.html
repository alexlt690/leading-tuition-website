The Engineering and Science Admissions Test (ESAT) is one of the most demanding pre-interview assessments in UK university admissions. It sits alongside your A-level studies, arrives at a point in the year when pressure is already high, and tests the kind of precise, applied scientific thinking that standard exam preparation rarely develops. Many strong students underestimate it — not because they lack ability, but because the ESAT rewards a specific style of reasoning that takes deliberate practice to build. Specialist preparation matters here not just for confidence, but because the test genuinely rewards those who have trained for its particular demands.

What Is the Engineering and Science Admissions Test?

The ESAT was introduced in 2024 as a replacement for several older admissions tests, and it is now used by the University of Cambridge as part of the admissions process for a range of engineering and science courses. At Cambridge, applicants to Engineering, Natural Sciences, Chemical Engineering and Biotechnology, and Veterinary Medicine are among those required to sit the test. Imperial College London also uses the ESAT for its Engineering programmes.

The test is administered online through Pearson VUE test centres and is typically sat in October, ahead of the UCAS deadline. It is a computer-based assessment, which itself requires some adjustment if you are more accustomed to written examinations.

What Does the Engineering and Science Admissions Test Test?

The ESAT is made up of five modules, each lasting 40 minutes and containing 27 multiple-choice questions. Every candidate sits Mathematics 1 as a compulsory module. They then choose two further modules from the following options: Mathematics 2, Physics, Chemistry, and Biology. The modules you sit will depend on the course you are applying for — Cambridge Engineering applicants, for example, typically sit Mathematics 1, Mathematics 2, and Physics.

The questions are not straightforward recall tasks. The ESAT is designed to assess how well you can apply mathematical and scientific knowledge to unfamiliar problems under time pressure. The Mathematics modules test topics including algebra, calculus, geometry, and statistics at a level that extends beyond GCSE but is broadly in line with A-level content — though the questions demand speed and accuracy that many students find challenging. The science modules test core A-level content but frame it in ways that require genuine understanding rather than memorised procedures.

How the Engineering and Science Admissions Test Is Scored

Each module is scored on a scale of 1.0 to 9.0, with 1.0 being the lowest and 9.0 the highest. Scores are calculated using a standardised process that accounts for the difficulty of each sitting, so your score reflects your performance relative to other candidates rather than simply the number of questions you answered correctly. There is no negative marking, which means leaving questions blank is never the right strategy.

Universities use ESAT scores as one part of a holistic admissions process. At Cambridge, your score is considered alongside your academic record, personal statement, and — if you are shortlisted — your interview performance. There is no single published cut-off score, but in practice, competitive scores tend to sit in the upper range of the distribution. Aiming for a score of 6.0 or above in each module is a reasonable working target, though this will vary by course and year.

How to Prepare Effectively

The most important thing to understand about ESAT preparation is that starting early makes a significant difference. Ideally, you should begin structured preparation at least eight to twelve weeks before the test date, which typically means starting in late July or August if you are sitting in October. Leaving preparation until September, when school resumes and other pressures mount, puts you at a real disadvantage.

Several preparation mistakes are common among applicants:

  • Treating it like an A-level paper. The ESAT rewards speed and problem-solving instinct, not just content knowledge. Practising under timed conditions is essential from early on.
  • Neglecting Mathematics 1. Because it is compulsory and may feel familiar, students sometimes under-prepare for it. In practice, it is where marks are most reliably won or lost.
  • Working through past papers without reviewing errors carefully. Completing questions is only half the work. Understanding precisely why a wrong answer was wrong — and what reasoning would have led to the correct one — is where real improvement happens.
  • Ignoring the computer-based format. Practising on screen, rather than on paper, helps you adapt to the interface and manage your time more effectively on the day.

Effective preparation combines thorough content review, targeted problem-solving practice, and regular timed sessions that simulate real test conditions. Building familiarity with the question style early means that by October, you are refining your approach rather than still learning the basics.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the registration deadline for the ESAT?

Registration for the ESAT typically closes in late September, ahead of the October test window. You must register through the official ESAT website and book a slot at a Pearson VUE test centre. Missing the registration deadline means you cannot sit the test that cycle, so it is important to confirm dates as early as possible — ideally in the summer before your application year.

What score should I be aiming for?

There is no officially published minimum score, and the competitive range shifts slightly each year. As a working target, aiming for 6.0 or above in each module puts you in a strong position for most Cambridge and Imperial courses. Your tutor can help you benchmark your practice scores and set realistic, course-specific targets as your preparation develops.

Are past papers available to practise with?

Because the ESAT is a relatively new test, the volume of official past material is currently limited. However, official specimen papers and some released materials are available through the ESAT website, and these should form the core of your timed practice. Your tutor will also draw on additional problem sets and question banks that closely replicate the style and difficulty of real ESAT questions.

How does working with a tutor actually help?

A tutor accelerates preparation in two specific ways. First, they identify the gaps and habits that are costing you marks — things that are genuinely difficult to spot when you are working alone. Second, they teach you how to approach unfamiliar problems systematically, which is the core skill the ESAT tests. Students who prepare with specialist support consistently develop stronger problem-solving instincts and better time management than those who rely on self-study alone.
