The Mathematics Admissions Test is one of the most demanding pre-interview assessments in the UK university application process. Unlike A-level papers, it is not designed to reward memorisation or formula recall — it is built to expose how you think mathematically under pressure. For many applicants, that distinction only becomes clear once they sit a past paper and find themselves genuinely stuck. Specialist preparation matters here not because the content is entirely unfamiliar, but because the style of reasoning the MAT demands is something most students have never been explicitly trained to do. Working with a tutor who understands the test deeply can make the difference between a score that opens doors and one that closes them before interview.

What Is the Mathematics Admissions Test?

The Mathematics Admissions Test, commonly known as the MAT, is a paper-based admissions assessment used by the University of Oxford and Imperial College London as part of their selection process for several mathematically intensive courses. At Oxford, it is required for applicants to Mathematics, Mathematics and Statistics, Mathematics and Philosophy, and Computer Science (including joint courses). At Imperial, it is used for Mathematics and related programmes.

The test takes place in late October each year, typically around the last week of the month, and must be sat at an approved test centre — usually your school or college. It lasts two and a half hours and is sat without a calculator. Crucially, the MAT is sat before any interview invitation is issued, which means your score directly influences whether you are called for interview at all.

What Does the Mathematics Admissions Test Test?

The MAT is designed to assess mathematical reasoning, problem-solving, and the ability to construct and communicate a logical argument. The syllabus is broadly aligned with AS-level Mathematics, which means the content ceiling is intentionally lower than A-level — but the depth of thinking required is considerably higher. The test does not reward students who have simply covered more material; it rewards students who can use core ideas flexibly and creatively.

The paper contains multiple-choice questions and longer written questions. The multiple-choice section tests a wide range of topics quickly, while the written questions require full working and reward clear mathematical communication. Topics include algebra, calculus, sequences, geometry, and combinatorics, among others. Some questions are shared across all candidates; others are specific to the course applied for, so Computer Science applicants, for example, will encounter questions on logic and algorithms that Mathematics applicants will not.

How the Mathematics Admissions Test Is Scored

The MAT is marked out of 100. The multiple-choice section carries 40 marks in total, with four marks awarded per correct answer and no penalty for incorrect answers. The written questions carry the remaining 60 marks, distributed across several parts that increase in difficulty. Partial credit is available in the written section, which means clear working is always worth showing even if you cannot reach a final answer.

Oxford publishes score data each year, and the picture is sobering: the average score for all applicants typically sits in the low-to-mid 50s, while shortlisted candidates — those invited to interview — tend to score in the mid-60s or above. A score in the 70s is considered strong. Imperial uses the MAT score alongside other application components, but a competitive score remains essential. Neither university publishes a fixed threshold, and scores are considered in context alongside the rest of your application.

How to Prepare Effectively

Effective MAT preparation requires a different mindset from A-level revision. The following principles consistently separate well-prepared candidates from those who underperform despite strong grades:

  • Start early. Ideally, begin structured preparation in June or July of Year 12, giving yourself four to five months before the October sitting.
  • Work through past papers under timed conditions. Oxford publishes a substantial archive of past MAT papers, and working through them systematically is the single most valuable thing you can do.
  • Review every question you get wrong — and every question you got right by accident. Understanding why an answer is correct matters as much as getting it right.
  • Practise writing mathematics clearly. Many students lose marks in the written section not because their reasoning is wrong, but because it is not communicated in a way an examiner can follow.
  • Do not neglect the multiple-choice section. It is easy to assume these questions are straightforward, but they are carefully constructed to catch common errors in reasoning.

The most common mistake applicants make is treating MAT preparation as an extension of A-level revision. Drilling textbook exercises will not build the problem-solving instinct the test requires. You need to spend time on unfamiliar problems, sit with difficulty, and develop the habit of exploring a question from multiple angles before committing to an approach.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the registration deadline for the MAT?

The registration deadline is typically in mid-October, a few weeks before the test date. Your school or college must register you as a candidate — you cannot register independently. It is important to confirm with your school well in advance, ideally by September, that they are aware you need to sit the test and have arranged an approved centre.

What score should I be aiming for?

There is no single target score, but based on Oxford's published data, a score of 65 or above gives you a realistic chance of being shortlisted for interview. Scores in the 70s are considered strong. Rather than fixating on a number, focus on consistent improvement through timed practice and honest review of your errors.

Are past MAT papers available to practise with?

Yes. Oxford publishes a substantial archive of past MAT papers on its website, along with worked solutions. These are an essential part of any serious preparation programme. Our tutors use these papers as the foundation of structured practice, supplemented by additional problem sets where needed.

How does working with a tutor actually help with the MAT?

The MAT tests a style of mathematical thinking that most students have not been explicitly taught. A specialist tutor helps you develop that thinking in a structured way — identifying the specific gaps in your reasoning, giving you feedback on your written answers that goes beyond simply marking them right or wrong, and helping you build the problem-solving instincts that the test is designed to reward. Many students find that their scores improve significantly once they understand what the test is actually looking for.
