The English Literature Admissions Test is one of the most intellectually demanding written assessments a sixth-form student can face. Unlike many admissions tests, it gives you no multiple choice questions, no right answers to memorise, and no formula to follow. Instead, you are handed a set of unseen literary passages and asked to write a comparative essay of genuine analytical quality — under timed conditions, with no preparation for the specific texts in front of you. For students who love literature, that can sound exciting in theory and terrifying in practice. Specialist preparation matters here not because it teaches you what to think, but because it trains you to think with the speed, precision, and confidence the test demands.

What Is the English Literature Admissions Test?

The ELAT, or English Literature Admissions Test, is an admissions assessment used by the University of Oxford for applicants to a range of English-based undergraduate courses. These include English Language and Literature, English and Modern Languages, English and Linguistics, Classics and English, History and English, and Philosophy and English. If you are applying to any of these courses at Oxford, you will almost certainly be required to sit the ELAT as part of your application.

The test is sat in late October or early November, in line with Oxford's admissions timeline. You must register separately for the test — it does not happen automatically when you submit your UCAS application. Registration is managed through Pearson VUE, and tests are taken at authorised centres. The test lasts 90 minutes and consists of a single essay question. You are given a booklet containing six short literary extracts — drawn from poetry, prose, and drama — spanning a range of periods and styles. You choose two of these extracts and write a comparative essay exploring connections and differences between them.

What Does the English Literature Admissions Test Test?

The ELAT is designed to assess the skills that Oxford tutors most want to see in a literature student: close reading, analytical precision, and the ability to construct a coherent, well-evidenced argument about language and form. It is emphatically not a test of how much literature you have read, or whether you recognise the texts in front of you. The passages are chosen precisely because most applicants will not have studied them before.

What the test rewards is your ability to read carefully and respond to what is actually on the page — noticing how a writer uses imagery, syntax, tone, structure, and voice to create meaning. It also rewards intellectual independence: the capacity to form your own interpretation rather than reaching for safe, generic observations. The most impressive responses tend to be those that identify something genuinely interesting in the texts and pursue it with rigour, rather than those that attempt to cover everything.

Common mistakes that weaker candidates make include:

  • Choosing the two extracts too quickly, without reading all six carefully
  • Writing about the texts separately rather than comparatively throughout
  • Paraphrasing content instead of analysing language and form
  • Making broad thematic claims without grounding them in specific textual evidence
  • Spending too long on planning and leaving insufficient time to write a full, developed essay

How the English Literature Admissions Test Is Scored

The ELAT is marked on a scale of 0 to 60. Essays are assessed by trained markers using a detailed mark scheme that rewards analytical quality, clarity of argument, and engagement with literary technique. There is no single correct interpretation — markers are looking for essays that demonstrate genuine critical thinking and precise use of evidence from the texts.

Oxford does not publish a fixed score threshold for interview, but historically, candidates who are shortlisted for interview tend to score in the region of 45 to 55 or above. The ELAT score is considered alongside your personal statement, predicted grades, and school reference, but it carries significant weight in determining who is called to interview. A strong score will not guarantee an offer, but a weak score will almost certainly end an application early.

How to Prepare Effectively

Effective ELAT preparation requires a minimum of six to eight weeks of focused, structured work, ideally beginning in September of your application year. Students who begin earlier — in the summer after Year 12 — have more time to develop the analytical habits the test rewards, rather than simply practising under pressure.

The most productive preparation combines regular close reading practice with timed essay writing and detailed feedback. Reading widely across poetry, prose, and drama from different periods will sharpen your instinct for literary technique, but it must be active reading — the kind where you ask yourself how a text is working, not just what it is saying. Practising with past ELAT papers is essential, and Oxford does make a number of past papers publicly available on its admissions website. Working through these under timed conditions, then reviewing your essays critically, is the most direct way to improve.

It is also worth developing a reliable process for the extract-selection stage. Spending five to seven minutes reading all six passages and identifying which pairing offers the richest comparative possibilities will consistently produce better essays than rushing to the first two that seem familiar or accessible.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the registration deadline for the ELAT?

Registration for the ELAT is typically required by mid-October, ahead of the test date in late October or early November. You must register through Pearson VUE and book a test centre separately from your UCAS application. Missing the registration deadline means you cannot sit the test, which will effectively end your Oxford application for English-based courses. Check the Oxford admissions website each year for the exact deadline, as dates can shift slightly.

What score should I be aiming for?

While Oxford does not publish a fixed cut-off, candidates who are shortlisted for interview typically score around 45 or above out of 60, with stronger candidates often scoring in the low to mid 50s. Aiming for 50 or above is a sensible target. More importantly, focus on the quality of your analytical thinking rather than chasing a number — consistent improvement in how you read and argue will naturally move your score upward.

Are past ELAT papers available to practise with?

Yes. Oxford makes a selection of past ELAT papers freely available on its undergraduate admissions website. These are an essential resource and should form the backbone of your timed practice. Working through them under realistic conditions — 90 minutes, no notes, handwritten — and then reviewing your essays critically will give you the clearest picture of where you need to improve.

How does working with a tutor actually help with the ELAT?

The ELAT is difficult to prepare for alone because it is hard to evaluate your own analytical writing objectively. A specialist tutor can identify patterns in your essays — whether you are paraphrasing rather than analysing, making claims without evidence, or writing descriptively rather than comparatively — and give you targeted feedback that self-study rarely produces. A tutor also helps you develop a reliable process for the test itself, so that on the day you are not making decisions under pressure for the first time.
