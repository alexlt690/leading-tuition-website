The Sixth Term Examination Paper is widely regarded as one of the most demanding admissions tests in the UK. Unlike A-level papers, it does not reward memorised methods or routine practice — it asks you to think mathematically in ways that may feel entirely unfamiliar at first. For many applicants, the gap between strong A-level performance and genuine STEP readiness is larger than they expect, and discovering that gap late can be costly. Specialist preparation matters here not because the content is beyond you, but because the style of thinking required takes sustained, guided effort to develop.

What Is the Sixth Term Examination Paper?

STEP is a written mathematics examination used primarily by the University of Cambridge as a condition of offer for its Mathematics and related degree courses. The University of Warwick also uses STEP for its Mathematics degree, and a small number of other universities may request or consider STEP results for courses with a strong mathematical component. If you have received or are expecting a Cambridge offer in Mathematics, Computer Science, or Engineering, it is worth checking your specific offer conditions carefully, as requirements vary by course and college.

There are two papers currently in use: STEP 2 and STEP 3. STEP 1 was discontinued after 2021. Both papers are sat in June, alongside your A-level examinations. Each paper lasts three hours and contains thirteen questions — eight in Pure Mathematics, three in Mechanics, and two in Statistics. You are expected to attempt no more than six questions per paper, and only your best six answers count towards your final mark.

What Does the Sixth Term Examination Paper Test?

STEP tests mathematical reasoning, not mathematical knowledge alone. The syllabus broadly aligns with A-level Further Mathematics, but the questions demand that you apply ideas in unfamiliar contexts, construct rigorous arguments, and sustain logical thinking across extended problems. A question may begin with a standard-looking setup and then ask you to generalise, prove, or adapt your method in ways that require genuine insight.

The skills being assessed include:

  • The ability to read a problem carefully and identify the correct mathematical approach without being guided towards it
  • Constructing clear, complete proofs and justifications rather than simply arriving at a numerical answer
  • Handling multi-part questions where later parts build on earlier results, sometimes in unexpected directions
  • Working fluently across Pure Mathematics topics including calculus, algebra, coordinate geometry, and series
  • Applying Mechanics and Statistics concepts with the same depth of reasoning expected in Pure questions

Students who have only practised A-level-style questions often find that STEP problems feel opaque at first. This is normal, and it is precisely why early, structured preparation makes such a significant difference.

How the Sixth Term Examination Paper Is Scored

Each question is marked out of 20. Your six best answers are totalled to give a raw score out of 120. This raw score is then converted into one of five grades: S (Outstanding), 1 (Very Good), 2 (Good), 3 (Satisfactory), or U (Unclassified). Cambridge offers in Mathematics typically require a grade 1 in both STEP 2 and STEP 3, though some colleges specify a grade S in at least one paper. Warwick commonly asks for a grade 2 in STEP 2 or STEP 3. You should treat your specific offer conditions as the definitive guide.

It is worth understanding that a grade 1 does not require a perfect paper. Producing four or five well-reasoned, substantially complete answers will often be sufficient. Attempting to rush through six questions and leaving several incomplete is a common and costly mistake.

How to Prepare Effectively

Effective STEP preparation requires time — ideally beginning no later than January of Year 13, which gives you approximately five to six months before the June sitting. Starting in the autumn term of Year 13 is better still, particularly if you are sitting both STEP 2 and STEP 3. Preparation in the final few weeks before the exam alone is not sufficient.

The most productive approach involves working through past papers systematically, but not simply attempting questions and moving on. When you cannot solve a problem, the priority is understanding why — identifying the insight or technique you were missing, and then returning to similar problems until that approach feels natural. Reading model solutions passively is far less effective than attempting problems under realistic conditions and then analysing where your reasoning diverged.

One of the most common mistakes is neglecting Mechanics and Statistics questions on the assumption that Pure questions are safer. In practice, well-prepared candidates often find that Mechanics questions offer a more accessible route to a high mark, and ignoring them unnecessarily limits your options on the day.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the registration deadline for STEP?

STEP is administered by Cambridge Assessment Admissions Testing. Registration is handled through your school or college, and the deadline typically falls in early spring — usually around March. You should speak to your examinations officer well in advance, as late entries may not be accepted. Check the Cambridge Assessment website for the confirmed deadline for your sitting year.

What score should I be aiming for?

This depends entirely on your offer conditions. Most Cambridge Mathematics offers require grade 1 in both STEP 2 and STEP 3. Some colleges ask for a grade S in one paper. Warwick typically requires a grade 2. Confirm your specific requirements with your offer letter and aim to exceed them where possible, since exam conditions can affect performance.

Are past papers available to practise with?

Yes. A substantial archive of past STEP papers, mark schemes, and worked solutions is freely available through the Cambridge Assessment website and through NRICH, which hosts a dedicated STEP preparation resource. These are an essential part of any serious preparation programme and should be used under timed conditions as your exam date approaches.

How does working with a tutor actually help with STEP?

STEP is difficult to prepare for independently because it is hard to know whether your reasoning is on the right track without expert feedback. A specialist tutor can identify the specific gaps in your mathematical thinking, show you how to approach problem types you find opaque, and give you honest, detailed feedback on your written solutions — the kind of feedback that past papers and mark schemes alone cannot provide.
