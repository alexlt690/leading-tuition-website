MLAT Preparation

The Modern Languages Admissions Test is one of the more demanding written assessments in the UK university admissions process — not because it requires obscure knowledge, but because it asks you to think analytically about language, literature, and translation under timed conditions. For many applicants, that combination is unfamiliar. You may be an excellent linguist with strong A-level grades, but the MLAT tests a different kind of ability: close reading, critical reasoning, and the capacity to engage with unseen texts in a structured, academic way. Specialist preparation matters because the skills the test rewards are learnable, but they need to be practised deliberately and with expert guidance.

What Is the Modern Languages Admissions Test?

The MLAT is used by the University of Oxford as part of its admissions process for undergraduate courses involving modern languages. It is sat in November, typically in the same window as other Oxford admissions tests, and is taken at your school or college, or at an authorised test centre if your school is not registered.

The test is required for the following Oxford courses: Modern Languages, Modern Languages and Linguistics, European and Middle Eastern Languages, and Asian and Middle Eastern Languages. If you are applying for a joint course that includes a modern language — such as Classics and Modern Languages, or History and Modern Languages — you will also be required to sit the MLAT. It is worth confirming the specific requirements for your course on the Oxford admissions pages, as the components you sit will depend on which languages you are applying to study.

What Does the Modern Languages Admissions Test Test?

The MLAT is not a single uniform test. It is made up of different components depending on the languages involved in your application. Most candidates will sit a combination of the following:

  • Essay in English: A 30-minute essay responding to a short passage, testing your ability to construct a clear, well-reasoned argument about language, literature, or culture.
  • Language-specific components: These vary by language and may include translation into English, comprehension of an unseen passage, or a short written response in the target language.
  • Linguistics section: Required for applicants to Modern Languages and Linguistics, this section tests logical reasoning about language structure and does not assume prior knowledge of linguistics.

The test is designed to assess how you think, not just what you know. Oxford tutors are looking for evidence of intellectual curiosity, precision in reading, and the ability to articulate ideas clearly. Strong A-level performance is expected, but the MLAT is specifically designed to differentiate between candidates who all have excellent predicted grades.

How the Modern Languages Admissions Test Is Scored

Each section of the MLAT is marked separately, and the scoring varies by component. Written responses are assessed by Oxford academics who are looking for quality of argument, accuracy, and engagement with the source material — not simply for correct answers in a multiple-choice sense. There is no negative marking.

Oxford does not publish a fixed score threshold for interview selection. Instead, your MLAT score is considered alongside your personal statement, academic reference, and predicted grades as part of a holistic review. That said, the test carries significant weight. Candidates who score well are considerably more likely to be called for interview, and the score continues to be a factor even at the interview stage. Oxford does release data on score distributions each year, which gives a useful indication of where competitive candidates tend to fall.

How to Prepare Effectively

Most applicants underestimate how much focused preparation the MLAT requires, and many begin too late. Ideally, you should start structured preparation at least eight to ten weeks before the test date, which typically falls in early November. If you are sitting in Year 13, that means beginning in September at the latest — while managing the demands of A-level coursework and your UCAS application simultaneously.

The most common mistake is treating the MLAT like a language exam and focusing only on vocabulary or grammar revision. The test rewards analytical thinking and essay construction as much as linguistic competence. Candidates who practise writing timed essays, who read widely in their target language, and who engage critically with literary and cultural texts tend to perform significantly better than those who revise passively.

Another frequent error is neglecting the English essay component. Many students assume that because it is written in English, it requires less preparation. In practice, this section is highly competitive and demands a level of academic writing that goes beyond what most A-level courses require. Practising with past prompts and receiving detailed feedback on your arguments is essential.

For the linguistics section, the key is to approach it as a logic puzzle rather than a knowledge test. Practising with sample questions helps you become comfortable with the format, but the underlying skill is careful, systematic reasoning — something that improves with practice and reflection.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the registration deadline for the MLAT?
You must be registered to sit the MLAT by mid-October — the exact date varies slightly each year, so check the Oxford admissions website and your school's exams office well in advance. If your school is not a registered test centre, you will need to arrange an alternative venue before the deadline.

What score should I be aiming for?
Oxford does not publish a fixed benchmark, but reviewing the score distribution data released after each admissions cycle gives a useful guide. In competitive components, candidates called for interview typically score in the upper portion of the distribution. Your tutor can help you interpret this data in the context of your specific course and language combination.

Are past papers available for the MLAT?
Yes. Oxford publishes past MLAT papers on its admissions testing pages, along with some specimen materials. These are an essential part of your preparation and should be used under timed conditions to simulate the real experience as closely as possible.

How does working with a tutor actually help?
The most significant benefit of working with a specialist tutor is the quality of feedback you receive on your written work. It is very difficult to identify weaknesses in your own essays or translations without an expert eye. A tutor can pinpoint exactly where your arguments lose clarity, where your translation choices are imprecise, and how to address those issues before test day — something self-study alone rarely achieves.
