The Physics Aptitude Test is one of the most demanding pre-interview assessments in UK university admissions. Unlike A-level papers, it is designed to stretch even the strongest physics students — testing not just what you know, but how well you can think under pressure with unfamiliar problems. Many applicants underestimate it until they sit a past paper and find themselves stuck within the first few minutes. If you are applying to Oxford to study Physics, Engineering, or a related course, specialist preparation is not a luxury — it is a practical necessity.

What Is the Physics Aptitude Test?

The Physics Aptitude Test (PAT) is a written admissions test set and used by the University of Oxford. It is required for applicants to the following undergraduate courses: Physics, Physics and Philosophy, Engineering Science, and Materials Science. The test is sat in late October or early November, in line with Oxford's admissions timeline, and must be taken before any interview invitation is issued.

Since 2023, the PAT has been delivered as an online test through Pearson VUE test centres. The test lasts two hours and consists of approximately 27 questions of varying length and difficulty. Questions are a mixture of multiple-choice and written answer formats. The written sections require candidates to show their working, which means mathematical reasoning and clarity of method are both assessed. There are no separate sections — physics and mathematics content is integrated throughout the paper.

What Does the Physics Aptitude Test Test?

The PAT draws on GCSE mathematics and A-level physics content, but the way it applies that knowledge is significantly more demanding than standard exam questions. Oxford expects applicants to have covered most of the A-level Physics syllabus before sitting the test, even if they are still in Year 12 when they apply. The mathematical content goes beyond what many students have formally studied by October of Year 13.

The test assesses problem-solving ability across several areas of physics and mathematics, including:

  • Mechanics — forces, motion, energy, and momentum
  • Waves and optics — including interference and diffraction
  • Electricity and magnetism
  • Thermal physics and properties of matter
  • Mathematical skills — algebra, calculus, geometry, and trigonometry
  • Problem-solving with multi-step reasoning and unfamiliar scenarios

What distinguishes the PAT from A-level papers is the expectation that you will encounter questions you have never seen before and work through them logically. Rote learning is not enough. The test rewards students who have developed genuine physical intuition and mathematical fluency.

How the Physics Aptitude Test Is Scored

The PAT is marked out of 100. Each question carries a set number of marks, and for written questions, partial credit is available — meaning that showing clear, correct working matters even if your final answer is wrong. There is no negative marking, so leaving a question blank is always worse than attempting it.

Oxford does not publish a fixed pass mark or minimum threshold. Scores are used alongside your personal statement, predicted grades, and reference to decide who is invited to interview. In practice, competitive applicants typically score in the range of 60 to 75 or above, though this varies by year and cohort. Oxford releases score distributions after each admissions cycle, which gives a useful picture of where successful candidates tend to fall. The key point is that the PAT is not a pass/fail test — it is a ranking tool, and every mark counts.

How to Prepare Effectively

The most common mistake applicants make is leaving PAT preparation too late. If you are applying in Year 13, you should begin structured preparation no later than June or July — giving yourself at least three to four months before the October sitting. Applicants who start in September and rely on cramming past papers rarely perform as well as those who have spent the summer consolidating their physics knowledge and mathematical technique.

Effective preparation has three stages. First, identify and close any gaps in your physics and mathematics knowledge — particularly topics that appear on the PAT but may not yet have been covered in your A-level teaching. Second, develop problem-solving technique by working through PAT past papers under timed conditions and reviewing every question you found difficult, not just the ones you got wrong. Third, practise working efficiently under time pressure, because two hours for 27 questions of varying complexity requires careful pacing.

Avoid the trap of only practising questions you find comfortable. The PAT is specifically designed to include unfamiliar problems, and the ability to stay calm and reason carefully when you do not immediately recognise a question type is one of the most important skills you can build.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the registration deadline for the PAT?

The registration deadline is typically in early October, ahead of the late October or early November test date. You register through a Pearson VUE test centre, not through Oxford directly. It is important to register well in advance, as test centre spaces can fill up — particularly in areas with high numbers of Oxford applicants.

What score should I be aiming for?

There is no official target, but based on Oxford's published score distributions, a score of around 60 or above is generally considered competitive, with stronger candidates often scoring in the 65 to 80 range. That said, the PAT is just one part of your application, and a very strong personal statement or interview can complement a slightly lower score.

Are past papers available to practise with?

Yes. Oxford publishes past PAT papers on its website, going back a number of years, along with mark schemes. These are an essential part of preparation. However, the format changed in 2023 with the move to online delivery, so it is worth being aware that some older papers reflect a slightly different structure.

How does working with a tutor actually help?

A good PAT tutor does more than go through past papers with you. They identify the specific gaps in your knowledge and reasoning that are costing you marks, teach you how to approach unfamiliar problem types systematically, and help you build the mathematical confidence the test demands. Many students find that their score improves significantly once they have someone experienced pointing out exactly where their thinking is going wrong — something that is very difficult to spot on your own.
