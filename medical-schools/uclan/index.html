Why Choose University of Central Lancashire for Medicine?

University of Central Lancashire (UCLan) is one of the newer medical schools in England, having admitted its first cohort of undergraduate medical students in 2015. That relative youth is, in many ways, a strength. UCLan Medicine was built with a clear purpose: to train doctors who are rooted in community and primary care, who understand the health needs of populations that are often underserved, and who are prepared for the realities of modern NHS practice from day one. If you are drawn to a medical school that takes its regional identity seriously — one where your clinical placements will take you into real communities across Lancashire and the North West rather than a single large teaching hospital — UCLan deserves serious consideration. Preston itself is an underrated city: affordable, well-connected by rail to Manchester and Liverpool, and with a student population large enough to give the university genuine energy without the overwhelming scale of a Russell Group campus.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

UCLan's typical A-Level offer for Medicine is AAA, which includes Chemistry as a required subject. Biology is strongly recommended and, in practice, the vast majority of successful applicants hold both Chemistry and Biology at A-Level. The third A-Level can be from a wide range of subjects, and UCLan does not penalise students for choosing humanities or social sciences alongside their sciences — something worth noting if you have a broader academic profile.

For students taking the Scottish Highers, the equivalent offer is AAAAB or AAAAA at Advanced Higher/Higher level, with Chemistry required. UCLan also considers applicants with a relevant Access to Higher Education Diploma, which reflects the school's genuine commitment to widening participation in medicine. If you are a widening participation applicant — for example, if you attended a state school in a low-progression area, or if you are the first in your family to apply to university — it is worth checking UCLan's contextual admissions criteria carefully, as these can make a meaningful difference to how your application is assessed.

UCLan does not accept General Studies or Critical Thinking as one of the three A-Levels counted in the offer. Resit applicants are considered, though the school will want to understand the circumstances behind any resit, so be prepared to address this clearly in your personal statement or at interview.

The course itself is a five-year MBChB programme. UCLan uses a systems-based, integrated curriculum, meaning that from early in the course you are studying clinical medicine and basic science together rather than spending two years in lecture theatres before you ever meet a patient. Early clinical exposure is a genuine feature of the programme, not just a marketing claim.

UCAT Requirements at University of Central Lancashire

UCLan uses the UCAT (University Clinical Aptitude Test) as part of its admissions process. The university does not publish a fixed minimum UCAT threshold in the way that some schools do, but your UCAT score is used to rank applicants and determine who is invited to interview. In practice, competitive applicants tend to score around or above the national average across the five cognitive subtests — roughly 2600 to 2700 combined — though this varies from cycle to cycle depending on the applicant pool.

The Situational Judgement Test (SJT) component of the UCAT is also considered. A Band 4 SJT score is likely to weaken your application, so it is worth preparing for this section as carefully as the cognitive subtests. UCLan's use of UCAT is broadly in line with other newer medical schools: it is a meaningful filter, but the school is not chasing the very highest scorers at the expense of other qualities. A strong UCAT combined with solid A-Level predictions and a well-constructed personal statement gives you a genuine chance of an interview invitation.

The Interview Process at University of Central Lancashire

UCLan uses a Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) format. MMIs consist of a series of short, structured stations — typically around eight to ten — each lasting a few minutes, with a brief gap between stations to read the next prompt. Each station is assessed independently by a different interviewer, which means a single difficult station does not define your overall performance.

At UCLan, MMI stations are likely to include scenarios testing your ethical reasoning, communication skills, and ability to reflect on your work experience. You may be asked to role-play a conversation, respond to a hypothetical clinical or social scenario, or discuss a current issue in healthcare. Stations assessing your motivation for medicine and your understanding of what a career as a doctor actually involves are common. UCLan places particular emphasis on community-oriented values and an understanding of health inequalities, so familiarity with NHS structures, primary care, and the social determinants of health will serve you well.

What Makes a Strong University of Central Lancashire Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, UCLan is looking for applicants who can demonstrate genuine engagement with medicine as a vocation rather than a status. The following qualities and experiences tend to characterise successful applicants:

  • Meaningful work experience: Shadowing a GP, volunteering in a care home, or working in a healthcare-adjacent role all demonstrate that you understand what patient care involves at ground level. UCLan's community focus means primary care experience is particularly relevant.
  • Reflective personal statement: UCLan wants to see that you have thought carefully about what you observed during your work experience — not just that you completed it. What did you learn? What challenged your assumptions?
  • Awareness of health inequalities: Given the school's mission to serve communities across Lancashire and the North West, applicants who can speak thoughtfully about why some populations have worse health outcomes will stand out.
  • Resilience and teamwork: Evidence from sport, volunteering, part-time work, or caring responsibilities all count. UCLan is not looking for a narrow academic profile.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to University of Central Lancashire

What UCAT score do I need for UCLan Medicine?
UCLan does not publish a fixed cut-off, but competitive applicants typically score around the national average or above — approximately 2600 to 2700 combined across the four cognitive subtests. Your SJT band also matters; aim for Band 1 or 2. Scores are used to rank applicants for interview, so the threshold shifts slightly each year.

Is work experience required to apply to UCLan Medicine?
UCLan does not specify a minimum number of hours, but work experience is expected and your personal statement should reflect on it meaningfully. Given the school's community health focus, experience in primary care, general practice, or community settings is particularly valuable, though hospital shadowing and care work are equally relevant.

How should I prepare for the UCLan MMI?
Practise thinking aloud through ethical scenarios and healthcare dilemmas — the GMC's Good Medical Practice guidance is a useful reference point. Mock MMI sessions, either with a tutor or through your school, will help you manage the time pressure of each station. Read about current NHS challenges, particularly in primary care and community health, as these themes align closely with UCLan's values.

Does UCLan Medicine accept international students?
UCLan does accept a small number of international students onto its MBChB programme, though the majority of places are reserved for home (UK) applicants. International applicants should check the university's current fees and entry requirements directly, as English language requirements and application processes may differ from the standard UCAS route.
