Why Choose King's College London for Medicine?

King's College London occupies a genuinely unique position in UK medical education. Situated across multiple campuses in the heart of London, it is one of the oldest and most respected medical schools in the country, with roots stretching back to 1829. What makes King's stand out is not simply its history or its rankings, but the sheer scale and quality of clinical exposure it offers from early in the programme. Students train across some of the busiest and most diverse NHS teaching hospitals in Europe — including King's College Hospital, Guy's and St Thomas', and the South London and Maudsley NHS Foundation Trust. If you want to understand what medicine looks like in a genuinely complex, multicultural, high-pressure environment, King's delivers that in a way that few other schools can match. For students who want London life alongside a rigorous, clinically grounded medical education, it is a compelling choice.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

King's College London is a competitive medical school, and its academic entry requirements reflect that. The standard offer for the five-year MBBS programme is A*AA at A-Level, with Chemistry as a required subject. Biology is strongly recommended and, in practice, the vast majority of successful applicants hold both Chemistry and Biology at A-Level. The third subject is flexible, though it should demonstrate academic rigour — subjects like Mathematics, Physics, Psychology, or a humanity are all well regarded.

King's also offers a six-year MBBS with an intercalated BSc route, which is built into the programme rather than being an optional extra. This means all students at King's graduate with both a medical degree and a Bachelor of Science, giving you deeper expertise in a chosen biomedical area and strengthening your academic profile for future specialty training or research. This is a genuine differentiator and worth factoring into your decision if you have an interest in the science underpinning medicine, not just its clinical practice.

For students taking Scottish Highers, the typical requirement is AAAAB or AAAAA at Higher level, usually with Advanced Highers in relevant subjects. International Baccalaureate applicants are typically expected to achieve 36–38 points overall, with 6,6,6 at Higher Level including Chemistry.

King's does not accept General Studies or Critical Thinking as one of your three A-Level subjects. Re-sit applicants are considered, though King's looks carefully at the circumstances and the trajectory of improvement.

UCAT Requirements at King's College London

King's College London uses the UCAT as part of its selection process, and your score plays a meaningful role in whether you are invited to interview. King's does not publish a fixed cut-off score, but in practice, competitive applicants tend to score in the higher deciles — a total score in the range of 2700 or above is generally considered competitive, though this varies year on year depending on the applicant pool.

King's uses the UCAT to rank applicants alongside academic performance before deciding who to invite for interview. This means a strong UCAT score can help offset a slightly less exceptional predicted grade profile, and vice versa. The Situational Judgement Test component is also considered, so do not neglect it in your preparation. Scoring in Band 1 or Band 2 for SJT is advisable.

Given that King's receives a very large number of applications each year — it is one of the most applied-to medical schools in the UK — your UCAT score is a genuine filter at the longlisting stage. Preparing thoroughly and sitting the test when you are properly ready is essential.

The Interview Process at King's College London

King's uses a Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) format. This means rather than a single panel interview, you rotate through a series of short, structured stations — typically around eight to ten — each lasting a few minutes and assessed by a different interviewer. Stations are designed to test a range of competencies rather than just your knowledge of medicine.

At King's, MMI stations commonly explore areas such as ethical reasoning, communication skills, empathy and patient-centred thinking, your understanding of the NHS and current healthcare challenges, and your motivation for medicine. Some stations may involve role-play scenarios — for example, responding to a distressed patient or navigating a difficult conversation — while others present ethical dilemmas or ask you to reflect on your work experience. There are also stations that assess your ability to think clearly under pressure and articulate your reasoning.

The MMI format rewards candidates who are genuine, reflective, and able to think on their feet. Rehearsing scripted answers tends to work against you — interviewers at King's are experienced at identifying candidates who are performing rather than engaging.

What Makes a Strong King's College London Application

King's admits approximately 340 students per year onto its MBBS programme, making it one of the larger medical school intakes in the UK. Competition is intense, and strong grades and a good UCAT score are necessary but not sufficient on their own.

Successful applicants to King's tend to share several qualities beyond academic achievement:

  • Meaningful work experience: King's expects you to have spent time in healthcare settings — this does not need to be clinical, but it should be reflective. Shadowing a GP, volunteering in a care home, or working in a hospital environment all count, provided you can articulate what you observed and what it taught you about the realities of medicine.
  • Awareness of NHS challenges: Given King's strong NHS partnerships, interviewers value applicants who understand pressures facing the health service — workforce issues, health inequalities, mental health provision, and the demands of working in a diverse urban population.
  • Academic curiosity: The integrated BSc means King's values students who are genuinely interested in the science of medicine, not just its practice. Evidence of wider reading, extended projects, or engagement with biomedical topics strengthens your application.
  • Resilience and self-awareness: Medicine in London is demanding. King's wants to see that you understand this and have thought seriously about why you are suited to it.

Your personal statement should be specific and honest. Avoid vague claims about wanting to help people — focus instead on what you have done, what you observed, and how it shaped your thinking.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to King's College London

What UCAT score do I need for King's College London?

King's does not publish a fixed minimum, but competitive applicants typically score around 2700 or above in the four cognitive subtests combined. The SJT score also matters — aim for Band 1 or Band 2. Scores are used to rank applicants before interview invitations are sent, so the higher your score, the stronger your position in a large applicant pool.

Is work experience required to apply to King's College London?

While King's does not specify a minimum number of hours, work experience in a healthcare or caring environment is expected and will be discussed at interview. What matters more than the quantity of experience is your ability to reflect on it meaningfully — what you saw, what challenged you, and what it confirmed or complicated about your decision to study medicine.

How should I prepare for the King's MMI?

Start by familiarising yourself with common MMI station types — ethical scenarios, role-play, current NHS issues, and motivation questions. Practise speaking your reasoning aloud, ideally with a partner who can give honest feedback. Read about healthcare topics in the news and think through your own views carefully. The goal is to be genuinely engaged and articulate, not to deliver polished scripts.

Does King's College London accept international students onto its medicine programme?

Yes, King's does accept a limited number of international students onto the MBBS programme each year. International applicants are subject to the same academic and UCAT requirements, and the application process follows the same UCAS route. Tuition fees for international students are significantly higher than for home students, and it is worth checking the King's admissions pages for the most current fee information and any country-specific requirements.
