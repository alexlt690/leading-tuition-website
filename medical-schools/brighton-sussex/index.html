Why Choose Brighton and Sussex Medical School for Medicine?

Brighton and Sussex Medical School (BSMS) occupies a genuinely unusual position in UK medical education. It is a joint school between the University of Brighton and the University of Sussex, meaning students benefit from the resources, campuses, and communities of two universities simultaneously. The school sits on the Falmer campus, nestled between the South Downs and one of the most vibrant cities in the UK, and it has built a reputation for producing doctors who are not only clinically competent but socially aware and research-literate. If you are drawn to a medical school that takes global health seriously, values critical thinking, and places you in a genuinely diverse clinical environment from early in your training, BSMS is worth serious consideration.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

The standard A-Level offer at Brighton and Sussex Medical School is AAA, with Chemistry required as one of the three subjects. Biology is strongly preferred as a second science, and most successful applicants do hold it. A small number of applicants are considered with one non-science A-Level alongside Chemistry and Biology, but the sciences form the backbone of what BSMS expects at entry. There is no A* requirement in the standard offer, which makes BSMS slightly more accessible on paper than some Russell Group schools — though competition remains fierce given the limited number of places available each year.

BSMS admits approximately 140 students per year to its five-year undergraduate medicine programme. Given the volume of applications medicine attracts nationally, that is a relatively small cohort, and every element of your application matters. AS-Level results, where available, and GCSE performance are also considered as part of the contextual picture, particularly when distinguishing between candidates with similar predicted grades.

International Baccalaureate applicants are welcome, with a typical offer of 36 points overall, including 6,6,6 at Higher Level with Chemistry required at Higher Level. Scottish Highers and other qualifications are considered on a case-by-case basis, and it is worth contacting the admissions team directly if you are taking a less common qualification pathway.

UCAT Requirements at Brighton and Sussex Medical School

BSMS uses the UCAT (University Clinical Aptitude Test) as part of its admissions process. The UCAT is sat in the summer before you apply, and your score is used alongside your academic profile and personal statement to determine whether you are invited to interview. BSMS does not publish a fixed minimum UCAT threshold, but in practice, competitive applicants tend to score in the region of 2700 or above across the four cognitive subtests, with a strong Situational Judgement score also valued. Because BSMS is a joint school between Brighton and Sussex, the admissions process is coordinated centrally, and UCAT scores are reviewed in the context of the full application rather than used as a blunt cut-off.

Preparing thoroughly for the UCAT is essential. The test rewards familiarity with question formats and timed practice far more than raw academic ability, so starting preparation several months in advance gives you a meaningful advantage.

The Interview Process at Brighton and Sussex Medical School

BSMS uses a Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) format. Rather than a single traditional panel interview, the MMI consists of a series of short stations — typically around eight to ten — each lasting a few minutes, where you rotate between different assessors and scenarios. Each station tests a different competency or quality, and your performance is scored independently at each one, which means a weaker moment at one station does not define your overall result.

At BSMS, MMI stations commonly explore areas such as ethical reasoning, communication skills, empathy, your understanding of the NHS and healthcare challenges, and your motivation for medicine. You may be asked to respond to a scenario, discuss a topical issue in healthcare, or demonstrate how you would handle a difficult interpersonal situation. Some stations are reflective — drawing on your work experience or personal statement — while others present entirely new situations you will not have encountered before.

Interviews are typically held between January and March. Preparing for the MMI format specifically, rather than rehearsing a single polished narrative, is the most effective approach. Practising out loud with another person, working through ethical dilemmas, and staying current with NHS news will all serve you well.

What Makes a Strong Brighton and Sussex Medical School Application

BSMS has a particular interest in students who demonstrate genuine curiosity about medicine as a science and a social practice. The school has a strong global health thread running through its curriculum, and applicants who show awareness of health inequalities, the social determinants of health, or international healthcare systems tend to stand out. This does not mean you need to have worked abroad — it means showing that you think beyond the clinical encounter.

Work experience is important, and BSMS expects applicants to have reflected meaningfully on what they observed. The following types of experience are all valued:

  • Shadowing in a GP surgery or hospital ward
  • Volunteering in a care home, hospice, or community health setting
  • Any role involving sustained responsibility for others
  • Research or extended project work with a scientific or medical focus

Your personal statement should not simply list what you have done — it should show what you learned and how it shaped your understanding of medicine. BSMS tutors read personal statements carefully, and a reflective, honest account of your experiences will always outperform a list of impressive-sounding activities with no depth behind them.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to Brighton and Sussex Medical School

What UCAT score do I need for Brighton and Sussex Medical School?

BSMS does not publish a fixed cut-off, but successful applicants typically score around 2700 or above across the four cognitive subtests. A strong Situational Judgement result also contributes positively. Because the score is considered alongside the rest of your application, a very strong academic profile or personal statement can provide some balance, but a significantly below-average UCAT score is likely to affect your chances of being shortlisted for interview.

Is work experience required to apply to BSMS?

Work experience is not formally mandated, but it is strongly expected. BSMS wants to see that you have had meaningful exposure to healthcare and that you have reflected on it. Applicants without any work experience are at a significant disadvantage. If access to clinical settings has been genuinely difficult, focus on what you have done and be honest about the context — admissions teams understand that opportunities are not equally distributed.

How should I prepare for the BSMS MMI?

The most effective preparation involves practising the MMI format itself — short, timed responses to scenarios — rather than memorising answers to common questions. Work through medical ethics frameworks, stay informed about current NHS issues, and practise speaking clearly and calmly under time pressure. Mock MMI sessions with a tutor or a practice partner who can give honest feedback are particularly valuable.

Does Brighton and Sussex Medical School accept international students?

BSMS does accept a small number of international students each year, though the majority of places are reserved for Home (UK) applicants. International applicants are subject to higher tuition fees and should check the current fee structure directly with the university. English language requirements apply, and all applicants — regardless of background — must sit the UCAT and follow the standard UCAS application process.
