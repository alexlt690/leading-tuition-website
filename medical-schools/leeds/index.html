University of Leeds Medicine Entry Requirements

Leeds is one of those medical schools that tends to grow on you the more you look into it. It has a large, well-established programme, a genuinely impressive clinical network across West Yorkshire, and a city that offers real quality of life without the cost of London. For students who want a rigorous, research-informed medical education alongside the experience of living in one of the UK's most vibrant student cities, Leeds makes a compelling case — and it does so consistently, year after year.

Why Choose University of Leeds for Medicine?

The University of Leeds School of Medicine has been training doctors for well over a century, and that experience shows in how the programme is structured. The curriculum integrates clinical exposure from early in the course, meaning students are not spending their first two years entirely in lecture theatres before they ever see a patient. Leeds uses a systems-based approach to teaching, weaving together anatomy, physiology, pathology, and clinical skills in a way that builds understanding progressively rather than in isolated blocks.

The clinical placement network is one of Leeds's genuine strengths. Students rotate through Leeds Teaching Hospitals NHS Trust — one of the largest teaching hospital trusts in the country — as well as placements across West Yorkshire and the wider Yorkshire and Humber region. This gives students exposure to a genuinely diverse patient population, from major trauma and tertiary referral cases at Leeds General Infirmary to community and primary care settings in surrounding towns. That breadth of experience is difficult to replicate at smaller schools.

Leeds itself is worth considering seriously as a place to live. It is a large, culturally rich city with a strong student community, affordable housing by UK standards, excellent transport links, and a medical school that feels embedded in its city rather than separate from it. For students who want to feel part of somewhere, rather than simply passing through, Leeds tends to deliver.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

The standard offer for Medicine at Leeds is AAA at A-Level, with Chemistry required as one of the three subjects. Biology is strongly preferred as a second science, and most successful applicants include it. A small number of offers may be made at A*AA depending on the applicant pool in a given cycle, so it is sensible to aim for that standard even if the published threshold is AAA.

Leeds does not accept General Studies or Critical Thinking as one of the three A-Level subjects. For students taking Scottish Highers, the typical requirement is AAAAB or AAAAA, with Chemistry and usually Biology included. International Baccalaureate applicants typically need 36 points overall, with 6,6,6 at Higher Level including Chemistry.

GCSE performance is considered as part of the application, particularly for shortlisting. Strong grades across a broad range of subjects — especially in the sciences and English — will support your application. Leeds takes a holistic view, but competitive applicants typically have a strong GCSE profile alongside their predicted grades.

Leeds offers approximately 230 places per year, making it one of the larger medical school intakes in the UK. This means the admissions process is well-practised and relatively transparent, but it also means competition is significant and every component of the application matters.

UCAT Requirements at University of Leeds

Leeds uses the UCAT as a key shortlisting tool. The university does not publish a fixed minimum score, but in practice, competitive applicants tend to score in the upper two deciles — broadly, a total scaled score above 2700 is a reasonable benchmark, though this varies year to year depending on the cohort. Leeds considers the overall UCAT score rather than weighting individual subtests, though a very low score in any one section can still affect your application.

The Situational Judgement Test (SJT) is also considered. A Band 4 result is likely to disadvantage an application significantly, and most successful applicants achieve Band 1 or Band 2. Given the size of the Leeds cohort and the volume of applications the school receives, UCAT performance plays a meaningful role in determining who gets invited to interview. Preparation should begin well before the test date — ideally several months in advance — using timed practice under realistic conditions.

The Interview Process at University of Leeds

Leeds uses a panel interview format. Applicants are interviewed by a small panel, typically including a clinician and a member of the academic staff, and sometimes a medical student. The interview is structured but conversational in style, and lasts approximately 20 to 30 minutes.

The panel will explore a range of areas, including your motivation for medicine, your understanding of the NHS and healthcare more broadly, your ability to reflect on work experience, and your awareness of ethical issues in medicine. Leeds is not looking for rehearsed answers — the panel is assessing how you think, how you communicate, and whether you demonstrate the values and self-awareness expected of a future doctor.

Candidates should be prepared to discuss their personal statement in detail, including specific experiences they have mentioned. Ethical scenarios and questions about current healthcare challenges are common. Demonstrating genuine curiosity about medicine — rather than a polished performance — tends to serve applicants well.

What Makes a Strong University of Leeds Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, Leeds is looking for applicants who can demonstrate genuine engagement with medicine as a vocation. The strongest applications tend to share several characteristics:

  • Meaningful work experience — not necessarily extensive, but reflected on thoughtfully. Shadowing a GP, volunteering in a care setting, or hospital volunteering all count, provided you can articulate what you observed and what it taught you.
  • A personal statement with depth — Leeds admissions tutors read a high volume of applications. Statements that describe experiences rather than simply listing them, and that connect those experiences to a developing understanding of medicine, stand out.
  • Evidence of resilience and teamwork — through sport, music, part-time work, or other sustained commitments. Leeds values applicants who have demonstrated they can manage responsibility and work with others.
  • Awareness of the wider NHS — understanding pressures facing healthcare, the role of primary care, and the importance of patient-centred practice shows intellectual engagement beyond the classroom.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to University of Leeds

What UCAT score do I need for Leeds Medicine?

Leeds does not publish a fixed cut-off, but competitive applicants typically achieve a total scaled score of around 2700 or above. The SJT is also considered, and a Band 4 result is likely to weaken an otherwise strong application. Because Leeds receives a large number of applications, UCAT performance is a significant shortlisting factor.

Is work experience required to apply to Leeds Medicine?

Work experience is not formally required, but it is strongly expected. Leeds wants to see that you have made a genuine effort to understand what a career in medicine involves. Shadowing, volunteering in healthcare or care settings, and any sustained engagement with vulnerable people all contribute. What matters most is your ability to reflect meaningfully on what you experienced.

How should I prepare for the Leeds Medicine panel interview?

Start by revisiting your personal statement carefully — the panel may ask about anything you have written. Practise discussing your work experience in a reflective way, and read around current NHS issues and medical ethics. Mock interviews with someone who can give honest feedback are valuable. The Leeds panel is looking for thoughtful, self-aware candidates, so practise thinking aloud rather than reciting prepared answers.

Does the University of Leeds accept international students for Medicine?

Yes, Leeds does accept international students, though the number of places available to overseas applicants is limited. International applicants are subject to the same academic and UCAT requirements, and should check the university's current guidance on English language requirements and any country-specific qualifications. Tuition fees for international students differ significantly from home fees, so early financial planning is important.
