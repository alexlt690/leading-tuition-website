St George's University of London Medicine Entry Requirements

St George's University of London occupies a genuinely unusual position in UK medical education. It is the country's only university dedicated exclusively to healthcare and medical sciences, meaning that from the moment you arrive, you are surrounded entirely by people training to work in health. There are no law students, no engineers, no business undergraduates — just a focused, clinically minded community on a single campus in Tooting, south London. For students who know they want medicine and want to immerse themselves in it completely, that environment is difficult to replicate elsewhere. Add to this the fact that St George's shares its site with St George's Hospital, one of the UK's major teaching hospitals, and you begin to understand why so many students choose it specifically rather than simply as a London option.

Why Choose St George's University of London for Medicine?

St George's has been training doctors since 1733, making it one of the oldest medical schools in the country. Its curriculum is built around early clinical exposure — students begin engaging with patients and clinical environments from the first year, rather than spending two years in lecture theatres before setting foot in a hospital. The integrated approach means that basic science and clinical practice are taught alongside each other throughout the degree, which many students find both more motivating and more effective for long-term retention.

The location in south London is a genuine advantage rather than a marketing point. St George's Hospital is a major trauma centre and one of London's busiest acute hospitals, and students benefit from placements across a wide network of NHS trusts spanning south London, Surrey, and beyond. London's population is extraordinarily diverse, which means students encounter a breadth of presentations, languages, cultures, and health inequalities that prepares them well for practice anywhere in the UK. The campus itself is compact and community-focused, which creates a collegiate atmosphere that larger, multi-faculty universities sometimes struggle to achieve.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

St George's typically makes offers of A*AA at A-Level, with Chemistry as an essential subject. Biology is strongly preferred as a second science, though some applicants with Physics or Mathematics alongside Chemistry are considered. It is important to check the current prospectus for any updates, as entry requirements can shift slightly between admissions cycles.

General Studies and Critical Thinking are not accepted as part of the offer. If you are taking four A-Levels, St George's will consider your performance across all of them, but the offer is based on three. For students taking Scottish Highers or the International Baccalaureate, equivalent qualifications are accepted — the IB offer is typically around 37 to 38 points with strong scores in Higher Level Chemistry and Biology.

St George's also offers a four-year graduate-entry programme for applicants who already hold a degree, typically requiring a 2:1 or above in a relevant subject. This is a separate and competitive pathway with its own application process and UCAT requirement.

UCAT Requirements at St George's University of London

St George's uses the UCAT (University Clinical Aptitude Test) as a significant part of its selection process. As the UK's only specialist medical university, it receives a high volume of academically strong applications, and the UCAT helps distinguish between candidates who meet the grade requirements. St George's uses UCAT scores to create a ranked shortlist for interview, which means your score directly affects whether you are invited to the next stage.

There is no fixed published cut-off score, but in practice, competitive applicants tend to score in the upper third of the national cohort — broadly speaking, a total scaled score above 2700 is generally considered competitive, though this varies year to year depending on the applicant pool. The Situational Judgement Test (SJT) is also considered, and a Band 4 result may disadvantage your application. Preparing thoroughly for the UCAT — ideally starting in the spring of Year 12 — is strongly advisable.

The Interview Process at St George's University of London

St George's uses a Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) format. Rather than a single panel interview, the MMI consists of a series of short, structured stations — typically around eight to ten — each lasting a few minutes, with a brief pause between them to read the next prompt. Each station is assessed independently by a different interviewer, which reduces the impact of any single poor performance and gives you multiple opportunities to demonstrate your strengths.

The stations at St George's are designed to assess a range of competencies rather than medical knowledge. You might encounter:

  • Ethical scenarios requiring you to reason through a dilemma calmly and clearly
  • Role-play exercises, such as breaking difficult news or navigating a challenging conversation
  • Questions about your motivation for medicine and your understanding of the NHS
  • Stations testing communication skills or empathy
  • Discussion of current healthcare issues or your work experience

Preparation should focus on practising out loud rather than memorising answers. The assessors are looking for genuine reflection, structured thinking, and the interpersonal qualities that suggest you will become a good doctor — not polished scripts.

What Makes a Strong St George's University of London Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, St George's is looking for applicants who can demonstrate genuine engagement with medicine as a vocation. Work experience is important — not because you need an impressive list of placements, but because you need to be able to reflect meaningfully on what you observed and what it taught you about patient care, clinical environments, and your own suitability for the profession. Shadowing a GP, volunteering in a care home, or spending time in a hospital ward all count, provided you can articulate what you took from the experience.

Your personal statement should be specific and honest. Admissions tutors read thousands of statements, and the ones that stand out tend to be grounded in real experiences rather than general enthusiasm. St George's values students who show awareness of the challenges facing the NHS, an understanding of the demands of the profession, and the resilience to meet them. Extracurricular activities matter less than your ability to reflect on what you have done and connect it to your reasons for choosing medicine.

St George's takes approximately 300 students per year onto its five-year undergraduate programme, making it a moderately sized intake by London standards. Competition is strong, and every element of your application — academic record, UCAT, personal statement, and interview — contributes to the final decision.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to St George's University of London

What UCAT score do I need for St George's?
There is no officially published minimum, but applicants who are invited to interview typically score in the upper third of the national cohort. A total scaled score above 2700 is broadly considered competitive, though this fluctuates each year. A strong SJT result — Band 1 or 2 — also strengthens your application. Starting UCAT preparation early gives you the best chance of achieving a score that keeps your application moving forward.

Is work experience required to apply to St George's?
Work experience is not formally required in the sense that there is no minimum number of hours specified. However, it is strongly expected, and your personal statement and interview will be weaker without it. What matters most is not the prestige of the placement but your ability to reflect on what you observed and what it confirmed — or challenged — about your decision to pursue medicine.

How should I prepare for the St George's MMI?
The most effective preparation involves practising with another person rather than writing notes alone. Work through ethical scenarios, role-play exercises, and motivation questions out loud, and ask for honest feedback. Familiarise yourself with common medical ethics frameworks and current NHS issues. St George's is assessing how you think and communicate under mild pressure, so the goal is to develop genuine fluency rather than rehearsed answers.

Does St George's accept international students?
Yes, St George's does accept international students, though the number of places available to overseas applicants is limited and competition is intense. International applicants must meet the same academic and UCAT requirements as home applicants. Tuition fees for international students are significantly higher than for home students, and it is worth checking the university's current fee schedule and any country-specific entry requirements before applying.
