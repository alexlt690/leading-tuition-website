Why Choose University of Aberdeen for Medicine?

Aberdeen is not the obvious choice for every applicant, and that is precisely why it suits certain students so well. If you are drawn to a medical school where you will not be lost in a crowd, where clinical exposure begins early, and where the surrounding environment genuinely shapes your training, Aberdeen deserves serious consideration. The city itself is compact and manageable, with a strong sense of community that many students find far more supportive than larger urban campuses. The medical school has a long-standing reputation for producing capable, grounded doctors, and its integration with NHS Grampian means that students are working alongside real clinical teams from the early years of the programme. For students who want a medical education that feels purposeful rather than purely academic, and who are open to living somewhere genuinely different, the University of Aberdeen offers something distinctive.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

The standard A-Level offer for Medicine at Aberdeen is AAA, with Chemistry required as one of the three subjects. Biology is strongly preferred as a second science, and most successful applicants hold both Chemistry and Biology at A-Level. Aberdeen does not typically require an A* at A-Level, which makes it slightly more accessible than some other UK medical schools in terms of grade thresholds — though this should not be mistaken for a lower standard overall, since competition remains strong.

Scottish Highers applicants are considered on the basis of AAAAB or AAAAA at Higher, with Advanced Highers typically required in Chemistry and one other relevant subject. Aberdeen actively welcomes applications from Scottish students and participates in widening access schemes, including the Medicine: Widening Access Programme (MAP), which offers an alternative route for students from underrepresented backgrounds who meet specific contextual criteria.

For students taking the International Baccalaureate, the typical requirement is 36 points overall, with 6,6,5 at Higher Level including Chemistry. AS-Level results and predicted grades are reviewed as part of the application, so consistency across your academic profile matters. Aberdeen also considers applicants who have completed a Foundation year in some circumstances, though direct entry to Year 1 is the standard route.

The course itself takes five years for standard entry graduates, with an intercalated year available for students who wish to pursue a BSc alongside their medical degree. Aberdeen has a strong tradition of intercalation, and a meaningful proportion of students take this option.

UCAT Requirements at University of Aberdeen

Aberdeen uses the UCAT as part of its selection process, and your score will influence whether you are invited to interview. The competitive threshold sits at approximately 2500 or above across the four cognitive subtests, though this figure can shift slightly from year to year depending on the applicant pool. Aberdeen does not publish a fixed cut-off, so treating 2500 as a working benchmark is sensible for planning purposes.

It is worth noting that Aberdeen considers the Situational Judgement Test (SJT) component of the UCAT separately. A Band 4 in the SJT — the lowest band — may disadvantage your application even if your cognitive scores are strong. Aiming for Band 1 or Band 2 in the SJT is advisable. Aberdeen uses UCAT scores alongside academic grades to create a ranked shortlist for interview, so a significantly higher UCAT score can strengthen an otherwise borderline academic profile.

The Interview Process at University of Aberdeen

Aberdeen uses a Multiple Mini Interview (MMI) format. This means that rather than a single panel interview, you will rotate through a series of short, structured stations — typically around eight to ten — each lasting a few minutes. Each station is assessed independently by a different interviewer, and the topics vary considerably between stations.

You might encounter a scenario requiring an ethical judgement, a role-play exercise involving a simulated patient or colleague interaction, a question about your motivation for medicine, or a station testing your communication skills under mild pressure. Some stations may ask you to respond to a piece of information you are given on arrival. The MMI format is designed to give a broader picture of a candidate than a single interview can provide, and it rewards candidates who are adaptable, calm, and genuinely reflective rather than those who have rehearsed a single polished narrative.

Aberdeen's MMIs have historically taken place in person at the medical school, and preparation should include practising speaking clearly and concisely under timed conditions, as well as familiarising yourself with current NHS issues, medical ethics frameworks, and the structure of the Aberdeen curriculum.

What Makes a Strong University of Aberdeen Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, Aberdeen is looking for applicants who can demonstrate genuine engagement with medicine as a vocation. Work experience is not formally mandated, but it is expected in practice — and more importantly, it should inform what you write. Admissions tutors are experienced at distinguishing between personal statements that describe work experience and those that reflect meaningfully on it.

Strong applicants to Aberdeen tend to share several characteristics:

  • Sustained work experience in a healthcare setting, whether shadowing, volunteering, or paid care work
  • Evidence of insight into the NHS and the realities of clinical practice, not just an idealised view of medicine
  • Genuine extracurricular interests that demonstrate resilience, teamwork, or leadership
  • A personal statement that is specific and honest rather than formulaic
  • Awareness of what Aberdeen's programme involves, including its early clinical integration and rural medicine opportunities

Aberdeen takes approximately 170 students per year across its standard and graduate entry routes, which means the cohort is relatively small and the learning environment is close-knit. Students who thrive here tend to value that intimacy and are willing to engage with the community around them — both within the university and across the wider NHS Grampian region, which includes rural and remote placements that are genuinely unlike anything available in larger cities.

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to University of Aberdeen

What UCAT score do I need for Aberdeen Medicine?

A competitive score is generally considered to be around 2500 or above across the four cognitive subtests. Aberdeen does not publish a fixed minimum, and the threshold can vary between application cycles. Your SJT band also matters — Band 4 is likely to weaken an otherwise strong application, so aim for Band 1 or 2.

Is work experience required to apply to Aberdeen Medicine?

There is no formal requirement for a set number of hours, but work experience in a healthcare context is strongly expected. More importantly, Aberdeen wants to see that you have reflected on what you observed and learned. Experience in a care home, hospital, GP surgery, or voluntary health setting all carry weight if discussed thoughtfully in your personal statement.

How should I prepare for the Aberdeen MMI?

Practise speaking concisely under time pressure, as each station is short. Familiarise yourself with common medical ethics principles, NHS current affairs, and the structure of the Aberdeen course. Mock MMI practice with a partner or tutor is particularly useful because the format rewards composure and adaptability rather than rehearsed answers.

Does the University of Aberdeen accept international students for Medicine?

Yes, Aberdeen accepts international students, including those from outside the UK and EU. International applicants are subject to separate fee structures and should check the university's admissions pages for the most current guidance on entry requirements, English language requirements, and visa considerations, as these details are updated regularly.
