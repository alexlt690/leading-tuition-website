University of Lincoln Medicine Entry Requirements

University of Lincoln's medical school is one of the newest in England, having welcomed its first cohort in 2019. That relative youth is not a weakness — it is, in many ways, the point. The school was built from scratch with a clear purpose: to train doctors who will serve the East Midlands and wider Lincolnshire region, an area that has historically struggled to recruit and retain medical professionals. If you are drawn to a school where your presence genuinely matters to the local community, where small cohort sizes mean you are known by name rather than student number, and where clinical exposure begins early in a region that needs committed doctors, Lincoln deserves serious consideration.

Why Choose University of Lincoln for Medicine?

Lincoln Medical School was established as a partnership between the University of Lincoln and the University of Nottingham, which means students benefit from the academic infrastructure of an established medical institution while studying in a smaller, more intimate environment. The curriculum is delivered with a strong emphasis on community medicine and primary care — reflecting the genuine healthcare needs of the region — but this does not come at the expense of hospital-based learning. Students rotate through United Lincolnshire Hospitals NHS Trust, one of the largest NHS trusts in the country by geography, giving exposure to a wide range of clinical presentations across acute, elective, and community settings.

Lincoln itself is a city that rewards those who invest in it. It is compact, affordable, and genuinely student-friendly without the anonymity of a large metropolitan campus. The cost of living is considerably lower than London or Manchester, which matters across a five-year degree. The medical school cohort is small — approximately 80 students per year — which creates a collegiate atmosphere that many students find both academically and personally supportive.

Entry Requirements and A-Level Grades

The standard A-Level offer for Lincoln Medicine is AAA, with Chemistry required as one of the three subjects. Biology is strongly preferred as a second science, though the school does consider applicants with other science or mathematics combinations. You should check the current UCAS entry requirements directly, as Lincoln has refined its offer criteria since opening, but AAA with Chemistry is the consistent baseline.

For Scottish Highers applicants, the equivalent offer is typically AAAAB or AAAAA at Advanced Higher and Higher level respectively. If you are taking the International Baccalaureate, a total of 36 points with 6,6,6 at Higher Level including Chemistry is the expected standard.

GCSE performance is considered as part of the application, with particular attention paid to grades in English, Mathematics, and the sciences. Lincoln's admissions process is holistic, so a strong academic trajectory matters — but so does evidence that you understand what medicine in a regional context actually involves.

UCAT Requirements at University of Lincoln

Lincoln uses the UCAT as a scored component of its admissions process. The school does not publish a fixed cut-off score, but in practice, competitive applicants tend to achieve a total scaled score in the region of 2600 to 2700 or above across the four cognitive subtests. The Situational Judgement Test is also considered, and a Band 4 result is likely to weaken an otherwise strong application.

Because Lincoln has a regional intake focus — meaning it actively seeks to recruit students with connections to Lincolnshire and the East Midlands — the UCAT is used alongside contextual factors rather than as a standalone filter. This does not mean the test matters less; it means the admissions team is looking at the whole picture. Preparing thoroughly for the UCAT remains essential, and sitting it early in the testing window gives you time to resit if needed, though Lincoln's policy on resits should be confirmed for the current cycle.

The Interview Process at University of Lincoln

Lincoln uses a Multiple Mini Interview format, commonly known as an MMI. This consists of a series of short, structured stations — typically around eight to ten — each lasting approximately five to eight minutes. Each station is assessed independently by a different interviewer, which means a single difficult station does not define your overall performance.

The stations at Lincoln are designed to assess a range of competencies rather than medical knowledge alone. You can expect scenarios covering ethical reasoning, communication skills, empathy, teamwork, and your understanding of the NHS and healthcare in a regional context. Some stations may involve role-play exercises, others will present written prompts or ethical dilemmas for discussion. Lincoln's MMI reflects its mission: interviewers are genuinely interested in whether you understand the challenges facing rural and semi-rural healthcare, and whether you are the kind of person who will thrive in and contribute to that environment.

What Makes a Strong University of Lincoln Application

Beyond grades and UCAT scores, Lincoln is looking for applicants who can demonstrate genuine engagement with medicine as it is practised — not just as it appears in textbooks. Work experience in primary care, community settings, or rural healthcare environments is particularly relevant here, though any substantive clinical or caring experience will be valued. Shadowing a GP in a market town carries real weight at Lincoln in a way it might not at a large urban school.

Your personal statement should reflect honest self-awareness. Lincoln's admissions team reads thousands of statements that describe hospital shadowing and volunteering in abstract terms. What distinguishes strong applicants is the ability to connect specific experiences to specific insights — what you observed, what it made you think, and how it shaped your understanding of what a doctor actually does.

A few qualities that tend to characterise successful Lincoln applicants:

  • A demonstrable connection to or interest in healthcare in the East Midlands or Lincolnshire region
  • Evidence of resilience and adaptability, particularly relevant to working in under-resourced settings
  • Strong communication skills shown through extracurricular involvement, volunteering, or caring responsibilities
  • Genuine curiosity about community medicine and primary care, not just hospital specialties
  • A mature understanding of the NHS, including current pressures on rural and regional services

Frequently Asked Questions about Applying to University of Lincoln

What UCAT score do I need for University of Lincoln?

Lincoln does not publish a fixed minimum UCAT score, but competitive applicants typically achieve a total scaled score of around 2600 or higher across the four cognitive subtests. The Situational Judgement Test result is also considered, so aim for Band 1 or Band 2. Given Lincoln's contextual admissions approach, a strong UCAT score combined with relevant regional ties and a compelling personal statement gives you the best chance of progressing to interview.

Is work experience required to apply to University of Lincoln?

Work experience is not formally required, but it is strongly expected and will significantly strengthen your application. Lincoln places particular value on experience in community, primary care, or rural healthcare settings, which aligns with the school's mission. If you have caring responsibilities or have worked in a healthcare-adjacent role, this is worth discussing in your personal statement. Quality and reflection matter more than the number of hours logged.

How should I prepare for the Lincoln MMI?

Start by familiarising yourself with common MMI station types: ethical scenarios, role-play, data interpretation, and values-based questions. Practice thinking aloud and structuring responses clearly under time pressure. For Lincoln specifically, read about the healthcare challenges facing Lincolnshire and the East Midlands — rural GP shortages, NHS workforce pressures, and community care models are all relevant. Mock MMI practice with honest feedback is the single most effective preparation tool.

Does University of Lincoln accept international students onto its medicine course?

Lincoln Medical School has historically focused its intake on home students, reflecting its founding purpose of addressing regional healthcare workforce needs in England. International student places, if available, are very limited. If you are an international applicant, you should contact the admissions team directly to confirm current policy before investing significant time in an application, as this may change between cycles.
