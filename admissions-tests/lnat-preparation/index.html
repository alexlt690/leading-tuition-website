The Law National Aptitude Test is one of the most demanding admissions assessments a sixth-form student will face. Unlike subject-specific exams, it tests reasoning and writing skills that many applicants have never been formally taught — and the pressure of knowing that top law schools will scrutinise your result makes preparation feel particularly high-stakes. Working with an experienced LNAT tutor UK students trust can make a significant difference, not by teaching tricks, but by building the analytical habits and essay discipline the test genuinely rewards.

What Is the Law National Aptitude Test?

The LNAT is a two-hour admissions test used by a number of leading UK universities to assess applicants for undergraduate law programmes. It is sat at a registered Pearson VUE test centre and must be completed within the application window set by each institution. The test is not a measure of legal knowledge — you do not need to know any law to sit it. Instead, it is designed to assess the intellectual skills that underpin legal study: reading carefully, reasoning precisely, and arguing clearly.

Universities that currently require the LNAT include Oxford, UCL, King's College London, Bristol, Glasgow, Nottingham, Durham, and SOAS. Requirements and deadlines vary by institution, so applicants should check each university's admissions page carefully. Oxford applicants face the earliest deadline, typically in late October, which means preparation must begin well before the UCAS cycle reaches its peak.

What Does the Law National Aptitude Test Test?

The LNAT has two distinct sections, each assessing different but related skills.

Section A consists of 42 multiple-choice questions based on twelve reading passages. You have 95 minutes to complete this section. Each passage is followed by three or four questions that ask you to identify arguments, draw inferences, assess the strength of reasoning, and distinguish between what is stated and what is implied. The passages are deliberately varied in subject matter — philosophy, politics, science, economics, history — and are written at a level of complexity that rewards careful, active reading.

Section B presents three essay questions and asks you to choose one and write a response in 40 minutes. Topics are deliberately broad and often provocative — questions about justice, democracy, freedom, or social policy. The essay is not marked by the test itself; it is sent directly to the universities you apply to, who use it as part of their holistic assessment of your application.

How the Law National Aptitude Test Is Scored

Only Section A is scored by the LNAT system. Your raw score out of 42 is converted to a percentage, and this is the figure universities receive. There is no negative marking, so leaving questions blank is always the wrong strategy. The national average score typically sits between 22 and 24 out of 42. Oxford, which is the most competitive destination, generally interviews candidates scoring above 27 or 28, though this varies by year and cohort.

Section B is not scored centrally. Universities read your essay themselves and assess it against their own criteria, which typically include clarity of argument, quality of reasoning, use of evidence, and written expression. A strong essay will not rescue a weak Section A score, but a poor essay can undermine an otherwise competitive application — particularly at Oxford, where tutors read essays closely before interview.

How to Prepare Effectively

Most students underestimate how much deliberate practice Section A requires. Reading quickly and assuming you have understood a passage is not the same as reading analytically. The most common mistakes include:

  • Choosing answers that sound reasonable rather than answers that are directly supported by the text
  • Confusing the author's view with a view the author is describing or critiquing
  • Misreading the scope of a conclusion — treating a qualified claim as an absolute one
  • Running out of time because passages have not been read efficiently
  • Writing Section B essays that are descriptive rather than argumentative

Effective preparation takes time. For most students, a structured programme of eight to twelve weeks produces meaningful improvement. Those applying to Oxford should begin no later than early September to allow sufficient practice before the October deadline. Preparation should include timed practice under realistic conditions, detailed review of every incorrect answer, and regular essay writing with structured feedback. Reading quality long-form journalism and opinion writing — from publications such as The Economist, The Guardian, or Prospect — also builds the reading stamina and critical awareness that Section A rewards.

Frequently Asked Questions

When do I need to register for the LNAT?

Registration deadlines depend on which universities you are applying to. Oxford requires the LNAT to be sat by late October, meaning you should register in September at the latest. Other universities have a January deadline. You register directly through the LNAT website and book your test at a local Pearson VUE centre. Do not leave this late — popular test centres fill up quickly in October.

What score should I be aiming for?

The national average is typically around 22 to 24 out of 42. For competitive universities such as Oxford and UCL, a score above 27 is generally considered strong, though there is no fixed threshold. Your score is considered alongside your personal statement, predicted grades, and — at Oxford — your interview performance. Aiming for 28 or above gives you a meaningful advantage.

Are past papers available to practise with?

The official LNAT website provides a number of practice tests, and these are the most reliable preparation material available. There are also third-party practice resources, though quality varies. Our tutors can guide you towards the most useful materials and help you use them effectively rather than simply working through them without structured review.

How does working with a tutor actually help?

Self-study with practice papers is valuable, but most students plateau without feedback. A tutor helps you understand why you are getting questions wrong — whether that is a reading habit, a reasoning error, or a timing issue — and gives you a method to correct it. For Section B, written feedback on your essays is particularly important, since it is very difficult to assess the quality of your own argument objectively under pressure.
