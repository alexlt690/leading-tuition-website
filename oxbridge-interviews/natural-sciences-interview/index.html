Cambridge Natural Sciences Interviews

Natural Sciences interviews at Oxford and Cambridge are unlike anything you will have encountered in a classroom. Your teachers assess what you already know; Oxbridge tutors are interested in what you can work out. You will be given problems you have never seen before — sometimes involving concepts slightly beyond A-level — and asked to reason through them in real time, out loud, in front of academics who have spent decades in the field. The subject knowledge you have built matters, but it is the raw material, not the finished product. What tutors are genuinely evaluating is how your mind engages with scientific uncertainty: whether you can form a hypothesis, test it against what you know, revise it when challenged, and keep thinking clearly under pressure. If you are expecting an interview that rewards polished answers, you will need to adjust your expectations — and your preparation.

What to Expect in a Natural Sciences Oxbridge Interview

Most Natural Sciences candidates at both Oxford and Cambridge will have two or three interviews, each lasting between twenty and thirty minutes. At Cambridge, interviews are typically arranged by college and are subject-specific — if you have applied to study Physics and Chemistry in your first year, expect separate interviews in each. At Oxford, the sciences are admissions-separate (you apply to a specific science), so the interview format is tailored to that discipline rather than the broader Natural Sciences framework Cambridge uses.

In both cases, you are likely to be handed a problem — a graph, a diagram, a short passage, or a numerical question — and asked to work through it with the interviewer. This is not a test of memory. It is a structured conversation in which the tutor is actively trying to understand how you think. They will push back on your answers, offer hints if you are stuck, and sometimes take the problem in a direction you did not anticipate. This is deliberate. The discomfort is part of the process, and how you respond to it tells the interviewer far more than a correct answer delivered without hesitation.

Cambridge Natural Sciences interviews tend to place particular emphasis on cross-disciplinary thinking, reflecting the broad first-year course structure. You may be asked to apply a physical principle to a biological system, or to think about the mathematics underlying a chemical process. Oxford interviews, being subject-specific, go deeper into a single discipline from the outset.

The Admissions Tests: Oxford and ESAT (Cambridge)

Oxford does not use a single admissions test for Natural Sciences because it does not offer Natural Sciences as a unified course — applicants apply to individual sciences such as Biochemistry, Chemistry, Physics, or Biology, each with its own admissions pathway and, in some cases, its own pre-interview assessment. You should check the specific requirements for your chosen Oxford science carefully.

Cambridge requires all Natural Sciences applicants to sit the Engineering and Science Admissions Test (ESAT). This is a computer-based test taken in October, before interviews are held. It includes a compulsory Mathematics section and two further modules relevant to your chosen subjects — for example, Physics and Chemistry, or Biology and Chemistry. The ESAT is not a knowledge test in the traditional sense; it is designed to assess scientific reasoning and mathematical fluency under time pressure. Strong ESAT performance will not guarantee an interview, but a weak score can close the door before you reach one. Crucially, the skills the ESAT tests — working quickly with unfamiliar problems, applying mathematical reasoning to scientific contexts — are exactly the skills your interviewers will be probing. Preparing for the ESAT and preparing for interview are, in large part, the same activity.

How to Prepare for Your Natural Sciences Interview

The single most important habit to develop is thinking aloud. Most candidates lose marks not because they reach the wrong answer, but because they go silent when they are uncertain. Interviewers cannot assess reasoning they cannot hear. Practise narrating your thought process — even when you are unsure, especially when you are unsure. Say what you notice, what you are trying, and what is not working. This is not a weakness; it is exactly what tutors want to see.

Beyond that, effective preparation involves:

  • Revisiting the mathematical foundations of your sciences — dimensional analysis, proportionality, orders of magnitude, and basic calculus appear frequently
  • Reading beyond the A-level syllabus in your strongest subject — popular science books, review articles, and university-level introductory texts all help build the conceptual vocabulary interviewers expect
  • Practising with past ESAT and NSAA papers (the NSAA was the predecessor to the ESAT and remains a useful resource) under timed conditions
  • Doing mock interviews with someone who will genuinely challenge you — not just ask questions, but interrupt, probe, and redirect
  • Reviewing your personal statement carefully, since interviewers at both Oxford and Cambridge may ask you to expand on anything you have mentioned

Super-curricular engagement matters here more than in many subjects. If you have read about a particular area of science — quantum biology, the thermodynamics of living systems, the chemistry of atmospheric change — be ready to discuss it with intellectual honesty. You do not need to be an expert; you need to be genuinely curious and able to say clearly what you understood, what you found difficult, and what questions it raised for you.

Example Interview Questions for Natural Sciences

The following questions are representative of the kind of problems you might encounter. They are not designed to be answered instantly — they are designed to be explored.

  • A graph shows enzyme activity against temperature, with a sharp peak followed by a steep decline. What is happening at the molecular level on either side of the peak, and how would you model this mathematically?
  • If you doubled the pressure on a gas and simultaneously halved its temperature, what would happen to its volume? Now explain what assumptions you made and when they might break down.
  • Why does ice float on water, and what would the consequences be for life on Earth if it did not?
  • A radioactive sample has a half-life of ten years. After how long will less than one percent of the original material remain? Show your reasoning without a calculator.
  • You are given a sample of an unknown compound and a mass spectrometer. Walk me through how you would begin to determine its structure, and what limitations you might encounter.
  • Why is it energetically expensive for a cell to maintain a concentration gradient across its membrane, and how does this relate to the second law of thermodynamics?

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most damaging mistake candidates make is trying to perform certainty they do not have. If you do not know something, saying so clearly and then attempting to reason from first principles is far more impressive than bluffing. Tutors have conducted hundreds of these interviews; they will notice immediately.

A second common error is treating the interview as a one-way test rather than a conversation. When an interviewer offers a hint or reframes a question, they are helping you — take the redirection seriously and adjust your thinking. Candidates who ignore hints and continue down an unproductive path signal poor intellectual flexibility.

Finally, many candidates under-prepare the mathematics. Even in biology-heavy interviews, quantitative reasoning appears regularly. If your algebra is slow or your confidence with logarithms is shaky, address that before interview day.

Frequently Asked Questions about Natural Sciences Oxbridge Interviews

How long do Natural Sciences interviews typically last?
Most interviews run for between twenty and thirty minutes. Cambridge candidates usually have two or three interviews across their visit, often in different science subjects. Oxford candidates, applying to a specific science, typically have two interviews within that discipline. The total time you spend in interviews across your visit is usually around an hour, though this varies by college.

Will I be tested on knowledge beyond my A-level syllabus?
You may encounter concepts that sit just beyond A-level, but interviewers are not expecting you to have studied them already. What they want to see is whether you can engage with an unfamiliar idea using the tools you already have. If a question introduces a new concept, the interviewer will usually explain it briefly — your job is to work with that explanation, not to have memorised it in advance.

How should I practise for the interview?
The most effective preparation combines timed problem-solving with spoken reasoning. Work through ESAT practice papers and past NSAA papers out loud, explaining each step as you go. Ask a teacher, tutor, or knowledgeable friend to conduct mock interviews that include follow-up questions and deliberate challenges. Practising alone in silence will not prepare you for the experience of thinking under observation.

What should I do if I genuinely do not know the answer?
Say so, and then keep going. Tell the interviewer what you do know that might be relevant, what approach you would try, and what is making the problem difficult. This is not a concession of defeat — it is a demonstration of scientific honesty and intellectual resilience, both of which are qualities Oxbridge tutors are actively looking for. Silence or guessing without acknowledgement are the responses that concern interviewers most.
