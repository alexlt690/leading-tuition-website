Oxford and Cambridge Physics Interviews

A Physics interview at Oxford or Cambridge is unlike anything you will have encountered in school. It is not a test of how much you have memorised, nor is it a chance to recite your personal statement back to a tutor. It is a live problem-solving session, conducted under pressure, with an academic who wants to see precisely how your mind works when it meets a problem it has never seen before. Most candidates arrive expecting to be questioned on their knowledge. The best candidates arrive ready to think — out loud, carefully, and without pretending to certainty they do not have. That distinction is what separates those who receive offers from those who do not.

What to Expect in a Physics Oxbridge Interview

Both Oxford and Cambridge Physics interviews are conducted by subject tutors or supervisors — practising physicists who teach undergraduates every week. They are not looking for a finished physicist; they are looking for someone who can become one. Expect to be given problems on the spot, often involving diagrams, equations, or physical scenarios. You may be handed a piece of paper and asked to work through something mathematically. You may be shown an unfamiliar graph and asked what it tells you. The tutor will almost certainly push you beyond your comfort zone — not to humiliate you, but to find your ceiling.

At Oxford, you will typically have two interviews, often with different tutors, each lasting around 20 to 30 minutes. Cambridge interviews tend to be slightly shorter and may involve a single interview, though this varies by college. Both universities use the interview to assess the same core qualities: physical intuition, mathematical fluency, intellectual curiosity, and the ability to reason under uncertainty. The difference, if any, is one of emphasis: Oxford interviews often feel more mathematically rigorous from the outset, while Cambridge interviews may begin with a broader conceptual discussion before moving into calculation. In practice, both demand the same level of preparation.

The Admissions Tests: PAT (Oxford) and ESAT (Cambridge)

Before you reach the interview stage, you will need to sit an admissions test. For Oxford Physics applicants, this is the Physics Admissions Test (PAT), a two-hour written paper covering mathematics and physics at a level that extends meaningfully beyond A-level. For Cambridge applicants, the relevant test is the Engineering and Science Admissions Test (ESAT), which includes a compulsory Mathematics section and a Physics section for those applying to Natural Sciences or Engineering.

These tests matter for two reasons. First, a strong performance significantly improves your chances of being shortlisted for interview. Second, and less obviously, preparing for the PAT or ESAT is one of the most effective ways to prepare for the interview itself. Both tests require you to apply physical principles to unfamiliar problems — exactly what the interview demands. Working through past PAT papers and ESAT practice materials builds the problem-solving habits that tutors are looking for when you sit across from them in December.

How to Prepare for Your Physics Interview

Preparation for a Physics Oxbridge interview has three distinct layers. The first is consolidating your A-level knowledge so that it is genuinely secure — not just revision-secure, but secure enough that you can use it flexibly under pressure. The second is extending your understanding into territory that sits just beyond the A-level syllabus: dimensional analysis, simple differential equations, energy methods in mechanics, and basic ideas from special relativity or quantum mechanics are all fair game.

The third layer is the most important: practising thinking aloud. In a real interview, silence is your enemy. Tutors cannot assess reasoning they cannot hear. When you encounter a problem you are unsure about, the correct response is not to freeze or guess — it is to narrate your thinking. Say what you notice, what you are trying, what is not working and why. This is not a performance; it is exactly how physicists actually work, and tutors find it far more compelling than a candidate who produces a correct answer without explanation.

Super-curricular preparation also strengthens your application. Reading accessible but intellectually serious books — such as Feynman's Six Easy Pieces, or popular accounts of modern physics — gives you a richer conceptual vocabulary. Engaging with physics beyond the classroom signals genuine enthusiasm, which tutors notice and value.

Example Interview Questions for Physics

The following questions are representative of the kind of problems you might encounter. They are not trick questions — but they do require careful physical reasoning rather than formula recall.

  • A ball is dropped from a height onto a surface that exerts a constant upward force. Sketch how the velocity of the ball varies with time from the moment it is released until it comes to rest. Justify each feature of your sketch.
  • Estimate the number of air molecules in this room. Walk me through every assumption you make.
  • A thin uniform rod is balanced horizontally on a single pivot. The pivot is then moved slightly to one side. What happens, and why? Can you describe the motion mathematically?
  • Why does a spinning gyroscope not fall over? Explain the physics without using the word "gyroscope" again.
  • If you doubled the radius of the Earth while keeping its mass the same, how would the length of a day change? Show your reasoning.
  • A resistor and a capacitor are connected in series to a battery. Describe and explain how the current through the circuit changes over time. What happens to the energy supplied by the battery?

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most frequent error candidates make is treating the interview like an exam — staying silent while they think, then presenting a polished answer. This approach fails because it gives the tutor nothing to work with and nothing to redirect. If your reasoning goes wrong in silence, you will not be corrected; you will simply run out of time.

A second common mistake is abandoning a line of thinking the moment it becomes difficult. Tutors expect difficulty — they have designed the question to be difficult. Persistence, combined with honest acknowledgement of where you are stuck, is far more impressive than a rapid but shallow answer. A third mistake is over-preparing specific answers to anticipated questions. The interview will almost certainly not go where you expect. Candidates who have rehearsed answers rather than practised reasoning tend to become rigid and flustered when the tutor takes the problem in an unexpected direction.

Frequently Asked Questions about Physics Oxbridge Interviews

How long do Physics Oxbridge interviews typically last?
At Oxford, most candidates have two interviews, each lasting approximately 20 to 30 minutes. At Cambridge, interviews are often slightly shorter — around 20 minutes — though this varies by college. In total, you should expect between 30 and 60 minutes of interview time across your visit.

Will I be tested on knowledge beyond my A-level syllabus?
You may encounter ideas that sit just beyond A-level, but tutors are not expecting you to have studied university Physics in advance. What matters is whether you can reason from first principles when something is unfamiliar. If a concept is genuinely new to you, say so — and then engage with it anyway using the physics you do know.

How should I practise for the interview?
The most effective method is to work through problems out loud with another person — ideally a tutor or teacher who can push back on your reasoning. Past PAT papers and ESAT practice questions are excellent starting material. You should also practise narrating your thinking on problems you find genuinely difficult, not just ones you can already solve.

What should I do if I do not know the answer to a question?
Say so — clearly and without panic — and then keep going. Tell the tutor what you do know that might be relevant, what approach you would try, and what is making it difficult. Tutors are not expecting perfection; they are assessing how you respond to not knowing. A candidate who engages honestly with uncertainty is far more impressive than one who bluffs or gives up.
